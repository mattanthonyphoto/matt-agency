## CAROUSEL POST 1 — The Transformation (8 slides)
**Platform:** Instagram
**Pillar:** Project Showcase
**Voice:** Lauren (first person plural)

### Slide sequence + photo mapping:

| Slide | Photo | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | LRD-2500px-1 (kitchen wide) | Hero: the transformed kitchen, whitewashed fir beams overhead (verify room) |
| 2 | LRD-2500px-29 (fir beam detail) | The whitewashed fir up close, the grain still visible (verify room) |
| 3 | LRD-2500px-7 (living room wide) | Living room with the lighter palette, hickory floors (verify room) |
| 4 | LRD-2500px-30 (hickory floor detail) | Hickory floors paired with the whitewashed fir (verify room) |
| 5 | LRD-2500px-8 (fireplace with wood fins) | The living room fireplace, the home's anchor (verify room) |
| 6 | LRD-2500px-14 (staircase) | The modernized staircase, existing bones made new (verify room) |
| 7 | LRD-2500px-17 (wardrobe with skylight) | The open wardrobe flooded with natural light (verify room) |

### Caption:
This home came to us full of warmth that had aged past its time. The existing fir throughout the house had that classical orange tone, and the family was ready for something lighter. Something that still honored the material but let the house breathe.

So we had all the fir refinished and applied a whitewash. The grain is still there. The character is still there. But the whole house opened up. We paired that with hickory floors and a lighter millwork, and then layered in these hits of color that the family chose together.

There were two lovely boys in this family who kept gravitating towards teals and turquoises. The parents were happy to honor that. So we wove those tones through the house, and the result is a home that feels like the people who live in it.

This is Tapleys, in Whistler. A classic home, completely transformed.

### Caption:
The palette in this house started with a conversation about what to keep.

The fir was staying. The bones were staying. So everything else had to respond to that material reality. We whitewashed the fir, brought in hickory floors, and then built the color story from there.

The tile selections are all organic in texture, a classic Moroccan zellige that varies in color across every surface. The stone is black Carmana, quarried from Vancouver Island, which we love because it's honest and local and grounded.

And then there's the color. Two boys in this family kept gravitating towards teals and turquoises, and we honored that. Those hits of color appear throughout the house, and they give every room a warmth that feels personal, not prescribed.

The hardware is black and brass by M-Tack. The fireplaces have these unexpected details, wood fins on the living room side, slatted panels in the family room. The niches and vanities are underlit. Every finish was chosen to feel intentional.

### Caption:
One of the most thoughtful parts of this home is a space most guests will never see.

There's a parent suite with its own kitchenette, ensuite, and wardrobe. It's completely self-contained. We designed it for families thinking about the long view, about other members of their family living here together for years to come.

That kind of design asks different questions. It's not just about how a room looks. It's about how three generations might share a home and still have privacy, dignity, and their own daily rhythm.

The family room downstairs has a slatted panel beside the fireplace that doubles as a storage closet for games. A two-sided fireplace with a stone ledge and a TV niche above. These are rooms designed for real life, for movie nights and board games and Sunday mornings where everyone ends up in the same room.

And those hits of teal and turquoise throughout the house? Chosen by the two boys. Because designing a home for a family means every voice gets heard.
