### Caption:
There's a round stone structure in the middle of this house. Everything revolves around it.

The circular staircase wraps the outside with floating wood treads and glass. Inside, there's a wine fridge, a hot-cold-sparkling water faucet, and a cedar ceiling with lit shelving. The stone repeats the kitchen. The warmth echoes the logs above. It's where you pause between floors.

Walk around the other side and you're in the powder room. An arched alcove holds a pedestal sink and a backlit mirror. It's tucked entirely within the same stone cylinder. You wouldn't know it from the stair side.

This tower anchors the house. Sunridge was an existing timber log home that we modernized while keeping every original beam. The stone became the connective thread between old and new, running from the kitchen through the stairs and into the living space.

### Caption:
The logs were staying. That was the starting point.

Sunridge is an existing timber log home in Whistler. Our clients wanted a full renovation, but the original log construction had to remain. Every design decision we made started with: how does this sit beside a round timber beam?

In the kitchen, we used a mix of white oak and black oak cabinetry. There's a moment where the new oak meets the original log wall, and if the tones didn't work together, the whole room would feel like a fight. We spent a long time getting that junction right. The marble island grounds the space and gives the eye somewhere to rest.

Throughout the house, natural stone, modern lighting, and clean lines were introduced not to contrast the heritage structure but to complement it. The logs give the house its warmth. Our job was to make sure the modern elements felt like they always belonged there.

### Caption:
Five bathrooms and a powder room. Each one different. All of them connected.

Sunridge was a heavy bathroom renovation. We approached it with a shared material language: natural limestone countertops, black Fantini plumbing fixtures, backlit mirrors, and black wall sconces. Those were the constants. Everything else shifted from room to room.

In one bathroom, it's finger tile with oak millwork. In another, a porcelain tile with a chapel quality to it. The primary ensuite has breccia sarda natural stone and a freestanding black tub with towel warmers and alabaster sconces flanking the window. There's a steam shower with niche and under-bench lighting. There's a custom sauna next door with the same careful attention to the light underneath.

The thread running through all six is restraint. Black fixtures. Natural stone. Backlit mirrors. Rounded details. Enough consistency to feel like one house, enough variation to make each room feel like its own moment.

### LinkedIn 1 — The Renovation Challenge

> Sunridge is an existing timber log home in Whistler. Our clients wanted a full modernization, but the original log construction was staying. Every design decision we made started with that constraint.
>
> In the kitchen, we introduced white oak and black oak cabinetry, natural stone, and a marble waterfall island. The challenge wasn't the materials themselves. It was making sure they sat comfortably beside round timber beams that have been there for decades. There's a point where the new oak cabinetry meets the original log wall, and that junction had to feel effortless, not forced.
>
> A round stone structure anchors the center of the house. The circular staircase wraps around it. Inside, there's a wine bar with cedar and lit shelving. On the other side, a powder room with an arched mirror. The stone runs from the kitchen through the heart of the home. It became the thread connecting old and new.
>
> Renovation work like this is different from new builds. You're not designing in a vacuum. You're designing around something that already has a voice. Our job was to listen to that voice and add to the conversation without talking over it.
>

### LinkedIn 2 — Supporting Local Artists

> Two of the lighting pieces at Sunridge are by local BC artists: Bachi, a hand-blown glass maker, and Matthew McCormick, a lighting designer. Both are based here and both made original pieces for the home.
>
