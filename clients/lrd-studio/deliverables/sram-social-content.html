SRAM's brand colors are black, grey, and red. We didn't paint them on a wall. We wove them through the whole design. Modular sofas in those three tones. Geometric backsplash tile. Recycled vinyl with polygonal patterns in the meeting rooms. Every surface carries the brand without feeling like a showroom.

The bike storage is fully visible at the entry, not hidden. The backlit logo on oak paneling casts a warm red glow. When the brand is authentic, it shows up in the materials, not just the signage.

SRAM Canada builds their work culture around a simple idea: work and life aren't separate. Kitchen bar with a proper espresso station, because the morning starts here. Modular sofas that are genuinely comfortable. Meeting rooms that curve with the architecture, wrapped in glass so the fig tree stays visible from every seat.

No assigned seating, no corner offices. And yes, that's someone riding a bike through the office. Commercial design, done honestly, tells you everything about the people inside.

Design Thinking · The Cog Concept
We designed SRAM Canada's marketing headquarters around a single concept: the wheel. The floor plan follows a curve that emanates the shape of a bike cog. You wouldn't walk in and think "bike wheel," but you'd feel the openness, the flow.

Within that wheel, geometric complexity. Trapezoidal tile in the backsplash. Polygonal recycled vinyl in the meeting rooms. The balance between the broad curve and the fine geometry became the design language for the entire space.

The building is an old shipping warehouse near the Shipyards in North Vancouver. Exposed fir timbers and ceiling, all original. Commercial design works best when the concept comes from the client's identity, not from a trend library.

Photography: Janis Nicolay · Construction: Ario Construction
Heritage · Adaptive Reuse
One of the most rewarding parts of the SRAM project was working within a heritage structure. Old shipping warehouse near the Shipyards in North Vancouver. Exposed fir timbers and original ceiling. Details you can't fabricate.

Timbers refinished, not replaced. Ceiling cleaned up and left exposed. Industrial garage door stayed. The new elements, curved walls, oak paneling, modular furniture, sit in conversation with the existing bones rather than compete with them.

More brands are moving into older structures instead of new construction. The character gives a workspace something glass and steel cannot. The work is in knowing what to keep and what to add.
