**Platform:** Instagram

| 6 | sram-25 (open office) | The work floor, someone riding a bike through the office |
| 7 | sram-15 (modular sofas) | Lounge seating in brand colors |

#### Caption:
The floor plan of this space started with a single idea: the wheel.

We designed the layout of SRAM Canada's marketing headquarters around a curve that emanates the shape of a bike cog. It's the kind of move that feels intuitive when you're in the space but only reveals itself when you look at the plan. Within the simplicity of that wheel, we layered geometric shapes, trapezoidal and polygonal forms that echo the intricate mechanical parts inside the components SRAM makes.

This is an old shipping building near the Shipyards in North Vancouver. The exposed fir timbers and ceiling are all original, refinished to honor the structure's history. Oak paneling along the entry wall was the client's nod to west coast heritage. And then there's the fig tree in the middle of everything, grounding the whole space.

Designing for a brand like SRAM meant understanding that the people who work here ride. They live the product. So the space had to feel like that too, grounded, active, honest about what it is.

| 5 | sram-3 (kitchen/bar) | The kitchen bar, black cabinetry, ceramic pendants |
| 6 | sram-5 (curved wall + full space) | The complete picture, brand expressed through space |

#### Caption:
SRAM's brand colors are black, grey, and red. We didn't paint them on a wall. We wove them through the whole design.

The modular sofas are upholstered in those three tones. The backsplash tile in the kitchen carries geometric shapes that echo the precision of the components SRAM engineers. The meeting room floors use a recycled vinyl with trapezoidal, polygonal patterns. Every surface carries the brand without ever feeling like a showroom.

When you walk in, you're greeted by the bike storage area. It's fully visible, not hidden in a back room. Because the bikes aren't just products here, they're the culture. The backlit SRAM logo flanks the oak paneling at the entry, casting a warm red glow.

There are wonderful felt panels above the seating area that act as sound dissipation. And lighting by local designers A&D, their white ceramic pendants floating under the original fir ceiling.

When the brand is authentic, it shows up in the materials, not just the signage.

#### Caption:
SRAM Canada builds their work culture around a simple idea: work and life aren't separate.

So we designed accordingly. The kitchen bar has five stools and a proper espresso station because this is where the morning starts, not at a desk. The modular sofas in the lounge are genuinely comfortable, not the stiff kind you see in lobbies. The meeting rooms curve with the architecture, wrapped in glass so the fig tree and the exposed timbers stay visible from every seat.

There's a standing desk area that flows into communal worktables. No assigned seating, no corner offices. And yes, that's someone riding a bike through the office. That's the culture.

The felt panels above the seating area handle the acoustics so you can have a real conversation without competing with the open floor. Thoughtful sound design is one of those things that makes a workspace actually work.

This project reminded us that commercial design, when it's done honestly, tells you everything about the people inside.

## 5. LINKEDIN POSTS (2)

### LinkedIn 1 — The Cog Concept (design thinking)

We designed the marketing headquarters for SRAM Canada around a single concept: the wheel.

The floor plan follows a curve that emanates the shape of a bike cog. It's not a literal interpretation. You wouldn't walk in and think "bike wheel." But you'd feel the openness, the flow, the way the meeting rooms wrap along the arc and the lounge seats follow the line.

Within the simplicity of that wheel shape, we introduced geometric complexity. Trapezoidal tile patterns in the kitchen backsplash. Polygonal recycled vinyl in the meeting rooms. These echo the intricate mechanical parts inside the components SRAM designs and manufactures. The balance between the broad curve and the fine geometry became the design language for the entire space.

The building itself is an old shipping warehouse near the Shipyards in North Vancouver. The exposed fir timbers and ceiling are original, refinished to let the structure's heritage read honestly. We didn't try to hide the industrial character. We designed around it.

Commercial interior design works best when the concept comes from the client's identity, not from a trend library. SRAM makes products that balance elegance with engineering. The space should do the same.

### LinkedIn 2 — Honoring Heritage Buildings

One of the most rewarding parts of the SRAM project was working within a heritage structure.

The building sits near the Shipyards in North Vancouver, an old shipping warehouse with exposed fir timbers and original ceiling. These aren't the kind of details you can fabricate. They carry a weight and warmth that comes only with age.

Our approach was straightforward: honor what's already there. The timbers were refinished, not replaced. The ceiling was cleaned up and left exposed. The industrial garage door stayed. We designed the new elements, the curved walls, the oak paneling, the modular furniture, to sit in conversation with the existing bones of the building rather than compete with them.

SRAM wanted oak at the entry to acknowledge the west coast. That impulse, wanting to root a brand in the place where it operates, aligned perfectly with how we approach heritage spaces. The material choices should feel like they belong in the building, not like they were dropped in from a catalogue.

More and more, we're seeing brands move into older structures instead of new construction. The character, the story, the imperfections. They give a workspace something that glass and steel cannot. The work is in knowing what to keep and what to add.
