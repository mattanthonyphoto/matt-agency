--- CONTEXT ---

This prospect received a cold email and a follow-up and has not responded to either. This is the final touchpoint. The goal is NOT to create urgency, guilt, or pressure. The goal is to close the loop gracefully while leaving the door open.

The tone is relaxed, respectful, and peer-to-peer. This email says: No worries — I am here if the timing works out later. It should feel like something a trusted colleague would send, not a salesperson closing their sequence.

--- STRUCTURE ---

The email should have exactly 3 components:

1. A one-sentence acknowledgment that the timing may not be right — without referencing previous emails
2. A sentence that shares the journal article as something genuinely useful, using the HOOK field from the JOURNAL ARTICLE block above. Include the article LINK. Frame it as giving, not pitching — this is a resource that helps them regardless of whether they hire Matt.
3. A warm, short close that makes it effortless to re-engage

--- RULES ---

1. Use first name in greeting (Hi [first_name],)
2. DO NOT reference previous emails (my last email, I reached out, I sent you, my previous message)
3. BANNED phrases: just following up, circling back, bumping this up, touching base, checking in, I know you are busy, sorry to bother, have not heard back, no worries if not, hope all is well, wanted to reconnect, reaching out again
4. BANNED superlatives: amazing, stunning, incredible, gorgeous, exceptional
5. No false urgency or scarcity
6. No pricing or packages
7. The journal article link replaces the generic portfolio link — include the article link from the JOURNAL ARTICLE block, not mattanthonyphoto.com
8. Always include the FULL signature block after the email body. No closing salutation before the name (no "All the best,", "Best,", "Cheers,"). Just the body, then the signature block.
9. Keep to 2-3 sentences max (not counting greeting and sign-off)
10. Subject line: short, casual, zero clickbait (3-5 words)

--- JOURNAL ARTICLE FRAMING ---

The article link is the value-add in this email. It shifts the final impression from "this person wants my business" to "this person understands my world."

RULES FOR REFERENCING THE ARTICLE:
- Use the HOOK field as a starting point but write a natural sentence
- Do not use the words "blog post" or "article" — say "a piece I wrote" or "something I put together"
- Do not summarize the article contents — one sentence, let the link do the work
- Do not editorialize: no "you might find this useful", "thought you might like this", "worth a read"
- Frame it as something Matt wrote because he thinks about this space, not as something chosen for the prospect

WRONG: "I wrote an article about ROI that I think would be valuable for your business."
RIGHT: "I put together a piece on the ROI of professional photography for custom builders: [link]"

The article should feel like Matt dropping something useful on the table on his way out, not making a final case.

--- TONE ---

This email should feel like a nod across a room — acknowledging someone without cornering them. The reader should finish thinking that is a good person to know rather than that is a salesperson who gave up.
