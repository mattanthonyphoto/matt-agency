--- CONTEXT ---

This prospect received a cold email 3 days ago and has not responded. This follow-up is not a chase. It is a brief, low-pressure reminder that adds something concrete: a portfolio link, a reference to relevant work you have done for someone in their position, and a link to a detailed example of that work.

The tone matches the cold email — peer to peer, no pressure, no urgency. This email should feel like a thoughtful addition to the first touchpoint, not a second pitch.

--- ICP-SPECIFIC ANGLES FOR FOLLOW-UP ---

BUILDER: If the original email did not mention cost-sharing or construction progress, the follow-up is a good place to introduce one of these angles naturally:
- Cost-share: "On most projects, the architect and designer split the cost, so everyone gets the documentation at a fraction of the price."
- Construction progress: "If you have a project in progress, I also document builds at key milestones, monthly visits, images within 48 hours."
- Georgie timing (Q2-Q3 only): "Projects shot this summer are eligible for the 2027 Georgie Awards. Entry window opens in August."
Do NOT force these in. Only use if the flow allows it naturally. One angle maximum per follow-up.

--- STEP 1 — IDENTIFY THE FIRST NAME ---

Use the DECISION MAKER FIRST NAME field directly. If empty, use "there".

--- STEP 2 — WRITE THE FOLLOW-UP EMAIL ---

Exactly 4 sentences across 3 paragraphs plus signature. No more. No less.

PARAGRAPH 1 — RECAP (1 sentence)
Acknowledge you reached out a few days ago. Do not repeat the project name from the cold email. Do not re-pitch. One simple, direct sentence.

RULES:
- One sentence only
- Do not mention the project from the cold email
- Do not re-explain what Matt does
- Keep it casual and direct
- The recap should be complete on its own. Do not use it to set up paragraph 2 (no "wanted to add something", "had one more thing", "meant to include this")
- Never use: "just following up", "circling back", "bumping this up", "touching base", "hope this finds you well"

PARAGRAPH 2 — VALUE ADD (2 sentences)
Sentence 1: Offer the portfolio as something concrete to look at. Include the portfolio link: mattanthonyphoto.com
Sentence 2: Reference the case study work using the DESCRIPTION field from the CASE STUDY REFERENCE block above. Include the case study LINK. Do not use the words "case study."

RULES:
- Portfolio link must appear in sentence 1
- Case study link must appear in sentence 2
- Do not use the words "case study" or "visual identity"
- If CLIENT NAMED is false, do not name the client in sentence 2 — the description already handles this
- If CLIENT NAMED is true, you may use the client name as provided in the description
- Under 25 words per sentence
- Use photographer language: documented, shot, photographed
- Never use business/vendor language: wrapped, completed, delivered, partnered on, worked with, collaborated on
- The sentence should sound like a photographer describing work they shot, not a vendor describing a contract
- Do not editorialize the link. No "worth a look", "worth checking out", "you might like", or similar. Let the link speak for itself

PARAGRAPH 3 — CTA (1 sentence)
Low-pressure, no urgency, no call ask. One conditional statement, one offer.

RULES:
- One sentence only
- No call or meeting ask
- No urgency language
- Shorter and lighter than the cold email CTA

THE FEEL TEST:
Read it back. It should feel like:
"Hey, just following up — here is something concrete if you want to look."

Not:
"Please respond to my email."

If it feels like chasing, cut words until it feels like a reminder.

--- STEP 4 — SCAN AND FIX ---

HARD BANS — rewrite if found anywhere:
- Em dashes or semicolons
- Superlatives: amazing, stunning, incredible, beautiful, gorgeous, exceptional
- Urgency: limited time, circling back, bumping this up, as per my last email, just checking in
- Filler: honestly, truly, really, actually, genuinely, certainly
- Cliches: touching base, reaching out again, hope this finds you well, wanted to reconnect, just wanted to follow up, following up again
- Vendor language: wrapped, completed, delivered, partnered on, collaborated on, worked with
- Link editorializing: worth a look, worth checking out, you might like, take a look at this, check this out

TONE VIOLATIONS — rewrite the sentence:
- Apologetic: "sorry to bother you" — remove
- Pushy: any implication they should respond — remove
- Corporate: formal language — simplify

FINAL CHECKS:
- Recap does not repeat the project name
- Portfolio link present in sentence 1 of paragraph 2
- Case study link present in sentence 2 of paragraph 2
- Client naming rule followed per CLIENT NAMED field
- CTA is one sentence, no call ask
- 4 sentences total in the body
- Shorter than the cold email
- No banned words or phrases anywhere
- No use of "case study" or "visual identity" anywhere in the email
- Overall feel: helpful reminder, not a chase
