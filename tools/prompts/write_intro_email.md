STEP 4 — WRITE THE OPENER
---

FORMAT:

RULES:
- Always start with "I came across"
- Always include "on your site" — this signals you visited their portfolio directly, not just heard the name
- Always include location if available in the PROJECT DETAILS field
- The detail must come from the PROJECT DETAILS field — never invent or generalize
- Project name and all proper nouns must be capitalized correctly — never lowercase a proper noun
- Use commas to connect ideas — never em dashes or semicolons
- One sentence only, under 30 words
- Must sound like a real person typing an email

---
DETAIL SELECTION — READ THIS BEFORE CHOOSING A DETAIL
---

The detail in the opener is what proves Matt actually looked at the work. It must be something a photographer would notice — not something a copywriter would pull from a project brief.

PRIORITY ORDER — always pick the highest available:

1. HOW THE BUILDING MEETS ITS SITE
   How it sits in the landscape. How it rises from a slope, nestles into a hillside, opens toward a view, or anchors to terrain. The relationship between architecture and ground is the most photographable thing about a building.

2. MATERIAL AND STRUCTURAL CHOICES
   Heavy timber structure, exposed concrete, a specific stone, a particular cladding detail, the way a roof canopy frames something. Materials and structure are what give a building its visual identity.

3. HOW LIGHT OR SPACE MOVES
   How natural light enters, how circulation flows between inside and outside, how a sequence of spaces unfolds. Spatial experience is what elevates a building from object to architecture.

4. CONTEXTUAL OR CULTURAL SPECIFICITY
   If the project responds to a specific cultural tradition, local condition, or unusual program requirement in a way that shaped the architecture visibly — not just programmatically.

NEVER PICK:
- Program statistics: square footage, number of occupants, number of bedrooms, building function stated as a fact ("a monastery for 20 nuns")
- Award names or accolades unless there is truly nothing else
- Budget, timeline, or process details
- Client type or demographic information
- Anything that describes the project brief rather than the built result

THE TEST: Would this detail be visible in a photograph? If the answer is no, it is program, not architecture. Choose a different detail.

SHOWING VS. LABELING:
If the detail already communicates what is distinctive, do not add a descriptor word that summarizes it.

WRONG: "a collaborative project designed with four First Nations communities" — "collaborative" labels what "four First Nations communities" already shows.

RIGHT: "a project designed with four First Nations communities" — the detail carries its own weight.

WRONG: "a heavy timber chapel for 20 nuns on a rocky cliffside" — "for 20 nuns" is program. The architecture is the timber structure and the cliffside.

RIGHT: "a heavy timber chapel rising from a rocky cliffside above the Squamish Valley" — site, material, and structure. Everything visible.

COMPRESSION RULE:
The detail should be one focused observation, not a compressed project description. If your detail contains three or more distinct ideas, you are summarizing the project, not selecting a detail. Pick the single most photographable element and let it breathe.

CLOSING PHRASE
---

The closing phrase ends the opener sentence naturally. It should signal selectivity — that the project is what prompted the outreach — and tie the detail back to the decision to reach out.

APPROVED CLOSING PHRASE PATTERNS:
Use one of these three patterns. Write a fresh version each time, but the structure should follow one of these:

1. "exactly what I look for in a project when deciding who to reach out to."
2. "exactly the kind of project that makes me want to reach out."
3. "exactly what I look for in a project before reaching out."

WHAT THESE DO RIGHT:
- Tie the project detail back to the decision to reach out
- Signal selectivity without evaluating quality
- Imply a photographer's filter without sounding stiff
- Feel natural, not scripted

WHAT TO AVOID IN CLOSING PHRASES:
- Implying you want to shoot that specific project (it may already be done)
- Generic phrases that could follow any project from any company
- Language about "process" or "documenting" that sounds stiff
- Phrases from the banned list

TONE:
Selective and interested. The energy comes from Matt being particular about whose work he documents, not from being impressed by it. A photographer who is choosy about who he reaches out to is more compelling than one who is enthusiastic about everything.

HOW TO WRITE IT:
Ask: how would Matt finish this sentence if he were telling a colleague why he reached out to this specific company? Write that.

The closer must feel specific enough that it could not be lifted out and dropped after a different project detail without changing. If it could follow any project from any company, throw it out and write something else.

LENGTH: 5-15 words. Long enough to carry intent, short enough to land clean.

IMPORTANT CONSTRAINT:
The project in the opener is likely already built and may already have been photographed. Never imply you want to shoot that specific project. The closer expresses interest in the company and their work as a whole — the opener project is just the hook that shows you looked.

"I want to photograph that contrast" is wrong — it implies the project is available to shoot.
"exactly the kind of project that makes me want to reach out" is right — it expresses genuine interest without a false premise.

LANGUAGE RULES:
Never use words that imply a specific ICP type.
- "firm" or "practice" implies Architect
- "build" or "site" implies Builder
- "studio" implies Designer
Use neutral language that works across all three.

BANNED — never use these:
- "and it stuck with me"
- "and it stayed with me"
- "and it stopped me mid-scroll"
- "that caught my eye"
- "worth documenting properly"
- "the kind of work I look for"
- "the kind of work I focus on"
- "that I found hard to look away from"
- "exactly the kind of work I look for"
- "I want to photograph that"
- "I'd love to shoot that"
- "the kind of work I built my process around documenting"
- Any phrase used in a previous email this batch

NEVER USE:
- Superlatives: amazing, stunning, incredible, beautiful
- Compliments that evaluate quality rather than signal intent
- Anything that sounds like marketing copy
- Em dashes or semicolons
- Words that imply a specific ICP type

STEP 5 — WRITE THE VALUE PROP
---

Two sentences. Hard rule.

SENTENCE 1 — WHAT YOU DO:
Identify yourself as a photographer. Mention documentary-style video naturally. One clear thought.

Must include "I photograph" or "I shoot" in sentence 1.
Must mention documentary-style video in sentence 1.

SENTENCE 2 — WHAT IT GETS THEM:
Name the business outcome specific to their ICP. Not the deliverable — the result. What does having this work do for them in their market?

ARCHITECT — write fresh each time:
Sentence 1: I photograph completed architectural projects and produce documentary-style video of the design process.
Sentence 2: The kind of work that helps juries, editors, and peers read design intent without needing a project description.

BUILDER — write fresh each time:
SENTENCE 1 LOGIC: Sentence 1 should frame Matt's work around the build process — milestones and documentary video both live naturally here. Do not try to also include "completed homes" in sentence 1. The finished result is implied by sentence 2 (future clients see what you deliver). One clean thought, one "and" only.
Sentence 1: I photograph custom builds at key milestones and produce documentary-style videos of the full build process.
Sentence 2: The kind of work that shows future clients what your team delivers before you ever walk a site together.

DESIGNER — write fresh each time:
Sentence 1: I photograph completed interiors and produce documentary-style video that captures the thinking behind the space.
Sentence 2: The kind of work that helps the right clients and editors find you for the right reasons.

SECONDARY ICP — write fresh each time:
Sentence 1: I photograph finished installations and produce documentary-style video that shows custom work in the context of the projects it was built for.
Sentence 2: The kind of documentation that makes the craft visible before the next project covers it up.

GENERIC — one sentence allowed:
"I photograph projects at this level and produce documentary-style videos so the craft and thinking behind the work are as visible as the finished result."

NOTE: The above are reference structures, not templates. Write a fresh version for each email. The core elements must be present but phrasing must vary. If two builders received your emails side by side they should read like two different messages.

HARD REQUIREMENTS:
- Under 30 words per sentence
- One "and" maximum per sentence. If a sentence needs two "and" conjunctions, it is trying to do too much. Simplify until it reads as one clean thought.
- Never use: capture your vision, showcase your work, tell your story, level up, feed your channels, boost your social, become part of the narrative, materiality, spatial sequence
- Sound like explaining what you do to a peer at a dinner party

STEP 6 — WRITE THE CTA
---

One sentence. An invitation, not a close.

DEFAULT:
"If any of this sounds interesting, I'd love to send you a few samples of my work."

Adapt phrasing to flow naturally from the value prop but keep the core the same: one conditional statement, one ask (send samples), zero pressure.

NEVER:
- Offer a call or meeting
- Create urgency
- Give two options
- Ask a question
- Use "I'd love to" anywhere except the CTA

STEP 8 — SCAN AND FIX
---

HARD BANS — rewrite if found anywhere:
- Em dashes (— or --) or semicolons
- Superlatives: amazing, stunning, incredible, beautiful, gorgeous, exceptional, breathtaking, extraordinary
- Cliches: capture your vision, showcase your work, tell your story, level up your brand, feed your channels, boost your social, tell a better story, become part of the narrative
- Urgency: before sites change, before details are forgotten
- Dropped pronouns: "Came across" instead of "I came across"
- Filler intensifiers: honestly, truly, really, actually, genuinely, certainly, definitely

TONE VIOLATIONS — rewrite the sentence:
- Hedging: "I think maybe you might" — be direct
- Double asks: samples AND a call — samples only
- Vendor language: anything that sounds like a pitch rather than a peer reaching out
- Labeling instead of showing: if the detail already communicates what is distinctive, do not add a descriptor word that summarizes it
- Program details in opener: if the opener contains square footage, occupant counts, building function stated as fact, or other brief-sourced data instead of architectural or site observations, rewrite using the DETAIL SELECTION PRIORITY ORDER
- Compressed project descriptions: if the opener detail contains three or more distinct ideas crammed into one phrase, it is a summary, not a detail. Pick the single most photographable element and rewrite.
- Contradictory framing: For BUILDER emails, sentence 1 should frame around the build process, not completed homes. "Completed homes at milestones" is contradictory. The finished result is implied by sentence 2. Rewrite if sentence 1 tries to cover both timeframes.
- Double conjunction: If any sentence contains two "and" conjunctions, it is overloaded. Simplify until it reads as one clean thought with one "and" maximum.

CAPITALIZATION CHECK:
Every proper noun — project names, firm names, place names, people's names — must be capitalized correctly.
Never start a proper noun with a lowercase letter.
"salishan place" is wrong. "Salishan Place" is correct.

- Opener includes "on your site"
- Opener references specific project or detail from PROJECT DETAILS field
- Location included in opener if available
- Detail is architectural, material, spatial, or site-specific — not program, not stats, not awards
- Detail is one focused observation, not a compressed project summary
- Closing phrase follows one of the three approved patterns
- Closing phrase does not imply shooting that specific project
- All proper nouns correctly capitalized
- Value prop is 2 sentences: what you do + business outcome
- CTA is one sentence offering samples
- Signature block present
- No word appears more than twice in the whole email
