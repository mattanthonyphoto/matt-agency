# Architect ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 27, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Designer-Entrepreneur Tension

Architects trained for 7-12 years to become licensed designers, not business operators. Architecture school rewards design excellence and peer critique — not financial management or marketing. When they open firms, they must suddenly become entrepreneurs without training.

**Identity:** They see themselves as the **authors of the design intent** — the ones who conceived the vision everyone else brings to life. This authorship identity drives everything: how they buy, what they value, why photography matters.

**Key traits:**
- **Design-obsessed.** Quality of the built environment is their measure of success.
- **Perfectionist.** 7-12 years of training instills extreme attention to detail.
- **Peer-recognition-driven.** Awards and publications matter enormously to their sense of professional worth.
- **Skeptical of business/marketing.** They became architects to design, not to market.
- **Intellectually rigorous.** They respect people who understand what they're doing at a conceptual level.

### Demographics
- **Age:** Median principal age 51. 66% are 40+.
- **Gender:** 73.8% male principals, 14.6% female (changing slowly)
- **Education:** Minimum 5-year B.Arch or 4+2 M.Arch + 3,740 hours internship + licensing exams
- **Firm size:** 75% of firms have fewer than 10 employees. 28% sole practitioners.

### Values
- Design excellence over revenue
- Peer recognition over client satisfaction (though they need both)
- Legacy — want buildings documented and remembered
- Intellectual rigor — respect design literacy in collaborators
- Authorship — the design vision is theirs

### How They Differ From Builders
- Builders measure success by schedule, budget, and construction quality
- Architects measure success by **design excellence, peer recognition, and integrity of the original vision**
- A builder wants photos that show craftsmanship and sell the next project
- An architect wants photos that **explain the design** — light, space, materials, site response

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week (Principal, 5-15 Person Firm)
- ~50% billable (20 of 40 hours on project work)
- Other 20 hours: biz dev, client meetings, project oversight, financials, HR, admin
- Most work ~45 hours/week
- They became architects to design but spend most time on everything else

### What Keeps Them Up at Night
1. **Cash flow.** Clients average 97 debtor days to pay. Heaviest work happens when cash burns fastest.
2. **Fee pressure.** Race to the bottom. Expected to do more with less.
3. **Losing design intent.** Value engineering strips their vision.
4. **Not being taken seriously.** Profession's perceived value has eroded.
5. **Time poverty.** Administrative burden consumes creative work.

### The Fear That Connects to Photography
Their best design work will never be seen because they can't afford to photograph it, submit for awards, or get published. Every unphoto­graphed project is a lost portfolio piece, lost award submission, lost publication opportunity.

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Decides
In firms of 2-20 people, **the principal almost always decides.** Marketing coordinators (where they exist) handle logistics, but the principal selects the photographer and approves creative direction.

### Purchase Triggers
1. Project completion — primary trigger
2. Awards deadline approaching (AFBC, Governor General's, Wood Design, Architizer A+)
3. Publication opportunity (Dezeen, ArchDaily, Canadian Architect want professional images)
4. Website feels stale — 2-3 projects unshot, portfolio dated
5. Competing firm gets published — jealousy is real
6. Cost-share prompt — builder or designer says "we're photographing, want in?" (30-50% of architect photography initiated this way)

### Budget Reality
- Small residential: $1,500-$3,500
- Mid-range custom: $3,000-$6,000
- High-end custom: $5,000-$10,000+
- BC market: $3,000-$8,000/project (base + licensing)
- Matt's pricing ($3,500 / $5,500 award-ready) = sweet spot for BC

**More price-sensitive than builders** — architect margins thinner (10-20% net), fees are 8-15% of construction cost. A $3,500 photo shoot is a bigger chunk of their project revenue.

**Cost-sharing is the unlock.** $3,500 becomes $1,200-$1,750 split with a builder. This is how you get architects who "don't have budget" to say yes.

### How They Evaluate Photographers (Different From Builders)
1. **Understanding of design intent** — #1 criterion. "Must understand the building and what the architect is trying to say."
2. **Portfolio variety without sameness** — "You don't want a sense of sameness."
3. **An interpretive eye** — "Find someone who sees something you don't, but reinforces a theme."
4. **Technical specialization** — tilt-shift, perspective control, invisible lighting
5. **Publication connections** — photographers who help get projects published are far more valuable
6. **Collaborative temperament** — ability to discuss concept and work as a team

### What Architects Want That Builders Don't Ask For
- Design intent photography (what they were trying to achieve, not what it looks like)
- Material and detail studies (how materials meet, how joinery works)
- Spatial sequence (story of moving through the building)
- Context and site relationship
- Twilight/dusk shots (most publication-worthy)

### Good Photo vs. Great Photo (Architect's Perspective)
A good photo: accurately documents with correct verticals, exposure, composition.
A great photo:
- Reveals design intent not obvious standing in the space
- Uses light as a design element
- Communicates materiality (you can feel the texture)
- Creates emotional response
- Has publication-quality composition
- Captures building's relationship to site

### Loyalty
Very loyal — more so than builders. The architect-photographer relationship is the closest creative partnership in construction. But they value "balancing loyalty and variety."

**You enter through a gap:**
- A project type the current photographer doesn't do well
- A cost-share where you're already shooting for the builder
- A recommendation from a trusted peer
- A project the existing photographer wasn't available for

## The Publication Game

### Why It Matters
"Almost two-thirds of new commissions come from reputation." For a private home that gets 50 visitors, 10,000+ see it on ArchDaily. Photography IS the building's public life.

### Publications Ranked
**Tier 1 (Global):** Dezeen (wants exclusivity), ArchDaily (15+ images, 2880px+), Architectural Record, AD
**Tier 2 (Design/lifestyle):** Dwell (perfect for BC), Wallpaper*
**Tier 3 (Canadian):** Canadian Architect (essential), Azure, Western Living (BC-specific, 25K distro)

### The Photographer's Role in Publication
Killer differentiator. Photographers who understand what editors want, shoot to editorial standards, and help prepare submissions provide enormous value. **No photographer in BC explicitly offers structured publication submission service.** This is a genuine competitive moat.

### The Flywheel
Publication → Awards → Peer credibility → Referrals → Premium clients → Better projects → More publication. Photography is the fuel.

## The Language That Works

### Say This
- "Design intent" not "how it looks"
- "Spatial narrative" not "room by room"
- "Material quality" not "finishes"
- "Your project" not "the house"
- "Document" or "interpret" not "shoot"
- Reference sectional relationships, threshold conditions, site response

### Never Say
- "Real estate photography"
- "Making it look good"
- "Marketing content"
- "Staging"
- "Property"

### The One-Line Pitch
**To builders:** "I document your craft."
**To architects:** "I help you get published and win awards."

## The Architect Retainer Model

### Differs From Builder Retainer

| | Builder (Creative Partner) | Architect Retainer |
|---|---|---|
| Primary goal | Marketing + sales enablement | Publication + legacy documentation |
| Content focus | Lifestyle, process, team, brand | Design intent, spatial quality, material narrative |
| Beyond photography | Website, SEO, social, brand | Awards submissions, publication pitching, portfolio curation |
| Image style | Warm, aspirational | Clean, precise, design-forward |
| Relationship | Embedded creative department | Trusted visual documentarian |

### Proposed Tiers

**Portfolio Partner — $2,000/month (12-month)**
- 4 project shoots/year (1/quarter)
- Priority scheduling
- Cost-share coordination
- 1 awards submission package/year
- A la carte equivalent: ~$14,000-$16,000

**Publication Partner — $3,000/month (12-month)**
- 6 project shoots/year
- Everything above, plus:
- 2 publication submissions/year (Canadian Architect, Western Living, Azure, Dwell, ArchDaily)
- Quarterly social media content drops
- Annual portfolio review
- Construction-phase documentation
- A la carte equivalent: ~$25,000-$35,000

### When to Pitch
Don't lead with retainer. Execute 2-3 exceptional shoots first. Prove value. Then propose retainer to systematize what's working.

## BC-Specific Context

### How BC Architects Differ
- Landscape-driven design — climate and landscape determine everything
- Wood as identity — CLT, mass timber, prefab wood = leading innovation
- Sustainability as default — BC Step Code requires highest performance for residential (March 2025)
- Anti-flashy ethos — collaborative, landscape-responsive culture

### The Passive House Angle
- BC = one of strongest PH markets in North America
- BC Passive House Factory in Pemberton (your backyard)
- PH architects need photography showing how buildings perform, not just look
- Construction documentation (air barriers, envelope details) matters for conference presentations
- Key firms: Hemsworth Architecture, Marken Design, RDC Fine Homes

### ~3,000 Registered Architects in BC
- ~511 architecture firms
- 84% in Mainland/Southwest (Metro Vancouver)
- AIBC is regulatory (mandatory), RAIC is voluntary advocacy
