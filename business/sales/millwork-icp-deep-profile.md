# Millwork & Cabinetry ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 30, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Craftsman-Entrepreneur Tension (Amplified)

The craftsman-entrepreneur tension that exists in builders is *even more pronounced* in millwork shop owners. These are people who fell in love with wood — the feel of it, the smell of it, the way a perfectly matched walnut book-match veneer catches light. They started as carpenters, finish carpenters, or furniture makers. Many built cabinets as a hobby for years before making it a business. The defining feature: **they identify as makers, not business people.**

Unlike builders who manage a wide network of trades and must develop general management skills, millwork owners often stayed deep in the craft. They can hand-cut a dovetail, program a CNC router, and debate the merits of pre-catalyzed vs post-catalyzed lacquer. They're technical craftspeople first. Running a business is the thing they do so they can keep making things.

**Key traits:**
- **Perfectionist makers.** Their standard is invisible: a seam that disappears, a grain match so precise you can't find the joint, a door that closes with exactly the right resistance. They notice flaws that no client would ever see.
- **Intensely proud, quietly so.** They don't brag. They let the work speak. But when someone notices the quality — really *sees* the detail — it means everything to them. As one industry observer noted: "Millworkers take pride in quiet wins — a seam that disappears, a door that aligns perfectly, a client who doesn't know why the space feels better, just that it does."
- **Control-oriented.** Many complete every stage in-house — design, manufacturing, finishing — because outsourcing means losing control of quality. Bowerman's in Squamish literally operates the only fully enclosed cross-draft finishing booth in the Sea-to-Sky region to maintain automotive-grade finish standards.
- **Skeptical of anything that isn't tangible.** They deal in physical objects with measurable tolerances. Marketing, branding, and photography feel abstract and unquantifiable.
- **Deep knowledge, poor at articulating it.** They can explain the structural advantages of a mortise-and-tenon joint over a pocket screw, but ask them to write website copy and they freeze.

### Demographics
- **Age:** 45-60 core concentration. Baby boomers own approximately 40% of small businesses in Canada, and the woodworking sector skews older. Many shops founded 15-30 years ago by craftspeople now approaching retirement — half with no succession plan.
- **Gender:** Overwhelmingly male ownership. Spouses play operational roles more often than in building — handling bookkeeping, scheduling, and client communication.
- **Education:** Trade school, Red Seal joinery certification, or self-taught. Formal education is rare and not respected as much as bench time. "I've been doing this for 30 years" carries more weight than any degree.
- **Company size:** Typically 5-25 employees. Average revenue $820K (Canadian industry average, NAICS 32191). High-end custom shops: $1M-$5M. The industry has ~1,506 businesses across Canada.
- **Owner compensation:** $70K-$180K depending on shop size and margins. Lower than builders because margins are tighter and capital equipment costs are higher.

### Values
- Craftsmanship above all — they chose precision work over speed
- Material integrity — they care about wood species, grain patterns, sourcing
- Reputation through work quality, not marketing
- Loyalty to long-term relationships (builders, designers, architects)
- Independence — built the shop to do things their way

### Self-Image vs. Market Perception
- **Self-perception:** Artists working in wood. The people who make the architect's vision actually work. The invisible craftspeople behind every stunning kitchen, library, and reception desk.
- **Market perception:** Varies wildly. Architects and designers who work with high-end shops deeply respect them. Homeowners often don't distinguish between a custom millwork shop and IKEA. The public rarely knows millwork companies exist.
- **The core wound:** Their best work is invisible. When a project is photographed, the photographer captures the room — not the 400 hours of hand-finishing on the walnut panels. When a project wins an award, the architect or designer gets credited. The millwork is "just the cabinets."

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week (Owner, 8-20 Person Shop)
- **6:00-7:00 AM:** In the shop before anyone else. Reviewing CNC programs, checking material deliveries, planning the production schedule.
- **7:00-10:00 AM:** On the shop floor. Troubleshooting a CNC setup, reviewing a finish sample, approving a templating for a complex install. This is the part they love.
- **10:00 AM-Noon:** Client or designer meetings. Reviewing shop drawings, discussing material selections, dealing with change orders. This is tolerable.
- **Noon-3:00 PM:** Back in the shop, or out on install sites checking quality. Problems always surface during installation — scribe issues, site conditions that don't match templates, general contractors who damaged panels during other trades' work.
- **3:00-5:00 PM:** Estimating, purchasing, supplier calls. Figuring out lead times on European hardware (Blum, Hettich, Hafele). Arguing with wood suppliers about veneer quality.
- **5:00-7:00+ PM:** The "office shift" — invoicing, bookkeeping, emails, quoting. The part they hate.
- **Saturdays:** Often in the shop catching up on production or doing finishing work that requires uninterrupted time.

They don't have a marketing problem. They have a **capacity and time problem** — and marketing is the first thing that gets cut when production demands spike.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night

1. **Labor.** The single biggest crisis. 93% of architectural millwork and cabinet manufacturers plan to expand their workforce but can't find people. In BC, 72% of contractors report skilled trades shortages. CNC operators, finishers, and experienced cabinetmakers are nearly impossible to recruit. The average age of skilled woodworkers keeps climbing, and fewer young people enter trades. One shop owner captured it: "The people I hire love wood — they love working with their hands. Our competitive edge is creating an environment that values craftsmanship."

2. **Material costs and availability.** Softwood lumber tariffs at 45%. US tariffs of 25% on Canadian kitchen cabinets and vanities (effective Oct 14, 2025) — affecting ~$610M in annual Canadian exports. Specialty hardwoods, veneers, and European hardware all subject to supply chain disruptions and price increases. Rework costs alone run 4-9% of total project cost when material waste is factored in.

3. **Margin pressure.** Net profit margins for custom millwork typically fall between 8-20%, with the industry benchmark suggesting 15-20% as sustainable. But the gap between estimated margin (avg 29% built into bids) and actual delivered margin (avg 21%) tells the real story — underestimating, rework, and change orders eat profit. Smaller shops actually report slightly higher margins (24%) than large ones (18%), but they're more fragile.

4. **Cash flow.** Custom millwork has a terrible cash flow cycle. Materials must be purchased and production completed before installation, but payment often comes 30-60 days after install. Progress billing helps but doesn't eliminate the gap. A single large project with a slow-paying GC can put a shop underwater.

5. **Finding the right projects.** Commercial millwork is bid work — low margins, competitive, commodity-driven. Residential custom is where the craft lives but requires different relationships (designers, architects, homeowners). The tension: commercial pays the bills, residential feeds the soul.

6. **Technology adoption pressure.** CNC tools market growing at 3.4% CAGR to $548M by 2032. Shops that don't adopt CNC, nesting software, and digital workflows fall behind. But equipment costs $100K-$500K+, requires trained operators (see: labor shortage), and the learning curve is steep.

### What Frustrates Them About Vendors
- Photographers who treat a kitchen like a real estate shoot — overhead lights on, no attention to the cabinetry, wide angles that flatten the joinery detail
- Marketing people who don't understand that a perfectly matched waterfall edge took 4 hours of grain selection
- Website designers who use stock photos of kitchens that look nothing like their work
- People who waste their time — they have production deadlines and can't afford to sit in unnecessary meetings
- Being lumped in with "kitchen renovation" companies when they do architectural-grade work

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Makes the Decision

**$500K-$2M (3-10 employees):** The owner decides everything. Marketing budget is essentially zero — photography would be a one-off expense justified by a specific need. The spouse or office manager may research options, but the owner approves (and likely resists). **The owner's wife/partner is often the one who pushes for better photos** because she manages the website or social media.

**$2M-$5M (10-25 employees):** Owner approves. May have a part-time office manager or estimator who also handles "marketing." More likely to have a website that needs updating. Decision still comes down to the owner seeing a concrete reason to invest.

**$5M+ (25+ employees):** More structured. May have a marketing coordinator or work with a small agency. More likely to submit for AWMAC awards and need professional photography for that. Still founder-led decisions on creative direction.

### What Triggers the Purchase

1. **AWMAC Awards deadline.** The BC Chapter Awards of Excellence (held at Vancouver Convention Centre, 250+ attendees including architects and designers) require photographed projects. This is the single most concrete, time-bound trigger. Projects must be submitted, photographed, surveyed, and inspected.
2. **A showpiece project completes.** The walnut library with integrated LED lighting. The 40-foot reception desk with book-matched veneer. The kitchen where every panel was hand-selected. They know this one is special.
3. **Website embarrassment.** A designer sends them a referral, the prospect visits their site, and the iPhone photos don't reflect the quality of work. They lose the job and finally realize the problem.
4. **Designer or architect request.** An architect says "we're photographing the project — can you participate?" This is the most common entry point, often through cost-sharing.
5. **Competitor visibility.** They see a competitor winning AWMAC awards or featured in Wood Industry magazine, and realize the difference isn't quality of work — it's quality of documentation.
6. **New showroom or shop.** Some higher-end shops have showrooms. Renovating or opening one creates a natural photography need.

### How They Currently Handle Photography

| Method | Prevalence | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Phone photos on install day | Most common | Poor — harsh site lighting, unfinished surroundings, tools in frame |
| Nothing at all | Very common | They finish, leave, move to next job. No documentation. |
| Client sends photos after move-in | Occasional | Cluttered, wrong angles, personal items obscure the millwork |
| Designer's photographer | Rare | Good quality but millwork is background, not subject |
| Professional millwork photographer | Extremely rare | Excellent — but almost nobody does this |

The critical insight: **millwork is almost never the primary subject of photography.** When a home gets shot, it's the architect's or builder's shoot. The millwork company might get a few images as a courtesy, but those images are composed to show the room, not the cabinetry. Close-up detail shots of grain, joinery, hardware, and finish quality almost never happen unless the millwork company commissions them directly.

### How They Evaluate Photographers
1. **Do you understand millwork?** Can you see the detail? Do you know what a book-matched panel is? Will you capture the grain pattern they spent 3 hours selecting?
2. **Portfolio relevance.** Real estate photography is an immediate disqualifier. They want to see detail shots, texture, close-ups of joinery and finish. Ideally, images that make wood look the way it feels.
3. **Low friction.** They can't babysit a photographer. Show up, know what to shoot, deliver quickly.
4. **Referral from a trusted trade partner.** Architect, designer, or builder they already work with says "this is who we use."
5. **Price transparency.** Simple, clear pricing. Not day rates with add-ons.

### What Makes Them Say Yes
- You demonstrate understanding of their craft by noticing and commenting on specific details
- You show millwork-specific images in your portfolio (not just wide room shots)
- Cost-share with the builder or designer removes the "why should we pay for this?" objection
- Clear before/after example of how good photography generated work for another shop
- Quick, painless process that doesn't pull them off the shop floor

### What Makes Them Say No
- Portfolio is all wide shots of kitchens — no detail, no texture, no craft
- Too much marketing language — feels like being sold a service they don't believe in
- Complicated process requiring multiple meetings
- No understanding of what makes their work different from a kitchen reno
- Cost feels unjustifiable because they've never quantified the value of photography

### Price Sensitivity — The Real Story

**More price-sensitive than builders or architects** because their margins are tighter and they've never budgeted for photography. But the objection is value, not price. A shop owner who bills $150/hour for a CNC operator understands the economics of skilled labor — they just haven't connected that to photography.

- Photography budget: typically $0-$3K/year (most spend zero)
- Realistic target: $1,500-$4,000/project for dedicated millwork shoot
- Cost-share entry point: $500-$1,500 as their portion of a shared project shoot
- Sweet spot: **bundle millwork-specific shots into an existing project shoot for the builder/designer at incremental cost.** This is the unlock — they get dedicated detail shots of their work for a fraction of what a standalone shoot would cost.

## Where They Hang Out

### Online (Actually Used)
- **Instagram** — the primary visual platform. They browse, occasionally post. Content is usually inconsistent phone photos from the shop floor or install sites. High-quality millwork Instagram accounts stand out dramatically because the bar is so low.
- **Houzz** — where homeowners find kitchen and cabinetry providers. Critical for residential-focused shops. Portfolio quality here directly impacts lead generation.
- **Facebook** — local community presence, sometimes their only online presence. Facebook business page may serve as de facto website.
- **LinkedIn** — minimal use except for commercial-focused shops networking with GCs and architects.
- **Pinterest** — passive. Their work gets pinned by designers. They rarely manage it themselves.

### What They Read/Listen To
- **Woodworking Canada** — Canada's original and leading industry magazine for wood product manufacturers, published six times yearly
- **Wood Industry** — trade publication covering Canada's secondary wood processing industry

- **Canadian Woodworking & Home Improvement** — more hobbyist-oriented but widely read
- **Woodworking Network** — US-based but widely consumed online for industry news and WOOD 100 data
- **Fine Woodworking** — aspirational. The craft-oriented publication they respect most.
- **WoodWeb forums** — where they ask each other business questions (pricing, marketing, shop layout)

- **AWMAC BC Chapter events** — the primary industry association. 400+ member companies nationally, strong BC chapter based in Burnaby (101-4238 Lozells Ave, 604-298-3555)

### How They Get Referrals (in order)
1. Interior designers — the #1 referral source for residential millwork
2. Architects — especially for commercial and high-end residential
3. General contractors/builders — ongoing relationships, often exclusive
4. Past clients — homeowners who recommend to friends
5. Houzz/Google reviews — growing but still secondary to relationship referrals

## BC Market Context (March 2026)

### Industry Scale
- Canadian millwork industry: $6.8B market, 1,506 businesses (2025)
- Average revenue per business: $820.4K
- 71.7% of businesses reported profits (2023)
- Total industry shipments: $5.8B
- Total exports: $1.0B; imports: $680.1M

### Tariff Impact — The Double Squeeze
- **Softwood lumber:** 45% combined AD/CVD + Section 232 tariffs on Canadian exports to US
- **Kitchen cabinets & vanities:** 25% US tariff (effective Oct 14, 2025). Originally set to escalate to 50% Jan 1, 2026 — delayed to Jan 1, 2027
- **Canadian export exposure:** ~$610M annually in kitchen cabinetry to the US. 20-30% of the Canadian cabinet industry directly impacted.
- **Millwork specifically:** Currently excluded from Section 232 tariffs, but customs rulings around components create uncertainty. Verification with customs brokers recommended.
- **Material cost pass-through:** Even shops that don't export face rising input costs as lumber, hardware, and finishing supplies all increase. European hardware (Blum, Hettich, Hafele) commands 50%+ global market share and prices are rising.
- **Net effect for BC millwork:** Shops focused on local/provincial work are somewhat insulated from direct export tariffs, but material cost inflation squeezes margins regardless. Shops that exported to Washington/Oregon are pivoting to domestic work, increasing local competition.

### BC Housing & Renovation Market
- Vancouver housing starts: +60% in Feb 2026 vs Feb 2025 (driven by multi-unit)
- Broader BC market: sluggish — home sales down ~10% YoY, economist warns new home construction could dip
- Custom home costs: $350-$800/sqft depending on finish level. Custom millwork pushes toward upper range ($100-$200+/sqft premium for high-end finishes)
- Renovation market: steady demand for kitchen and bathroom renovations, which drives cabinet and millwork orders
- Sea-to-Sky corridor: Squamish detached median $1.86M, Whistler $2.3M-$6M+. Luxury renovation demand remains strong in this corridor.

### Technology Trends
- **CNC adoption accelerating.** Multi-axis machines, IoT-enabled predictive maintenance, enhanced 3D modeling and nesting software. Shops that don't adopt fall behind on precision and efficiency.
- **European hardware dominance.** Blum, Hettich, Hafele, GRASS, DTC — combined 50%+ global market share. Soft-close everything. Integrated LED lighting. Touch-to-open mechanisms. This hardware sophistication is a differentiator for high-end shops.
- **Sustainable materials.** Low-VOC finishes, FSC-certified materials, engineered panels designed for shorter production runs. 30% of new hardware lines include recycled metals or composites.
- **Digital workflows.** Shop drawing software, CNC programming, cut optimization (reducing waste up to 20%). The gap between digitally-equipped shops and traditional shops is widening.

### Labor Crisis — BC Specific
- 72% of BC contractors report skilled trades shortages
- Employment in sawmills fell 32% in BC between 2017-2025
- CNC operators and qualified finishers are the hardest roles to fill
- Aging workforce: declining vocational training programs + fewer young people in trades = shrinking talent pool
- Retention challenge: "If the only competitive advantage you offer is a higher paycheck, you're missing the bigger picture." — Marc Sanderson, CEO, INNERGY

## The Language That Works

### How They Talk About Their Work
- "Book-matched" — veneers arranged so grain patterns mirror each other
- "Waterfall edge" — where grain wraps continuously from horizontal to vertical surface
- "Scribe" — fitting millwork precisely to imperfect walls and surfaces
- "Soft-close" — the Blum/Hettich hardware that's now baseline expectation
- "Shop drawings" — the detailed production drawings they create before manufacturing
- "Grain selection" — the process of choosing and arranging wood pieces for visual continuity
- "Hand-rubbed finish" — a finish applied and rubbed by hand for depth and warmth
- "Flush inset" — doors and drawers that sit flush with the frame, requiring extreme precision
- "Reveal" — the intentional gap between elements, measured in 64ths of an inch
- "Run" — a continuous piece of millwork (crown moulding, baseboard)
- "Architectural grade" vs. "builder grade" — the distinction that defines their identity

### Say This
- "Document the craft" not "take photos of your kitchens"
- "Detail" and "close-up" — they want their joinery and grain patterns seen
- "Your work" not "the project" — the project belongs to the architect. The *work* is theirs.
- "Texture" and "finish" — the tactile qualities they obsess over
- "Material story" — how the wood was sourced, selected, and arranged
- Reference specifics: the species, the hardware brand, the finish type. This signals you understand what you're looking at.

### Never Say
- "Kitchen photos" — reduces architectural millwork to a kitchen reno
- "Content" — feels disposable. Their work is permanent.
- "Marketing materials" — they don't think in marketing terms
- "Social media" as the primary value — too abstract for tangible craftspeople
- "Before and after" — this is renovation language, not architectural millwork language

### Killer Lines
1. "Your CNC can hold tolerance to a tenth of a millimeter. Your phone photos hold tolerance to... nothing."
2. "Designers are sharing your kitchen photos on Instagram right now. Are they your photos, or someone else's bad ones?"
3. "The architect gets the award. The designer gets the feature. Your work is in every frame, but your name is nowhere. Professional photos change that."
4. "A book-matched walnut panel took you four hours to select and align. In the right light, from the right angle, a photo can show someone exactly why."
5. "Eighty percent of your business comes from referrals. What do those referrals see when they visit your website?"
6. "You're not competing with other millwork shops on price. You're competing on perceived quality. Photography is how quality gets perceived."

## Photography-Specific Angles

### Why Millwork Photography Is Different

Millwork photography is fundamentally different from architectural photography and requires a distinct approach:

**What must be captured:**
- **Grain patterns and figure.** The chatoyance of quarter-sawn white oak. The cathedral pattern of flat-cut walnut. This requires specific lighting angles — often raking light that reveals depth and texture rather than flat, even illumination.
- **Joinery precision.** The mitered corner where two pieces meet so cleanly you can't find the joint. Close-up, sharp, showing the tolerances they achieve.
- **Finish quality.** The difference between a sprayed lacquer and a hand-rubbed oil finish. The depth of a multi-coat conversion varnish. This requires controlled lighting to show sheen without glare.
- **Hardware integration.** How Blum Servo-Drive touch-to-open mechanisms work. How LED lighting is recessed into shelving. The engineering behind the beauty.
- **Material selection.** Book-matched panels, continuous grain waterfall edges, transition details between species or materials (wood meeting stone, wood meeting metal).
- **Scale and context.** The 12-foot ceiling library wall. The 30-foot reception desk. These need wider contextual shots, but composed to show the millwork as the hero, not just part of the room.

**Why stock photos fail them:**
Stock kitchen photography features generic white shaker cabinets with subway tile — the aesthetic antithesis of custom architectural millwork. A shop that does hand-selected walnut, integrated lighting, and book-matched panels using stock imagery is actively undermining their own positioning. It tells prospective clients: "We make generic work."

**Why the designer's/architect's shoot isn't enough:**
When a designer commissions photography, the cabinetry is *environment*, not *subject*. The photos are composed to show the room — the layout, the styling, the overall design concept. The millwork company gets images where their 400-hour installation is backdrop. They need shots where the wood is the hero: close-ups of grain, detail of joinery, the way light moves across a hand-finished surface.

**AWMAC Awards:**
- **AWMAC BC Awards of Excellence** — annual gala, Vancouver Convention Centre. Categories include Commercial Over $500K, Commercial Under $500K, Private Residence, Export, and Design awards. Recent winners: Stirling Woodworks (multiple categories), Feature Millwork, Beckville Woodcrafts, Cowichan Woodwork. Professional photography is essential for submissions — projects are submitted, photographed, surveyed, and inspected.

**Trade Publications:**

- **Woodworking Canada** — Canada's leading industry magazine (contact: bert@kleisermedia.com)

### The Cost-Share Unlock

The single most important sales strategy for this ICP: **offer millwork-specific add-on shots during existing project shoots for builders and designers.** Structure:

- Builder/designer books the project shoot (their usual photography)
- During the same session, spend an additional 30-60 minutes capturing millwork-specific details: grain close-ups, joinery, hardware, finish quality, material transitions
- Millwork company receives their own curated set of images for $500-$1,500
- They get professional photos of their work that they could never justify commissioning on their own
- You build the relationship and demonstrate value for future standalone shoots

This transforms the millwork company from "too small to be a photography client" to "ongoing add-on revenue on every project where their work appears."

## Objection Patterns & Craft-Language Responses

### Objection 1: "We get all our work from referrals. We don't need marketing."

**The psychology:** This is true — and it's also the trap. Referral-driven businesses feel stable until they're not. When a key architect retires, a designer moves firms, or a builder slows down, the pipeline dries up overnight. Industry data confirms: 80%+ of successful millwork business comes from referrals.

**Response:** "That's exactly right — your work sells itself through relationships. But here's what I see with shops like yours: the referral comes in, the prospect visits your website, and the photos don't match what the designer described. Professional images don't replace referrals — they *close* the referrals you're already getting. When someone hears about your work from a designer, what do they see when they look you up?"

### Objection 2: "Our installer takes photos on site."

**The psychology:** Install photos serve as documentation, not marketing. They're taken under harsh construction lighting, with tools in frame, unfinished surroundings, and a phone camera that compresses the depth and texture that makes wood beautiful.

**Response:** "Install photos are great for your project records. But wood is one of the hardest materials to photograph well — the grain, the finish, the way light moves across a hand-rubbed surface. A phone flattens all of that. It's like showing someone a photocopy of a painting and asking them to appreciate the brushwork. Your finishes deserve light that reveals them."

### Objection 3: "We don't have the budget for professional photography."

**The psychology:** They're comparing the cost to zero — their current spend. The math needs reframing. They bill $150/hour for CNC time, $60-$80/hour for skilled cabinetmakers. They understand the value of specialized skill.

**Response:** "I understand — you've never budgeted for it because it hasn't been part of how you operate. But consider: you bill out a CNC operator at $150/hour because that machine and that operator produce work a hand tool can't match. Photography works the same way. And here's the thing — when the builder or designer is already photographing the project, I can add dedicated millwork detail shots for a fraction of what a standalone shoot would cost. We're talking about a few hundred dollars to capture work you spent hundreds of hours on."

### Objection 4: "The designer/architect already photographs the projects we're in."

**The psychology:** They're getting incidental coverage, not dedicated documentation. The designer's photos show a beautiful kitchen — the millwork is part of the scene, not the subject.

**Response:** "That's great — and those photos serve the designer's portfolio beautifully. But I'd bet those images show the room, not the work. Do they capture the grain pattern you spent three hours selecting? The mitered joint where two panels meet at the waterfall? The way your finish catches light differently than a stock cabinet would? When a designer sends you those photos, do they show a prospective client what *you* do, or what the *room* looks like?"

### Objection 5: "We're too busy. I can't coordinate a photo shoot right now."

**The psychology:** Real. They're understaffed, over-committed, and every hour off the shop floor costs money. Photography feels like a luxury when you're trying to meet install deadlines.

**Response:** "That's exactly why I shoot the way I do. I coordinate with the builder or designer on timing. I show up when the install is done and before the homeowner's furniture arrives — that window exists on every project. You give me the address, I handle everything else, and the photos show up in your inbox. The only thing I need from you is ten minutes on the phone telling me what you're most proud of in this project."

### Objection 6: "Photography is for bigger companies. We're just a small shop."

**The psychology:** Imposter syndrome meets frugality. They don't see themselves as a "brand" — they're a shop. Photography feels like something large commercial millwork companies do, not a 12-person operation in an industrial park.

**Response:** "The small shops are actually the ones who benefit most. The big commercial outfits compete on price and volume — they don't need to differentiate. You compete on craft. And the only way someone who hasn't stood in your shop can understand the difference between your work and a big-box kitchen is to *see* it, properly photographed. Some of my best work is with shops your size — because the craft is real, and it shows."

### Objection 7: "We submitted for AWMAC awards before with our own photos and it was fine."

**The psychology:** "Fine" may mean they submitted and didn't win. Or they won despite the photos. Either way, they're rationalizing the current approach.

**Response:** "AWMAC submissions are judged by architects, designers, and industry peers who know what they're looking at. When two shops submit comparable quality work, the one with professional photography has a significant advantage — the jury can actually see the craftsmanship. Stirling Woodworks and Feature Millwork consistently win at the BC Awards of Excellence, and consistently present their work with professional imagery. It's not a coincidence."

### The AWMAC Awards Play

The single highest-ROI prospecting strategy for this ICP:

1. **Identify the AWMAC BC Awards of Excellence submission deadline** (typically submissions due ~6 months before the September gala)
2. **Contact AWMAC BC award committee or chapter office** (info.bc@awmac.com, 604-298-3555) to understand photography requirements and timing
3. **Offer "AWMAC Awards Photography Package"** — dedicated shoot designed to produce award-winning submission imagery, including the detail shots, context shots, and specific compositions that win awards
4. **Time outreach for 2-3 months before submission deadline** — when shops are thinking about which projects to submit but haven't solved the photography problem yet
5. **Use previous winners as case studies** — "Here's the level of photography that won last year. Let's make sure your entry looks like that."

## The Emotional Journey of Hiring a Photographer

### Hesitations
- "Is this really going to help? We've done fine without professional photos."
- "I don't want some photographer moving my work around or staging it wrong."
- "The last time we tried to update the website, it was a nightmare."
- "What if the photos are good but we still don't win the award?"
- "I'd rather spend that money on a new router bit or hire another finisher."

### What Creates Loyal Repeat Clients
- **Seeing their craft properly captured for the first time.** The moment a shop owner sees a photo that captures the grain pattern the way it looks in person — not the flat, washed-out version from a phone — they're converted. This is the emotional trigger that turns skeptics into advocates.
- **Detail shots they've never had.** Close-ups of joinery, grain matching, finish depth. Images they can point to and say "this is what we do."
- **Making the invisible visible.** Photographing the precision, the material selection, the hand-finishing — the things that cost the most time and money but are hardest to communicate.
- **Zero friction.** No meetings, no complicated staging requests. Show up, capture it, deliver it.
- **Speed around deadlines.** AWMAC submissions, website updates, designer requests — deliver fast when it matters.
- **Giving them credit.** When the photo is used in a publication or award submission and the millwork company is properly credited, the loyalty is permanent.

## Summary: The Millwork Photography Opportunity

**Market size:** 1,506 Canadian millwork businesses, ~150+ in BC (AWMAC BC chapter alone has significant membership). Virtually zero of them have a dedicated photography relationship.

**Entry strategy:** Cost-share add-ons during existing builder/designer shoots. Dedicated AWMAC Awards photography packages. Free or low-cost "proof of concept" shoots for 2-3 local Sea-to-Sky shops to build portfolio and testimonials.

**Revenue model:** $500-$1,500/project as add-on revenue (10-15 projects/year = $5K-$22.5K). Standalone millwork shoots at $1,500-$4,000 for award-track clients. Potential Creative Partner retainer for 1-2 high-volume shops at $1,000-$1,500/month.

**Competitive advantage:** Almost no architectural photographers specifically market to millwork companies or offer millwork-specific detail photography. This is an underserved niche within an already underserved market. The shops that photograph well stand out dramatically because the industry standard is phone photos or nothing.

**The pitch in one line:** "You spend hundreds of hours making the wood perfect. I spend two hours making sure everyone can see it."
