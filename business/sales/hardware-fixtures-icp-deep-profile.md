# Hardware & Fixtures ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 30, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Curator-Retailer Tension

Hardware and fixtures showroom owners occupy a unique identity crisis in the construction supply chain. They are **curators trapped in retail bodies.** They didn't open a showroom to move units — they opened it because they have taste, an eye for material quality, and a belief that the details matter. The doorknob, the faucet, the cabinet pull — these are the pieces that occupants actually touch every single day. And yet the market treats them like commodity retailers.

This tension defines everything about how they operate, market, and buy:

**Key traits:**
- **Deeply aesthetic, quietly competitive.** They know finishes, proportions, and material quality at a visceral level. They can tell you the difference between PVD and electroplated brass in their sleep — and they're offended when the market can't.
- **Relationship-dependent.** Revenue lives and dies on designer and builder specification. A single designer who specs consistently through their showroom can be worth $50K-$200K/year. Losing one is existential.
- **Proud gatekeepers.** They see themselves as the bridge between European artisanship and the North American home. They import, curate, and present — they don't just sell.
- **Frustrated educators.** Constantly explaining why their $85 cabinet pull is worth 10x the $8 big-box option. The value proposition is obvious to them and invisible to many buyers.
- **Control-oriented about presentation.** How products are displayed, lit, and contextualized matters deeply. A poorly presented vignette is a personal failure.

### The Sub-Segments

This ICP covers four distinct but overlapping business types:

| Sub-Segment | Core Identity | Key Tension |
|---|---|---|
| **Decorative hardware showrooms** | Curators of door, cabinet, and bath hardware | Artisan taste vs. retail economics |
| **Luxury plumbing fixture suppliers** | European brand representatives and consultants | Specification-driven sales vs. consumer walk-ins |
| **Smart home integrators** | Technology consultants for luxury builds | Cutting-edge innovation vs. reliability expectations |
| **Custom ironwork / artisan metal shops** | Craftspeople and fabricators | Maker identity vs. business scaling |

### Demographics

- **Age:** 45-60 for established showroom owners. Younger (35-45) for smart home integrators.
- **Gender:** Mixed. Many decorative hardware showrooms are family-owned, often with husband-wife partnerships (one sources/manages, one handles clients/designers). Custom ironwork skews heavily male.
- **Education:** Varied. Showroom owners often come from design, sales, or family business backgrounds. Smart home integrators typically have technical education. Ironworkers come through trades.
- **Company size:** Typically 3-20 employees for independent showrooms. Larger chains (Splashes with 29 locations, Ensuite as Emco division) have corporate structures.
- **Revenue range:** Independent showrooms $1M-$10M. Custom ironwork shops $500K-$3M. Smart home integrators $500K-$5M.

### Values

- **Material authenticity.** Hand-forged bronze over stamped zinc. Living patinas over powder coat. Real craftsmanship they can verify and stake their reputation on.
- **Design partnerships.** They exist in service to designers and architects, and the best ones know it. The relationship IS the business.
- **European heritage.** Many position around Italian, German, or Scandinavian manufacturing traditions. "Imported from" carries weight.
- **Longevity over trends.** A Sun Valley Bronze door handle carries a lifetime guarantee. Rocky Mountain Hardware pieces are meant to outlive the home. This permanence is part of the identity.
- **Sensory experience.** Weight, texture, finish, the click of a mechanism — these are the selling tools that no website can replicate.

### Self-Image vs. Market Perception

- **Self-perception:** "We are the jewelry store of the home." Tastemakers. Design consultants. The people who ensure a $3M home doesn't have $30 hardware.
- **Market perception:** To homeowners — expensive and intimidating. To builders — a necessary spec stop that adds project time. To designers — trusted partners (when the relationship exists) or unknown showrooms (when it doesn't).

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week (Showroom Owner)

- **Monday-Tuesday:** Inventory management, vendor communications, placing restock orders. European lead times are 8-16 weeks, so ordering is perpetual.
- **Wednesday-Thursday:** Designer appointments (the revenue days). Walk clients through collections, discuss finish options, pull samples. Site visits to active projects.
- **Friday:** Showroom maintenance, display updates, bookkeeping. Maybe updating the website or social media (probably not).
- **Ongoing:** Trade show planning, catalog reviews, managing custom orders, handling returns and warranty claims.
- **Seasonal surge:** Spring and early summer = specification season. Designers finalizing selections for projects starting in summer/fall.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night

1. **Designer attrition.** If a key designer starts specifying through a competitor showroom (or worse, going direct to manufacturer online), revenue drops overnight. There's no marketing funnel that replaces a designer relationship.

2. **Lead times and inventory risk.** European manufacturers operate on their own timelines. A 12-week lead time from Italy becomes 16 weeks after customs. Carrying inventory ties up cash. Not carrying it means lost sales to quicker competitors.

3. **The Amazon/Wayfair threat.** Consumers Google the model number after a showroom visit, find it 30% cheaper online, and buy direct. Showrooms eat the consultation cost and lose the sale. This "showrooming" behavior is an industry-wide crisis.

4. **Tariff uncertainty.** Canada's 25% tariff on steel derivative products (effective December 2025) directly impacts metal hardware, fixtures, and components. European imports may get preferential treatment through CETA, but the complexity of navigating tariff classifications creates ongoing cost pressure and pricing uncertainty.

5. **Succession and relevance.** Many independent showrooms are first-generation businesses approaching owner retirement. The next generation may not want the business. Meanwhile, Millennials and Gen Z (projected 45% of luxury market by end of 2025) shop differently — digital-first, experience-driven, values-conscious.

6. **Marketing they don't know how to do.** They're product experts, not marketers. Instagram feels mandatory but overwhelming. The website hasn't been properly updated in years. And they're competing with manufacturer websites that have million-dollar content budgets.

7. **Smart home integration complexity.** For integrators: Matter protocol adoption in 2025 solved some interoperability issues, but the pace of product releases means constant retraining. BC's strict data residency and privacy regulations add compliance overhead.

### What Frustrates Them About Vendors

- Photographers who light metal wrong — blown highlights on polished chrome, lost detail on matte finishes
- Anyone who doesn't understand the difference between finishes (brushed nickel vs. satin nickel is NOT the same thing)
- Marketing people who treat hardware as "just accessories" rather than design features
- Vendors who promise and miss deadlines (they deal with enough of that from European manufacturers)

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Makes the Decision

**Independent showrooms (3-15 employees):** Owner/principal decides. Often a personal decision driven by taste and immediate need. Spouse/partner may manage the marketing side and initiate the conversation.

**Multi-location chains (Splashes, Ensuite):** Regional or national marketing manager decides. More process-oriented — RFPs, brand guidelines, corporate approval.

**Custom ironwork shops:** Owner decides. Usually triggered by completing a signature piece or needing portfolio shots for a specific pitch.

**Smart home integrators:** Owner or operations manager. Often needs case study photography to sell high-ticket packages ($50K-$200K+ installs).

### What Triggers the Purchase

1. **Showroom renovation or expansion.** The #1 trigger. New displays need new photography — and the old photos were probably just iPhone shots anyway. Cantu's 25,000 sq ft Vancouver showroom is a perfect example of a space that demands professional documentation.

2. **New product line or brand partnership.** When a showroom picks up a premium line (Waterworks, Brizo, Buster + Punch), they need local photography showing those products in their space and in local installations.

3. **Website overhaul.** The current site uses manufacturer-provided images that look identical to every other dealer's site. They need differentiation.

4. **Designer marketing materials.** Showrooms that actively court designers need high-quality lookbooks, specification sheets with lifestyle imagery, and portfolio pieces showing their products installed in completed projects.

5. **Social media pressure.** Competitors posting beautiful installed shots on Instagram. Their own feed is a graveyard of manufacturer renders and poorly lit showroom snaps.

6. **Trade show preparation.** DPHA Conference (October 22-25, 2026, Salt Lake City), KBIS (February 2026, Orlando), IDS Vancouver — all require polished visual assets.

7. **Award submissions.** DPHA awards recognize products in nine categories (Kitchen Faucet & Fixture, Bathroom Fixture, Cabinet Hardware, Door Hardware, etc.). Professional photography is required for submissions.

8. **Custom project completion.** For ironwork shops and integrators — finishing a statement staircase railing, a custom gate, a whole-home automation install. These are portfolio-defining moments.

### How They Currently Handle Photography

| Method | Prevalence | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturer-provided product shots | Universal | Sterile, identical to every dealer |
| iPhone photos of showroom displays | Very common | Poor lighting, reflections, no context |
| Manufacturer lifestyle renders/CGI | Common | Generic, not their showroom or local projects |
| E-commerce white-background product shots | Occasional | Functional but emotionless |
| Professional showroom or lifestyle photography | Rare | Excellent (usually one-off, not maintained) |

### The Manufacturer Image Problem

This is the critical gap. Every showroom gets the same manufacturer product shots. When a designer visits the Kohler website, then the Splashes website, then the Cantu website — they see identical images. **There is zero differentiation.**

Manufacturer images are:
- Shot in controlled studios, not real spaces
- Styled for a global audience, not the West Coast aesthetic
- Product-focused, not lifestyle or emotion-focused
- Unavailable for custom finishes or local installations
- Often restricted by usage rights for dealer marketing

**The photography Matt provides fills the gap between "here's what the manufacturer says it looks like" and "here's what it looks like in a $3M Sea-to-Sky home with morning light on hand-forged bronze."**

### How They Evaluate Photographers

1. **Understanding of materials and finishes.** Can you capture the warmth of silicon bronze vs. the cool tones of white bronze? Do you know what PVD means? This is the first filter.
2. **Portfolio showing hardware/fixture detail work.** Macro shots, texture detail, finish accuracy. If they see blown-out highlights on polished chrome, they're done.
3. **Lighting mastery.** Hardware is metal. Metal reflects everything. A photographer who can't control reflections on a polished nickel faucet is useless.
4. **Understanding of installed context.** They want to see their products in completed homes — not just on white backgrounds. Lifestyle/installed photography is the differentiator.
5. **Designer referral.** If a designer they trust recommends you, that's more powerful than any portfolio.
6. **Speed and reliability.** They work on tight schedules coordinated around project completions and homeowner move-ins.

### Budget Reality

- **Product photography session:** $500-$1,500 (studio/showroom product shots, 10-20 items)
- **Showroom photography:** $1,500-$3,000 (full showroom documentation, 20-40 images)
- **Installed/lifestyle photography:** $1,500-$3,500 per project (their products in completed homes)
- **Annual marketing budget:** 2-5% of revenue. For a $3M showroom, that's $60K-$150K total marketing spend.
- **Photography allocation:** Typically $5K-$15K/year — often less because they rely on free manufacturer images.

**Price sensitivity:** Moderate. They understand quality costs money (they sell $400 door handles). The barrier isn't price — it's **not knowing they need it.** They've operated on manufacturer images for so long they don't realize how much better they could look.

## The Designer Relationship — The Revenue Engine

### How Specification Works

The specification process is THE revenue driver for luxury hardware showrooms. Here's how it flows:

1. **Designer develops concept** — mood boards, material palettes, overall aesthetic direction
2. **Designer visits showroom** — touches, feels, tests products. This is where the relationship happens.
3. **Showroom consultant guides selection** — cross-referencing finish families, recommending coordinating pieces, alerting to lead times
4. **Designer specifies products into project documents** — FF&E schedules, specification sheets with exact model numbers and finishes
5. **Builder/contractor orders through showroom** — the specification drives the purchase
6. **Products installed** — final result either validates or undermines the specification

**Key insight:** The showroom's job is to make the designer's job easier. The designer doesn't want to research 40 faucet options. They want a trusted showroom to present the best 5, explain the tradeoffs, and handle the logistics.

### What Showrooms Need From Photography

Photography serves this relationship in two critical ways:

1. **Specification support:** Showing products installed in real local projects gives designers confidence to spec them. "Here's what our Waterworks line looks like in a Whistler renovation" is 10x more convincing than a manufacturer catalog shot.

2. **Portfolio proof:** When a designer considers a new showroom relationship, they look at the showroom's portfolio. Professional installed photography signals credibility, taste, and quality — the same signals the designer uses for their own portfolio.

## BC Market Context

### The Tariff Landscape (March 2026)

- **Steel derivative tariffs:** 25% on steel derivative products from all countries, effective December 26, 2025. Directly impacts metal hardware, fixtures, hinges, pulls, and structural ironwork components.
- **European preferential treatment:** CETA (Canada-EU Trade Agreement) provides reduced tariff rates for European imports — significant for showrooms importing Italian, German, and Scandinavian hardware. Caps of ~10-15% vs. 25% for non-CETA countries.
- **Copper impact:** +36% tariff increase on copper affects plumbing fixtures, faucets, and bronze hardware. Rocky Mountain Hardware and Sun Valley Bronze use silicon bronze alloys (copper, silicon, zinc) — directly affected.
- **Net effect:** Luxury hardware prices rising 10-20% across the board. Showrooms absorbing some margin to stay competitive. Creates urgency to justify premium pricing through better presentation.

### BC Luxury Home Market (2026)

- Luxury in 2026 means "a home that connects: to nature, to the craft behind it, and to the people living in its spaces." This aligns perfectly with artisan hardware positioning.
- Sea-to-Sky corridor trending toward layered materials, warmth, and performance as symbols of sophistication.
- Kitchen trends: 60% of designers favouring minimalism with slab doors and concealed appliances — which makes hardware choices MORE critical, not less. When everything is minimal, the hardware IS the design statement.
- "Closed concept" and zoned kitchens returning — more rooms = more hardware.
- Outdoor living in Squamish/Whistler requires marine-grade and weather-resistant fixtures — a specification expertise opportunity.

### Smart Home Integration in BC

- Canada smart home market: USD $4.18 billion in 2025, growing at 10.1% CAGR to $6.77 billion by 2030.
- BC specifically driving growth due to high tech adoption, urban density, and sustainability mandates.
- BC's strict data residency and privacy regulations (beyond federal PIPEDA) push smart home integrators toward edge processing and localized data storage — a differentiator for BC-based firms.
- Luxury buyers expect smart lighting, security, and climate control as baseline in modern builds.
- Crestron, Lutron, and Savant dominate the luxury segment. Photography of these invisible-but-expensive systems is uniquely challenging and valuable.

## The Language That Works

### Say This

- "The **jewelry of the home**" — they already think this way. Validate it.
- "**Finish accuracy**" — the #1 technical concern. Can you capture the difference between polished brass and satin brass?
- "**Living finish**" / "**patina**" — language borrowed from Rocky Mountain Hardware. Bronze that ages, brass that develops character. This is the story they want told.
- "**Specification-grade imagery**" — images good enough for a designer to confidently spec from.
- "**Installed context**" — their products in real homes, not on white backgrounds.
- "**Tactile quality**" — the weight, the click, the temperature of metal. Photography that makes you want to reach out and touch it.
- "**Curated collection**" — not "inventory." They curate. Acknowledge it.
- "**Design intent**" — how the architect or designer envisioned the hardware functioning within the whole. Matt's existing positioning language.
- "**Material story**" — hand-poured bronze at 2,200 degrees. Twelve patina combinations from two alloys. The making IS the marketing.
- "**Showroom experience**" — the reason they exist. The sensory, tactile, consultative experience that no website can replicate.

### Never Say

- "Product shots" (sounds like Amazon e-commerce)
- "Stock photos" (they hate being compared to generic imagery)
- "Content" (too marketing-speak for this audience)
- "Hardware store" (they are NOT a hardware store)
- "Accessories" (hardware is not an accessory — it's a design element)
- "Affordable" or "value" (they sell premium; don't undercut their positioning)
- "Quick and easy" (craftsmanship takes time; they respect that)

### Killer Lines

1. **"Every designer who visits your showroom should see your products in homes like the ones they're designing — not in a manufacturer's studio in Milan."**

2. **"Your showroom is the only place a designer can feel the weight of that lever set. Your photography should make them feel it from their desk."**

3. **"You're curating the finest hardware in North America. Right now, your website looks identical to every other dealer carrying the same lines."**

4. **"The difference between a $12 pull and a $120 pull is impossible to communicate with a white-background product shot. It takes light, context, and craft to tell that story."**

5. **"When a designer specs your product, they're betting their reputation. Give them installed photography that proves they made the right call."**

6. **"You pour bronze at 2,200 degrees and hand-apply twelve different patinas. The story of how it's made is the story that sells it — and that story needs to be photographed, not described."** (for artisan/ironwork segment)

## Photography-Specific Angles

### The Three Layers of Hardware Photography

**Layer 1: Product / Detail Photography**
- Macro shots showing finish quality, texture, grain
- Focus-stacked images ensuring every surface is sharp
- Controlled lighting to manage reflections on polished metals
- Finish-accurate color — the difference between "satin nickel" and "brushed nickel" must be visible
- Multiple angles: face, profile, installed grip position
- **Why it matters:** Replaces manufacturer images with locally shot, brand-consistent alternatives

**Layer 2: Showroom / Vignette Photography**
- The showroom experience captured — the rows of faucets, the curated display walls, the consultation space
- Lifestyle-styled vignettes showing coordinated hardware suites (matching door hardware, cabinet pulls, bath accessories in a single finish family)
- The "walk-through" feel — images that make a designer want to book a showroom appointment
- **Why it matters:** Differentiates the showroom itself as a destination, not just a product source

**Layer 3: Installed / Lifestyle Photography**
- Products photographed in completed local homes
- Context shots: a hand on a lever set, water flowing from a faucet, morning light catching a bronze pull
- The full room with hardware as a design element within the space
- Before/after potential: builder-grade vs. specified hardware
- **Why it matters:** This is what no manufacturer provides. This is what closes designer relationships.

### Why Manufacturer Photos Aren't Enough

1. **No local context.** A Hansgrohe faucet photographed in a German studio tells a BC designer nothing about how it looks in a West Coast modern kitchen with mountain light.
2. **No differentiation.** Every authorized dealer gets the same image library. Cantu, Splashes, and Ensuite all show the same Kohler product shot on their websites.
3. **No emotional resonance.** Studio product shots are informational. Installed lifestyle photography is aspirational. The first answers "what does it look like?" The second answers "what will my life feel like?"
4. **Usage restrictions.** Manufacturer images often come with usage limitations for dealer marketing, social media, and advertising.
5. **No showroom identity.** The manufacturer's visual brand overwrites the showroom's visual brand. Cantu's 25,000 sq ft showroom experience is invisible online.
6. **Missing the tactile story.** Rocky Mountain Hardware's hand-poured process, the living patina that ages over decades — these stories need to be visually told, not just written about.

### Technical Photography Challenges (What Sets You Apart)

- **Reflection control on polished metals.** Chrome, polished nickel, polished brass — these are mirrors. Controlling reflections while maintaining the shine requires specific lighting technique and post-production skill.
- **Finish differentiation.** Capturing the visible difference between 12 patina options on bronze requires precise color accuracy in both capture and delivery.
- **Scale communication.** A cabinet pull photographed alone tells you nothing about its proportion on a drawer face. Installed context photography solves this.
- **Texture at macro level.** Hand-forged bronze has surface variations that machine-made hardware doesn't. Capturing this texture IS capturing the value proposition.
- **Water and fixture photography.** Flowing water from a luxury faucet requires specialized timing and lighting. The cascade of a rain showerhead is a technical challenge worth mastering.

## Objection Patterns & Responses

### Objection 1: "We get product images from our manufacturers."

**Psychology:** This is the default position. They've always used manufacturer images. It feels free. Changing feels like an unnecessary expense for something that "works."

**Response:** "Every showroom carrying those brands gets the same images. When a designer is comparing your website to three competitors, they're seeing identical photography. The question isn't whether those images are good enough — it's whether they differentiate you at all. Your showroom is a 25,000-square-foot experience. Your website should reflect YOUR space, YOUR curation, YOUR expertise — not a manufacturer's studio in Milan."

---

### Objection 2: "Photography is expensive for what we'd get."

**Psychology:** They're used to getting "free" manufacturer images. Any photography spend feels like it goes from zero to thousands instantly.

**Response:** "You sell a single door lever set for $400-$800. One additional specification from a designer who discovered you through better photography pays for the entire shoot. And that specification doesn't happen once — it becomes a relationship that repeats for years. You're not paying for photos. You're investing in the asset that makes designers choose you over the showroom down the road."

---

### Objection 3: "Our showroom speaks for itself — designers come in and see the product."

**Psychology:** True pride in the physical space. Belief that the in-person experience is irreplaceable (and they're right about that). But blind spot: designers research online BEFORE visiting.

**Response:** "Your showroom is extraordinary — that's exactly the point. But a designer narrows their shortlist to two or three showrooms before they ever walk through your door. That decision happens on your website and Instagram. If your online presence doesn't match the experience of being in your space, you're losing specification appointments to showrooms with better photography and worse product."

---

### Objection 4: "We don't have time to coordinate a shoot."

**Psychology:** They're already managing European lead times, designer appointments, inventory, and displays. Adding a photography project feels like another operational burden.

**Response:** "I handle the logistics. You tell me when the showroom is quietest or when a new display goes up, and I show up ready. For installed project shots, I coordinate directly with the builder or designer — I'm usually already shooting their home. Your products just get documented as part of that shoot. Zero additional time from you."

---

### Objection 5: "We tried photography before and the images didn't look right — the finishes were off."

**Psychology:** Legitimate burned experience. A generalist photographer lit the chrome too hot, made the brass look yellow, couldn't capture the difference between satin and brushed. This is a real and valid concern.

**Response:** "Finish accuracy is non-negotiable — I understand that. I photograph architectural metalwork, interior hardware, and building materials as my core practice. I know the difference between PVD and electroplated finishes, and I calibrate for accurate color rendition. I'm happy to do a test shoot on a few pieces so you can evaluate the results before committing to anything larger."

---

### Objection 6: "We're a small showroom — we can't justify the spend."

**Psychology:** Smaller independents feel photography is for the big chains. Marketing budget is limited and photography competes with trade show fees, digital advertising, and showroom maintenance.

**Response:** "The smaller you are, the more differentiation matters. Splashes and Ensuite have corporate marketing budgets. Your advantage is personality, curation, and the personal touch. Professional photography amplifies exactly what makes you different — your eye, your space, your relationships. A single shoot gives you 12 months of Instagram content, an updated website, and spec sheets that make designers take notice."

---

### Objection 7: "Our customers want to see and touch products in person — photography doesn't matter as much for us."

**Psychology:** Deeply held belief in the tactile sales model. And they're not wrong — the showroom IS the selling tool. But this creates a blind spot about how people find the showroom in the first place.

**Response:** "You're absolutely right that nothing replaces the in-person experience. But photography is what gets them through your door. A designer with 30 projects a year can't visit every showroom for every project. They shortlist based on what they see online. Your photography doesn't replace the showroom — it earns the appointment."

---

### Objection 8: "We're a custom ironwork shop — our clients come through builder/architect referrals, not marketing."

**Psychology:** Artisan makers who believe their craft speaks for itself. Portfolio is mostly word-of-mouth and a few job site photos.

**Response:** "Referrals start the conversation. But the architect who refers you also sends the client to your website — and that client is spending $50K on a custom staircase railing. If your portfolio is phone photos from the shop floor, the client doesn't see the artistry that the architect is recommending. Professional images of your installed work close the referral that word-of-mouth opens."

## The Cost-Share Angle

This ICP has a natural cost-share dynamic that Matt should leverage:

**Scenario 1:** Matt shoots a completed custom home for a builder. The home has Cantu-specified hardware throughout. Matt photographs the hardware details as part of the shoot. The builder pays for the project photography. Matt approaches Cantu: "I just documented a home full of your products. Want the hardware detail images?"

**Scenario 2:** A designer specs through Cantu for a Whistler renovation. The designer hires Matt for the project reveal. Matt captures installed hardware details. Both the designer and Cantu benefit from the images. Three-way cost share: builder + designer + showroom each pay 33%.

**Scenario 3:** A custom ironwork shop completes a statement staircase for an architect's project. Matt is already shooting for the architect. The ironwork detail shots are a natural extension. Cost-share between architect and ironwork shop.

**The pitch:** "I'm already in these homes photographing for your design partners. Your products are in the frame. For a fraction of a standalone shoot cost, you get specification-grade installed photography of your products in real local homes."

## First Moves

1. **Audit Cantu Bathrooms' online presence.** Compare their website/Instagram to the actual showroom experience. Document the gap. Use this as a case study for approach.
2. **Identify which designers in Matt's network specify through which showrooms.** LRD Studio (existing client) likely specs through Cantu or Splashes. That's the warm introduction.
3. **Build a "Hardware Detail" section in Matt's portfolio.** Pull existing detail shots from completed projects that show cabinet hardware, plumbing fixtures, door hardware. These shots already exist in the archives — they just need to be surfaced and contextualized.
4. **Approach through the designer relationship, not cold.** "I just shot a project for [designer]. Your hardware looked incredible. I pulled some detail shots — happy to share them for your marketing."
5. **Target one showroom, one ironwork shop, one integrator.** Don't spray. Build three case studies that prove the model for each sub-segment.
