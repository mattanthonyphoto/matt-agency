# Landscape Architect ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 30, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Steward-Designer Tension

Landscape architects trained for 6-8 years (4-year BLA or 2-3 year MLA after undergrad, plus supervised internship and exams) to become licensed professionals. But the core tension that defines them is different from building architects: they sit between **environmental stewardship and client demands**. They were drawn to the profession because they care about ecology, habitat, water systems, and the health of the land. Then they must design outdoor spaces for clients who often want manicured lawns, swimming pools, and high-maintenance ornamental plantings.

**Identity:** They see themselves as **stewards of the land who design with nature, not against it**. This ecological identity shapes everything: what language resonates, what photography matters, which projects they are proud of. They are not "landscapers" — and this distinction is a deep sore spot.

**Key traits:**
- **Ecologically minded.** Environmental health is their north star, not aesthetics alone.
- **Systems thinkers.** They think in terms of water flow, soil health, sun exposure, plant communities, and habitat corridors — interconnected systems, not isolated features.
- **Patient.** Their work takes years to mature. A building is done on move-in day. A landscape reaches its vision in 5-10 years.
- **Seasonal.** Their awareness of time, seasons, and cycles is woven into their professional DNA.
- **Underestimated.** They are frequently treated as a secondary consultant after the architect and builder. This creates a chip-on-the-shoulder dynamic similar to interior designers.
- **Title-protective.** In BC, "Landscape Architect" is a legally protected title under BCSLA. Only licensed members can use it. Being called a "landscaper" is genuinely offensive.

### Demographics
- **Gender:** Roughly equal — landscape architecture is one of the most gender-balanced design professions in Canada. Income gap between men and women has narrowed over the past decade.
- **Age:** 54% are under 44. The proportion over 55 has increased from 19% (2011) to 25% (2021) — an aging profession, but not as top-heavy as building architecture.
- **Education:** Accredited BLA (4 years) or MLA (2-3 years post-undergrad) + supervised internship + LARE exams for licensure.
- **Firm size:** Median firm has ~4 staff and ~$570K gross annual revenue (ASLA 2024). The vast majority are small practices.
- **Visible minorities:** 14% (up from 10% in 2016), still lower than comparable professions.

### Values
- Ecological integrity over aesthetic showmanship
- Long-term performance over immediate visual impact
- Sense of place — designs that respond to the specific land, climate, and culture
- Climate action — increasingly central to professional identity
- Indigenous reconciliation — growing awareness of traditional land stewardship practices
- Peer recognition through CSLA/BCSLA awards and publications
- Collaboration with other design disciplines (architects, engineers, ecologists)

### How They Differ From Building Architects
- Building architects measure success by design excellence, spatial quality, materiality
- Landscape architects measure success by **how the designed landscape performs over time** — ecological function, stormwater management, habitat value, human well-being
- A building architect wants photos that explain the design intent of the structure
- A landscape architect wants photos that **show the landscape as a living system** — mature plantings, seasonal character, how hardscape and softscape interact, how people inhabit the space

### How They Differ From Builders
- Builders want photos that sell the next project
- Landscape architects want photos that **document the design at its peak expression** — which may be years after installation
- Builders think in terms of before/after transformation
- Landscape architects think in terms of **establishment, maturity, and seasonal evolution**

### How They Differ From Interior Designers
- Interior designers obsess over styling, finishes, and the "Instagram moment"
- Landscape architects value **ecological context** — the relationship between designed and natural environments
- Interior designers photograph in controlled environments
- Landscape architects must photograph in **uncontrolled outdoor environments** where light, weather, and plant growth are variables

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week (Principal, 2-8 Person Firm)
- ~60% of time in CAD/design work (drawings, grading plans, planting plans)
- ~15% site visits — verifying construction against plans, plant installation oversight
- ~15% client meetings, consultant coordination, municipal approvals
- ~10% admin, business development, marketing, invoicing
- Most work 40-50 hours/week, with project deadline crunches pushing to 50-60+
- No two days alike — mix of studio work, outdoor site visits, and meetings
- Seasonal workload: heaviest spring through fall (construction season); winter for design and planning

### What Keeps Them Up at Night
1. **Being treated as an afterthought.** Brought in late, budget already consumed by the house. "Can you just do the planting plan?" when they were designed to shape the entire outdoor experience.
2. **Climate unpredictability.** Drought years killing installations. Extreme rain events overwhelming drainage designs. Fire risk changing plant palettes. Their work is directly exposed to climate volatility in a way buildings are not.
3. **Municipal approval delays.** Stormwater management plans, tree preservation bylaws, development permits — bureaucratic timelines that stall projects and frustrate clients.
4. **Plant availability.** Specifying plants that nurseries cannot supply. Supply chain gaps for native species. Substitutions that compromise the design.
5. **Fee pressure.** Landscape architecture fees are typically 8-15% of landscape construction cost — smaller budgets than building architects get. Clients often view landscaping as the part to trim.
6. **Proving ROI.** Harder to prove the value of good landscape design than good building design. "Why pay a landscape architect when I can just hire a landscaper?"
7. **Photography gap.** Their best work is invisible to the public because they never photograph it. The landscape matures 3-7 years post-install, by which time the project relationship has ended and there is no budget for documentation.

### The Fear That Connects to Photography
Their life's work is literally growing and changing. Unlike a building that looks its best on opening day, a landscape reaches its vision years later — and **nobody is there to photograph it**. Every unphoto­graphed mature landscape is a lost portfolio piece, lost award submission, lost publication opportunity, and lost proof that their design works as intended. The temporal nature of their medium makes photography both more valuable and more elusive.

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Decides
In firms of 2-8 people, **the principal decides**. There is rarely a dedicated marketing person. In larger firms (15+), a marketing coordinator may handle logistics, but the principal selects the photographer and directs the creative vision.

### Purchase Triggers
1. **Project reaching maturity** — the #1 unique trigger. 3-7 years post-installation, when plantings have filled in and the design is finally "readable." This is different from every other ICP.
2. **Awards deadline approaching** — CSLA Awards of Excellence (January deadline), BCSLA Awards, BCLNA Landscape Awards of Excellence (October deadline).
3. **Seasonal peak moment** — a specific week when the garden is at absolute peak bloom. Miss it and wait another year.
4. **Cost-share initiated by another party** — builder or architect says "we're photographing the project, want in?"
5. **Portfolio feels inadequate** — current work not represented; losing proposals because portfolio is stale.
6. **Publication opportunity** — Landscape Architecture Magazine, Garden Design, Western Living, Canadian Architect want images.
7. **Website refresh** — firm is redesigning their website and needs current project imagery.
8. **Completed hardscape installation** — the built elements (patios, walls, water features) are ready before planting maturity.

### Budget Reality
- **Landscape-only shoot:** $800-$2,500 (smaller scope than full architectural shoot)
- **Combined architecture + landscape:** $3,000-$6,000 (where cost-share makes sense)
- **Return visit for maturity documentation:** $500-$1,500 (half-day, targeted)
- **Marketing budget:** 2-5% of gross revenue for established firms. On a median $570K firm, that is $11,400-$28,500/year total marketing spend. Photography competes with website, print, events.
- **Price sensitivity:** HIGH. Smaller budgets than building architects. Landscape architecture is often the last line item funded. Cost-sharing is not just useful — it is essential.

**Cost-sharing is the unlock.** Landscape architects are the most natural cost-share partners for architectural photography. They are already on the project. They need a subset of the same images. Adding landscape documentation to an existing architectural shoot is a marginal cost increase — $800-$1,500 for the landscape architect's license — that delivers massive value.

### How They Evaluate Photographers
1. **Understanding of plants and seasons** — #1 criterion. "Does this photographer know when to shoot? Can they read a planting plan?"
2. **Ecological sensitivity** — photos that show the landscape as a system, not just a pretty garden
3. **Drone capability** — aerial views are the only way to see a landscape the way it was designed (plan view)
4. **Patience with timing** — willingness to return for peak bloom, golden hour, seasonal conditions
5. **Portfolio showing outdoor work** — interior/architecture specialists who treat landscape as an afterthought are immediately disqualified
6. **Collaborative approach** — ability to discuss the design intent of the planting, not just "make it look nice"
7. **Publication experience** — photographers who can help get landscape projects into magazines

### What Landscape Architects Want That Other ICPs Don't
- **Seasonal documentation** — the same project shot in spring bloom, summer fullness, fall color, winter structure
- **Maturity documentation** — return visits years after installation to show the design has grown into its vision
- **Aerial/drone views** — landscape architecture is designed in plan view. Aerial photography is the only way to see the spatial layout as the designer conceived it
- **Plant detail shots** — texture, form, bloom, how species interact with each other and with hardscape
- **Stormwater/ecological function** — rain garden after a storm, bioswale with water flowing, habitat in use
- **Before/after documentation** — particularly powerful for landscape transformation projects
- **Context shots** — showing how the designed landscape relates to the broader environment (mountains, waterfront, neighborhood)

### Loyalty
Moderate loyalty — less than building architects, more than builders. Landscape architects often have looser, less frequent relationships with photographers because:
- Shoots are less frequent (projects take longer, maturity timing is variable)
- Budget constraints mean they do not photograph every project
- They are often folded into someone else's shoot (the architect's photographer)

**Entry points:**
- Being the photographer already shooting for the builder or architect on a shared project
- Offering a "maturity return visit" at a reduced rate 3-5 years after a project you already shot
- Demonstrating drone capability they do not currently have access to
- Showing a portfolio with genuine understanding of planting design and ecological landscapes

## The Publication & Awards Game

### Why It Matters
Landscape architects face an acute visibility problem. Their work is outside, experienced by passersby but rarely credited. A park, garden, or public space gets used daily but the designer is invisible. Photography + publication is the only way to build professional reputation beyond the immediate project circle.

## BC Market Context

### The Climate-Driven Design Boom
BC's landscape architecture market is being reshaped by three converging forces:

**1. Drought and Water Restrictions**
Metro Vancouver watering restrictions now run May 1 - October 15 annually, limiting lawn watering to one day per week ($250 fines). This is driving massive demand for:
- Drought-resistant native planting design
- Rainwater harvesting systems
- Rain gardens and bioswales
- Permeable paving
- Landscape architects who can design beautiful spaces that survive on minimal irrigation

**2. Fire-Smart Landscaping**
Following successive devastating wildfire seasons, FireSmart BC requirements are reshaping landscape design in the Sea-to-Sky Corridor, Okanagan, and all interface areas:
- Non-combustible 1.5m zone around homes mandatory
- No coniferous trees within 10m of structures
- Gravel/rock mulch replacing bark mulch near buildings
- Fire-resistant plant palette requirements
- Landscape architects must balance FireSmart compliance with design beauty — a tension that creates need for documentation showing it can be done well

**3. Stormwater Management Requirements**
Vancouver's Rain City Strategy and municipal stormwater bylaws across BC require onsite stormwater management. Landscape architects are the lead consultants on:
- Rain gardens
- Bioswales
- Green roofs
- Permeable paving systems
- Water retention and infiltration design

These three forces mean landscape architects are more in demand than ever, AND their work is more technically sophisticated and worth documenting.

### Indigenous Planting and Reconciliation
Growing movement in BC landscape architecture toward:
- Incorporating Indigenous planting knowledge and traditional land management practices
- Using First Nations plant species with cultural significance
- Collaborating with Indigenous communities on public landscape projects
- Referencing works like Nancy Turner's "Ancient Pathways, Ancestral Knowledge" (UBC curriculum)
- This is a differentiator for progressive firms and creates a documentation opportunity

### Outdoor Living Explosion
The post-COVID outdoor living trend has not slowed:
- Outdoor kitchens, fire features, pergolas, year-round comfort systems
- Outdoor "rooms" designed as extensions of interior spaces
- Higher budgets for landscape design on custom homes
- Builders and architects increasingly bringing landscape architects in earlier

### Key BC Landscape Architecture Firms (Prospects)

**Sea-to-Sky / Squamish:**
- **Elevated Outdoor Design** (Squamish) — residential outdoor living, Sea-to-Sky + West Vancouver

## Photography-Specific Angles

### Seasonal Timing (BC/Pacific Northwest)

| Season | Window | What to Capture |
|---|---|---|
| **Early Spring** (March-April) | Bulb bloom, fresh green, cherry blossoms | Spring color, clean hardscape before full leaf-out |
| **Late Spring** (May-June) | Perennial peak, longest days | Peak bloom, full sun, golden hour lasts longest |
| **Summer** (July-August) | Full maturity, outdoor living in use | Lush fullness, evening entertaining, pool/water features |
| **Early Fall** (September-October) | Ornamental grasses, fall color | Warm palette, low-angle light, harvest textures |
| **Winter** (November-February) | Evergreen structure, hardscape, frost | Structural bones, evergreen form, dramatic light |

**Critical BC timing:** Peak bloom in coastal BC is typically May-June. Interior/Okanagan peaks slightly later. Sea-to-Sky can be 2-3 weeks behind Vancouver due to elevation.

### The Maturity Problem (Unique to This ICP)
Newly installed landscapes look sparse and immature. The design vision takes 4-10 years to fully realize. This creates a documentation gap:
- **Year 0-1:** Hardscape looks great, plantings look bare. Good for before/after but not for portfolio.
- **Year 2-3:** Filling in. Starting to show promise. Worth a check-in visit.
- **Year 3-5:** Canopy developing, perennials established, design intent becoming visible. Good time for initial portfolio shoot.
- **Year 5-10:** Peak maturity. This is when the design is fully realized. This is the money shot — but nobody budgets for it because the project ended years ago.

**The business opportunity:** Offer a "Maturity Documentation Package" — schedule a return visit 3-5 years after the initial install. Charge $800-$1,500 for a half-day session. This is a service no photographer in BC currently offers, and it solves the landscape architect's deepest documentation frustration.

### Drone Opportunities
Drone photography is more valuable for landscape architects than for any other ICP:
- **Plan view documentation** — landscape architecture is designed from above. Aerial photos show spatial layout as conceived.
- **Site context** — how the designed landscape integrates with surrounding environment
- **Before/after aerials** — transformation documentation that is impossible from ground level
- **Stormwater function** — capturing rain gardens and bioswales from above during/after rainfall
- **Scale communication** — showing the full scope of large residential or public landscapes
- Matt's **Transport Canada Advanced Drone Pilot certification** is a genuine competitive advantage

### Hardscape vs. Softscape Balance
The photography challenge unique to landscape architecture:
- **Hardscape** (stone, concrete, wood, metal) photographs like architecture — controlled, permanent, consistent
- **Softscape** (plants, lawn, groundcover) is alive and unpredictable — changes with season, weather, maintenance, growth
- The skill is capturing both in one frame where each enhances the other
- Trees create "dark pools" of shadow underneath that break up compositions — requires careful timing and exposure technique
- Wind is the enemy of sharp plant photography — early morning calm is essential

### Light Considerations for Outdoor Spaces
- **Golden hour is everything.** Midday outdoor photography is harsh and flat.
- **Dappled shade** under tree canopy requires careful exposure management
- **Water features** photograph best with overcast sky or early morning calm (reflections)
- **Night/twilight** is powerful for landscape lighting design documentation
- **Seasonal light angle** changes dramatically in BC's northern latitude — winter low-angle light reveals texture and form that summer overhead light flattens

## Objection Patterns

### 1. "Our projects don't photograph well until years later."
**Response:** "That's exactly why I offer maturity documentation — I come back when your planting design has reached its vision. We can shoot the hardscape and structure now, then return in year 3 or 5 for the full story. Your portfolio shows the design at its peak, not just at installation."

### 2. "We usually just get included in the architect's or builder's photoshoot."
**Response:** "That makes sense for the building documentation — but the architectural photographer is there for the house, not the landscape. Your planting design, your stormwater features, your grading — those need attention with someone who understands what you were trying to achieve. I can add dedicated landscape documentation to that same shoot for a fraction of standalone cost."

### 3. "We don't have the budget for professional photography."
**Response:** "Most of the landscape architects I work with get their projects documented through cost-sharing. The builder or architect is already photographing — your share is $800-$1,500 for a full license. That is less than what most firms spend on a single marketing dinner."

### 4. "We just use our phones / take our own photos."
**Response:** "Phone photos work for process documentation and social media in the moment. But for CSLA submissions, your website portfolio, and publication pitches, the standard is 300 dpi professional images with controlled composition. One properly documented project generates more business than ten phone-photo projects in your portfolio."

### 5. "We only do a few residential projects a year."
**Response:** "That actually makes each one more important to document well. If you complete 3-5 projects per year, having even 1-2 professionally shot creates the portfolio diversity that wins the next project. And with cost-sharing, you are talking about $800-$1,500 per project — less than 1% of most landscape construction budgets."

### 6. "Photography doesn't show the ecological function of our work."
**Response:** "That is a real challenge — and it is exactly what I focus on. Drone aerials showing stormwater flow patterns, detail shots of native plant communities establishing, seasonal series showing how your design performs across the year. ### 7. "The homeowner won't let us back on the property."
**Response:** "I handle the homeowner relationship as part of the process — scheduling, communication, what to expect. ### 8. "We are more focused on public/municipal work."
**Response:** "Public landscapes are even more important to document — they are your most visible portfolio pieces and the work that wins CSLA Awards in the Large-Scale and Medium-Scale categories. Plus, drone documentation of parks, plazas, and public spaces shows scope that ground-level photography cannot communicate."

### 9. "We had a bad experience with a photographer who didn't understand our work."
**Response:** "That happens when architectural photographers treat the landscape as background. I approach your planting design with the same intentionality I bring to documenting a building's design intent. I want to understand your plant palette, your grading decisions, your stormwater strategy — so the images communicate your design thinking, not just how the space looks."

### 10. "Seasonal timing is too unpredictable — we can't schedule shoots."
**Response:** "I work on a flexible scheduling model for landscape documentation. We identify the target window — peak bloom, fall color, winter structure — and I hold availability. When you see your design hit the moment, we move. I am 30 minutes from Squamish to Whistler projects and can be in Vancouver in an hour."

## Summary: Why This ICP Matters for Matt

1. **Lowest-friction revenue addition.** Landscape architects are already on Matt's projects. Cost-share adds $800-$1,500/project with near-zero marginal effort.
2. **Drone differentiation.** Matt's Transport Canada Advanced certification is a genuine competitive moat for this ICP. Landscape design is conceived in plan view — aerial photography is essential, not optional.
3. **Maturity return visits create recurring revenue.** No other ICP has a built-in reason to re-hire the same photographer 3-5 years later for the same project.
4. **Seasonal documentation creates multiple touchpoints.** One project can generate 2-4 shoots across a year, deepening the relationship.
5. **Underserved market.** Fewer photographers understand landscape architecture than building architecture. The expertise bar is lower to clear because the competition is thinner.
6. **Cross-referral potential.** Landscape architects recommend photographers to architects and builders they collaborate with. A landscape architect relationship opens doors to the building professionals on their projects.
7. **Sea-to-Sky gap.** There is no established landscape architecture photographer in the corridor. The market is open.
