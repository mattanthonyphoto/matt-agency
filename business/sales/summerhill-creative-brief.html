01

Wilson Residence, Oles Cove.

5383 Oles Cove Road  ·  Second Nature Designs  ·  Completed  ·  0.928 ha waterfront parcel

A finished house with the Wilsons on site. I haven't been on this site yet, so a lot of the planning gets finalised on our pre-shoot call — the story behind the design, the decisions I can't pull from the drawings, how the clients actually use the place.

Known
  • Finished waterfront build on private acreage. Rock bluff to the north, water to the west.
  • The Wilsons will be in the frames — lifestyle shots, not just architecture.
  • I haven't shot here yet — will do a site walk or scout the morning of if time allows.
  • Window suppliers (Innotech and Kolbe) are both interested in cost-sharing the shoot.
Pre-shoot
conversation

Twenty minutes, any time before the 14th. What I'd want to ask:

  1. The brief. What did the Wilsons originally ask for, and what was non-negotiable?
  2. Why here. Why is the house where it is on the lot? Wind, views, sun, existing trees?
  3. The one thing. If you walked a stranger through, where would you stop and say “look at this”?
  4. Materials. Siding, roof, millwork, stone — where did the budget go, where did you hold back?
  5. How they live here. Where do they drink their coffee? Where do they eat in summer? Where does the dog sleep?
  6. Landscaping. Mature or freshly planted? Anything blooming in mid-May?
  7. Where the images will go. Publication candidate or internal portfolio piece? That changes the edit.
Intended
frames
  • House in its setting. Drone at low altitude, low tide if it works. The house read against the water and the land.
  • The arrival. Driveway to front door. Two or three frames that build up to the entry.
  • The view shot. Living room out to the water — the view the house was designed around.
  • Portraits with the Wilsons. Three to five frames. Kitchen island, deck, dock, a quiet interior. Neutral wardrobe.
  • Material close-ups. Three to four tight shots on the finishes that cost real money.
  • Reels, 15–30s, two to three. An approach push-in, a human moment, a window reveal, an evening exterior. Vertical, licensed music.
Logistics
  • Wilsons on site for two to three hours. Wardrobe note: solids, no logos, no bright primaries.
  • I'll pull the tide chart before the shoot for the waterside frames.
  • Need to confirm drone airspace over the cove is clear.

02

Madeira Park Residence, Johnstone Road.

4976 Johnstone Road  ·  Keck Architecture + Design  ·  Completed, unfurnished  ·  Waterfront harbour

This is a designed house, not a spec build. The drawings are clean and restrained — Keck's signature. Worth photographing as architecture, not just as a listing.

What the
drawings show

Two gabled wings — east and west — connected by a lower middle section that holds a double-height staircase. A long run of skylights brings light down into the plan from above. The east wing has a covered upper deck on wood posts, with a glass guardrail, looking out over the water. The only exterior materials are vertical wood siding and a standing-seam metal roof. No decorative trim, nothing fussy.

From the road, the house reads almost like a simple barn. From the water, the glass-guarded deck and the open east-wing elevation give it a clearly modern feel.

On staging

No landscaping, no furniture — that's the reality of a listing shoot. An empty interior is going to read cold. There's nothing inside yet to soften it: no furniture, no art, no plants.

My take: shoot the house honest first. The one piece of live staging worth doing is the sailboat at the dock — it anchors the waterside exterior and makes the place feel lived-in.

Digital furniture for the interiors is a post-production job, not a shoot-day decision. Once we have the clean empty frames, you pick three to five we stage digitally for the listing. That way you end up with two edits off the same shoot: a clean architectural portfolio set, and a staged MLS set.

Intended
frames, exterior
  • Road-side gable. Straight on, symmetrical, neutral light. Pure geometry.
  • Water-side gable. From the shoreline or the water, drone at roof height.
  • The arrival. Driveway approach, showing the detail where the siding meets the concrete flush.
  • Under the deck. From below on the water side, looking up at the covered deck and the wood posts. Strong geometry.
  • Stepped concrete retaining walls. The way the site steps down the rock toward the water.
  • Design overview. Drone from the water, 30° angle. One frame that shows the two wings and the middle section all at once.
Intended
frames, interior
  • Staircase looking up. From the lower floor, looking up through the glass wall. Probably the best single frame in the house.
  • Staircase looking down. From the upper landing, showing the steps and the skylights overhead.
  • Kitchen / living / deck. Framed through the opening out to the deck and the water.
  • Skylight light. Mid-morning, watching for harsh strips of light on the floor.
  • Transitions. Where each wing meets the staircase volume — both east and west. The clean trim detail.
  • Wood ceiling. Where the clear-finish wood continues from the outside soffit to the inside ceiling.
Reels, 15–30s
Two to three
  • Driveway approach, panning across to reveal the gables.
  • Staircase with morning light coming through the glass wall — one slow moment.
  • A dolly shot across the covered deck out toward the water.
  • Drone pullback at the end, showing the house in its harbour setting.
  • Vertical cuts, licensed music. Calm, not cinematic.
Logistics
  • Tide chart for the waterside frames and the dock staging.
  • Confirm the sailboat is available and in the berth for the shoot day.
  • Check drone airspace around Madeira Park harbour.
  • Site needs a cleanup pass beforehand — construction dust, ladder marks, tape residue on the glass.
  • All vendor stickers off vents, windows, and appliances.
  • Confirm whether any window coverings are going in before we arrive.
