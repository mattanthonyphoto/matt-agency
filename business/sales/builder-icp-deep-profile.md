# Builder ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 26, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Craftsman-Entrepreneur Tension

Most custom home builder owners started in the trades — carpenters, framers, finish carpenters — and worked their way up. They didn't go to business school. They learned business by doing it, often painfully. The defining psychological feature: they're craftsmen trapped in entrepreneur bodies.

**Key traits:**
- **Hands-on control freaks.** Built their reputation on quality, struggle to delegate because no one does it as well as they did. Applies to marketing too.
- **Pride-driven, not ego-driven.** A finished home is a physical manifestation of their skill. When it's poorly photographed, it's a personal insult to their work.
- **Skeptical of "marketing people."** Burned by agencies, SEO vendors, and social media managers who don't understand construction.
- **Relationship-oriented.** Entire business model runs on trust, referrals, and long-term relationships.

### Demographics
- **Age:** 45-55 core concentration
- **Gender:** Overwhelmingly male, but spouses frequently play significant operational roles
- **Education:** Mixed — many came through trades. Higher revenue ($5M+) correlates with more formal education, but hands-on experience is what they respect.
- **Owner compensation ($1M-$10M range):** Typically $80K-$250K depending on margins and reinvestment. 46.8% intend to surpass $120K/year.

### Values
- Quality over volume (chose custom over production deliberately)
- Reputation is everything (referral-driven business)
- Autonomy (don't want a boss, resist being "managed" or sold to)
- Tangible results (abstract marketing metrics mean nothing)

### Self-Image vs. Market Perception
- **Self-perception:** Artisans. Problem-solvers. The unsung heroes — architects get design credit, designers get aesthetics credit, builders get blamed when things go wrong.
- **Market perception:** Varies. Homeowners see them as expensive. Architects see them as execution partners. Public lumps them with "contractors."

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week
- **6:30-7:00 AM:** On job sites before trades arrive
- **7:00-9:00 AM:** Meeting site supervisors, coordinating subs, handling deliveries
- **9:00 AM-Noon:** Client meetings, design reviews, walking prospects through builds
- **Noon-5:00 PM:** More sites, estimating, dealing with problems
- **5:00-8:00 PM:** "Second shift" — paperwork, invoicing, emails, plan reviews
- **Weekends:** Often working, especially in good weather

They don't have a marketing problem. They have a **time problem.**

### What Keeps Them Up at Night
1. **Cash flow.** 12-24+ month build cycles. Progress draws and holdback create constant pressure.
2. **Labor.** Finding/retaining skilled trades. BC workforce dropped 7% in 5 years.
3. **Finding the RIGHT clients.** Lead quality, not volume. People who can afford $1.5M+ and understand the process.
4. **Competing without cheapening.** Production builders dominate visibility. Custom builders can't communicate their value.
5. **Tariff/cost squeeze.** Lumber 45%, carbon tax up 37.5%, margins thinner than they look.

### What Frustrates Them About Vendors
- People who don't understand construction timelines and site conditions
- Over-promising and under-delivering
- Unnecessary meetings or complexity
- Photographers who don't know how to shoot a house

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Makes the Decision

**$1M-$3M (1-5 employees):** The owner decides, but the **spouse frequently influences or manages the decision.** Many are husband-wife operations where the spouse handles sales, marketing, and office operations. **If you're only selling to the builder, you may be selling to the wrong person.**

**$3M-$7M (5-15 employees):** Owner approves, but may delegate research to office manager or marketing coordinator.

**$7M-$10M (15-30 employees):** More likely to have a dedicated marketing person or agency. Owner still has final approval.

### What Triggers the Purchase
1. A signature project completing — "this one is too good not to photograph"
2. Awards deadline approaching (Georgies, CHBA, BALA require professional images)
3. A competitor wins an award
4. Website redesign — realize iPhone photos don't cut it
5. Embarrassment — show up to a pitch, client already saw terrible website
6. Architect/designer request for portfolio images

### How They Currently Handle Photography

| Method | Prevalence | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| iPhone from site super | Most common | Terrible |
| Nothing at all | Very common | N/A |
| Realtor photographer | Occasional | Generic |
| One-off local photographer | Rare | Inconsistent |
| Professional architectural photographer | Very rare | Excellent (usually only for awards) |

### How They Evaluate Photographers
1. **Portfolio first.** If your work doesn't look like what they build, they're done.
2. **Referrals from architects/designers** — the #1 sourcing method.
3. **Houzz profiles** — where homeowners find them, where builders check vendors.
4. **Understanding of architecture** — they want someone who "gets" construction.
5. **Lighting quality** — blown-out windows or dark interiors are immediate disqualifiers.
6. **Low friction** — show up, know what you're doing, deliver fast.

### What Makes Them Say Yes
- Portfolio matches their quality level and style
- Referral from someone they trust
- Clear, simple pricing (lump sum > complicated day rates)
- Cost-sharing facilitation
- Quick turnaround
- Demonstrated understanding of their specific work

### What Makes Them Say No
- Portfolio looks like real estate, not architecture
- Too much "marketing speak"
- Feeling sold to (they respond to peer-to-peer conversation)
- Complicated pricing or nickel-and-diming
- Requiring too much of their time

### Price Sensitivity — The Real Story

**Less sensitive than they appear.** Won't haggle over $500 if they believe in quality, but will refuse $2,000 if unconvinced it matters. The barrier isn't price — it's **perceived value.**

- ~60% see photography as an **expense** ("we need photos of the house")
- ~40% see it as an **investment** (strategic, recurring, ROI-oriented)
- Your sales job: move builders from camp one to camp two.

## Where They Hang Out

### Online (Actually Used)
- **Instagram** — primary visual platform. Browse competitors, get inspired, occasionally post.
- **Facebook** — local community engagement, builder groups. Many use as primary online presence.
- **Houzz** — critical. Where homeowners find and vet builders.
- **YouTube** — consume heavily (Matt Risinger, Build Show), rarely create.
- **LinkedIn** — peer networking, not client acquisition.

### What They Read/Listen To
- **Fine Homebuilding** — most widely read construction magazine
- **JLC (Journal of Light Construction)** — technically oriented, more business content
- **Pro Builder, Custom Builder** — B2B, industry news
- **Podcasts:** The Build Show (Matt Risinger), The Curious Builder (Mark Williams), NS Builders (Nick Schiffer)

### Where They Network
- Local HBA/CHBA meetings
- **Sea to Sky CHBA** — your chapter
- NAHB International Builders' Show (IBS) — 60K+ visitors
- Parade of Homes events
- **Georgie Awards Gala (May 23, 2026)**

### How They Get Referrals (in order)
1. Past clients (3-5x higher conversion rate)
2. Architects and designers (most powerful professional referral)
3. Real estate agents
4. Other trades and suppliers
5. Houzz and Google reviews

## The Language That Works

### Say This
- "Document" not "shoot" or "photograph"
- "Your craft" not "your product"
- "Portfolio" not "content"
- "Creative partner" not "vendor"
- Reference specific details: the timber frame, the exposed CLT, how light enters a space

### Never Say
- "Marketing" or "content creation"
- "ROI" or "lead generation"
- "Social media strategy"
- Anything that sounds like selling them something they didn't know they needed

### Killer Lines
1. "Every project you don't photograph is a project that can't work for you."
2. "The builders winning Georgies aren't building better homes than you. They're photographing them better."
3. "You're spending $2M on the build. The photography is 0.1% of project cost to capture 100% of the value."
4. "Your next client is looking at your website right now."

## The Emotional Journey of Hiring a Photographer

### Hesitations
- "Is this really worth it? I can take decent photos with my phone."
- "The last photographer didn't understand our homes."
- "I don't have time to coordinate this."
- "The homeowner already moved in — we missed the window."

### What Creates Loyal Repeat Clients
- **Consistency.** Every shoot delivers the same high standard.
- **Low friction.** Hand you the address and key, photos appear in inbox.
- **Understanding of their style.** Know what makes their work special without being told.
- **Speed.** Quick turnaround, especially for awards deadlines.
- **Making them look good.** When a client sees photos and is blown away, the builder gets credit.

## BC / Pacific Northwest Specifics

### Sea-to-Sky Corridor
- Squamish detached median: $1.86M; Whistler: $2.3M-$6M+
- Mix of primary residences (Squamish) and recreational (Whistler)
- Small, tight community — 3-5 strong relationships could fill local pipeline

### BC-Specific
- **BC Energy Step Code** adds $40K+ per home. Builders who master it take pride in it.
- Seismic and environmental requirements add complexity
- More stringent building regulations than most US jurisdictions
- West Coast modern dominates: glass, wood, steel, flat/shed roofs, indoor-outdoor living

### How PNW Builders Differ
- Sustainability focus is regulated, not optional — builders are more technically sophisticated
- Landscape integration defines the architecture (and the photography)
- Shorter building seasons compress timelines
- Floor-to-ceiling windows showcasing mountains/coast = baseline expectation

## Market Context (March 2026)

- NAHB Housing Market Index at 38/50 (below threshold 21 straight months)
- BC housing starts falling 42,200 → 34,500
- BoC rate 2.25%, stable but not sub-2% boom era
- Tariffs: lumber 45%, steel/aluminum 50%, copper +36%
- Market consolidating around quality — fewer projects but more custom/complex
- Primary concern shifted from "can't find workers" to "can't find projects"
- Builder marketing budgets: 1.5-3% of revenue typically ($15K-$300K for $1M-$10M companies)
- Photography budget: typically $3K-$15K/year covering 2-5 shoots
