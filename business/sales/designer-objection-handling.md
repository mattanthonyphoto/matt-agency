# Interior Designer Objection Handling Scripts

*Speak editorial, lifestyle, Instagram. Collaborative, not directive.*

**Philosophy:** Designers are the most visually sophisticated ICP. They understand composition intuitively. Respect their styling expertise. Lead with Instagram content value and publication access. Be generous with usage rights.

---

## 1. "We style everything ourselves — we just need someone to click the shutter"

*Protective of their creative domain. Hidden price objection.*

"I completely respect that — your styling is what makes the space. My job isn't to restyle your work. It's to translate what you've created into images that do it justice. Which angle catches the texture of that fabric, how light interacts with your material palette, when a vignette needs a micro-adjustment to photograph well. The best shoots are true collaborations: you bring the spatial vision, I bring the compositional eye. That's the difference between Instagram photos that get liked and portfolio images that land your next dream project."

---

## 2. "We can't afford professional photography on every project"

*Real budget constraint. Thinking per-project, not strategically.*

"You shouldn't photograph every project — but be strategic about which ones you do. Identify 3-4 per year that represent the work you want more of. A single well-photographed project fuels your Instagram, website, awards, and press for months. And most of my shoots involve cost-sharing with the builder or architect — which brings your investment down 30%+. Instead of $3,000, you're looking at $1,800-$2,000. Over a year, that's $6K-$8K in photography that becomes the engine for all your marketing."

---

## 3. "Our iPhone photos do well enough on Instagram"

*Some truth — but they don't know what they're missing.*

"iPhone is great for day-to-day — Stories, BTS, process shots. Don't stop doing that. But there's a tier above that opens doors iPhone can't. Western Living won't consider iPhone images. Award submissions require specific quality standards. And when a potential client with a $500K renovation is comparing your portfolio to another designer's, professionally photographed work signals a level they respond to. The sweet spot is both: iPhone for everyday content, professional for your 3-4 signature projects that become your portfolio foundation and press submissions."

---

## 4. "We already have a photographer we love"

*Genuine loyalty. Don't try to displace.*

"That's great — a strong photographer relationship is one of the best investments. I'd love to be a resource if you ever need a second option — maybe they're booked on your date, or you have a project in the Sea-to-Sky outside their area. I also specialize in cost-share coordination, so if you're on a project where the builder is already hiring me, I can make sure your interiors are covered at a fraction of the cost. No pressure — just good to know each other."

---

## 5. "The architect/builder is handling the photography"

*Deferring to avoid cost. May not realize architect's photographer won't prioritize interiors.*

"That can work — but architectural photographers tend to prioritize the building: wide angles, exterior shots, structural features. Your design details — the custom millwork, the material transitions, the way you styled that console — can get treated as background. It's worth asking for the shot list to make sure your interiors are represented. If there's a gap, I can come in for a focused 2-hour session just on interiors and details after the main shoot. Your cost is minimal, and you walk away with 8-10 images specifically about your design work."

---

## 6. "We need the images RIGHT NOW for our Instagram"

*Urgency is real. The reveal window is everything.*

"Here's how I handle that: same-day or next-day, I send you 3-5 lightly edited preview images. They're more than good enough for Instagram. You get your reveal content immediately, and the full edited set follows in 2 weeks for portfolio and submissions. Most of my designer clients post the day after the shoot. We can even plan the shot list so the first 5 images are your highest-priority Instagram content."

---

## 7. "Can you do it for less? It's just interiors, not a full architectural shoot"

*Misunderstanding. Interiors are actually more involved.*

"Interiors are actually more involved than exteriors, not less. Every room is a new lighting scenario. Every vignette requires precise composition. Detail shots need macro-level attention. A thorough shoot involves 15-20 individual setups in a half day. What I can do is scope to match your budget — focus on the 3-4 most important spaces, or do a half-day instead of full. Quality stays the same, we just narrow the scope."

---

## 8. "We only need a few detail shots, not a whole shoot"

*Minimizing cost. Actually a great gateway opportunity.*

"I love focused detail sessions — that's where texture, composition, and the craft of your selections really shine. Book a 2-hour session. We pick the 5-8 details that matter most — hardware, a material transition, a styled moment. I deliver 8-10 polished images cropped for both portfolio and social. Those detail images often outperform room-wide shots on Instagram because they're more intimate. We can build from there."

---

## 9. "The homeowner is difficult about photography"

*Real challenge. Looking for someone who can navigate diplomatically.*

"This comes up a lot. I provide a client-facing overview explaining the process — something you can share that makes it professional and low-key. I'm fast on site — in and out in 3-4 hours. And I always offer the homeowner personal images at no charge. That usually turns resistance into enthusiasm. If privacy is the concern, we agree in advance on which rooms and avoid anything identifying."

---

## 10. "We've had photographers who don't understand how to style a space"

*They've been burned. This is actually the best objection — they're telling you what they want.*

"That's one of the most common frustrations I hear. It's why I focus on interiors and architecture rather than being a generalist. Every object in a space is there for a reason. I don't move anything without asking. If I see something that photographs differently than it looks in person — a reflection, a shadow, an angle that flattens a texture — I flag it and we discuss. Before the shoot, we walk the space together and you tell me what matters most. During, you're as involved as you want. After, you see every image before anything goes public."
