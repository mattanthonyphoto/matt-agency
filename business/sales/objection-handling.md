# Objection Handling

Common pushback and how to respond. Written in Matt's voice — consultative, not pushy.

**Philosophy:** Never argue. Ask questions first. Speak craft language, not marketing language. Always offer a low-risk entry point. Use cost-share to reduce perceived cost. Create urgency through the photography window, not sales pressure.

---

### "We just use our phones / iPhone photos are good enough."

*What they're really saying: I don't see enough difference to justify paying for it.*

For progress shots and social stories, absolutely — phone photos are great for that. Keep using them.

But here's the thing: your homes are your portfolio. Every potential client looks at your website and Instagram before they ever call. They're comparing your images to every other builder in the market. The builders winning the best projects — their photography looks like it belongs in Western Living. It's not about the camera. It's about understanding how to capture space, light, and the details you spent months getting right.

Your craftsmanship deserves to be documented at the same level it was built.

---

### "We can't afford professional photography right now."

*What they're really saying: I see it as an expense, not an investment. Cash flow is tight.*

I get it — cash flow in construction is brutal. You're floating materials, paying subs, waiting on draws.

But what's your next project worth? $1.5M? A $3,500 shoot is 0.23% of project value and generates images you'll use for 3-5 years across your website, proposals, social media, and award submissions. It's the cheapest marketing per impression you'll ever do.

And with cost-sharing — where the designer and architect split the fee — your out-of-pocket drops to $2,000-$2,500. That happens on about 90% of my projects.

If budget is genuinely tight, start with one project. No retainer, no commitment. Just let me show you what your work looks like when it's captured properly.

---

### "We only need photos for awards season."

*What they're really saying: I see photography as a once-a-year event.*

Awards are a great reason to shoot — and the CHBA is explicit: photographs are literally the only way judges see your project. Bad photos kill great work.

But here's the thing: awards season is one week. Your website is 52 weeks. Your Instagram is 365 days. Your proposals go out every time you pitch.

The builders growing fastest treat every completed project as both an award submission AND a marketing campaign. Same shoot, same images, 10x the use. If you're only shooting for awards, you're leaving 90% of the value on the table.

---

### "We already have a photographer."

*What they're really saying: We're loyal and switching feels risky.*

That's great — having someone you trust matters. I'm not here to replace anybody.

But are they shooting your construction progress too, or just the finished product? Delivering images formatted for social media, or just high-res files? Prepping your award submissions?

A lot of builders I work with keep their existing photographer for certain projects and bring me in for the signature builds — the award contenders, the projects that really define your brand. It doesn't have to be all-or-nothing.

If you're ever curious what a different approach looks like, happy to do a single project. No pressure.

---

### "We don't really do marketing."

*What they're really saying: We get referrals and marketing feels inauthentic.*

That's actually the best position to be in — your work is strong enough that people refer you without being asked.

But even your referrals are Googling you before they call. They're looking at your website, your Instagram, maybe your Google reviews. The referral gets them to your door, but your visual presence confirms their decision.

I don't think of what I do as marketing. I think of it as documentation. You spend 12-18 months building something extraordinary. I make sure there's a record of it that matches the quality of what you built. What you do with those images is up to you.

---

### "Can you just do it cheaper?"

*What they're really saying: I'm comparing you to the $500 real estate photographer.*

I appreciate you being direct. I can absolutely make it cheaper — fewer angles, skip the twilight exterior, less time in post. But I won't, because it would produce images that don't do your work justice.

You wouldn't put cheap trim in a custom home. Same principle.

What I can do is make the investment work harder. On most projects, the interior designer or architect wants the same images. I handle the licensing, and cost-share typically covers 30% of the shoot fee. So your $3,500 drops to around $2,450 out of pocket.

---

### "We'll think about it" / "Maybe next project."

*What they're really saying: I'm not convinced enough to act now. No urgency.*

Totally fair. No rush.

The one thing I'd flag is timing. Once the homeowner moves in, we've got a narrow window — usually 2-3 weeks — before furniture changes, personal items accumulate, and landscaping shifts. I've had builders call 6 months later and the house doesn't photograph the same.

If you've got a project completing in the next couple months, it's worth locking in a date now even if we sort out details later. No deposit until you're ready.

What's your next completion looking like?

---

### "Our projects aren't done yet / bad timing."

*What they're really saying: Interested but nothing to shoot. I'll forget about you.*

That's actually perfect timing. If you've got a project in framing or rough-in, that's the ideal time to start construction progress photography. Monthly visits, images within 48 hours for client updates and social posts. By completion, you've got 8-12 months of content showing the craftsmanship that goes into a build.

Clients love seeing the process — it's the most engaging content on social. And when we do the final shoot, I already know the project inside out.

What stage is your current build at? I can come by for 30 minutes, walk the site with you, and show you exactly what I'd capture.

---

### "We tried professional photography before and it wasn't worth it."

*What they're really saying: I got burned. Spent money and didn't see results.*

That's frustrating, and I hear it more than you'd think. Can I ask what specifically didn't work — the images themselves, or was it more that they didn't end up getting used?

If the images weren't right — wrong angles, didn't capture the details that make your work special, felt generic — that's a photographer who doesn't understand residential construction. I only shoot architecture and interiors. I know what a builder cares about: the joinery, the material transitions, how natural light hits custom millwork.

If the images sat on a hard drive unused — that's a delivery problem. I deliver formatted for your website, social media, proposals, and award submissions. Not just a Dropbox link with 200 unorganized files.

The fact that you invested before tells me you understand the value. The execution just didn't match.

---

### "The homeowner doesn't want people in their house."

*What they're really saying: I don't want to risk the client relationship.*

That's the right instinct — homeowner relationship always comes first.

A few things that usually help: Most homeowners are actually proud and excited to see their home photographed professionally. Frame it as "our photographer would love to capture your home for our portfolio" — most feel flattered.

We shoot in the 2-3 week window after completion, before full move-in. No personal items, no privacy concerns. The homeowner doesn't need to be there.

The easiest long-term fix: include a photography clause in your build contract. Simple permission language. Most builders I work with have this as standard. Removes the awkward ask entirely.

---

### "We need the images tomorrow / this week."

Rush delivery (48-hour turnaround) is available as an add-on. For the shoot itself, I can sometimes accommodate short notice depending on the season. Let's look at the calendar.

Retainer clients get priority scheduling — guaranteed availability when a project completes and the light is perfect.

---

### "Your style doesn't match what we're looking for."

My editing is intentionally natural and true-to-material — I want your work to look like your work, not a filtered version. That said, every project gets edited to suit its own palette and mood. If you have references, happy to review and see if we're aligned.

---

### "Can you just do a quick shoot?"

I offer half-day rates for smaller scopes. There's a minimum investment to do the work properly — showing up, setting up, and capturing a space with intention takes time regardless of size. Happy to scope it and find the right fit.

---

### "We're not on social media."

The images go far beyond social. They're the backbone of your website, proposals, award submissions, and any marketing material. Most clients use the images for years across every channel. Social is just one piece — and often the least important one.
