# Windows & Doors ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 30, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Engineer-Merchant Tension

Window and door company owners live in a unique psychological space: they're technical people running relationship businesses. Unlike builders (craftsmen who became entrepreneurs) or architects (designers who became business operators), window/door owners straddle manufacturing precision and dealer-style salesmanship. They speak in U-values and thermal breaks, but their revenue depends on builder loyalty, showroom walk-ins, and architect specifications.

The defining tension: **they sell an invisible product.** When a window is installed perfectly, nobody notices it — they notice the view, the light, the comfort. When it fails, it's catastrophic: condensation, drafts, leaks, warranty calls. Their entire professional identity revolves around something that's meant to disappear into the architecture.

**Key traits:**
- **Technical pride.** They know thermal performance numbers cold. U-values, R-values, air infiltration ratings, Solar Heat Gain Coefficients — this is their language of expertise and credibility.
- **Relationship-dependent.** Unlike commodity manufacturers, custom/boutique window companies live or die by their builder and architect relationships. A single architect who specs your product on every project is worth $200K+/year.
- **Caught between commodity and craft.** Big box vinyl windows are $300. Their custom aluminum-clad triple-pane units are $3,000+. They must constantly justify the premium.
- **Quietly competitive.** They watch what other window companies are doing — whose products show up on magazine-worthy homes, who's getting specified by the best architects.

### Demographics
- **Age:** 40-60 core concentration. Many second-generation family businesses.
- **Gender:** Predominantly male ownership, though sales/showroom management often includes women in key roles.
- **Education:** Mixed. Some engineering backgrounds (especially in manufacturing), many came through construction/trades and moved into distribution. European-origin brands often have owners with formal engineering or business education.
- **Company size (BC market):** Ranges from 5-person boutique dealers (The Window Merchant, ~3-5 staff) to mid-size manufacturers (Westeck, 200+ employees; Centra, employee-owned with multiple facilities). The sweet spot for Matt: boutique dealers and regional manufacturers with 5-50 employees.
- **Owner compensation:** Highly variable. Boutique dealers: $80K-$150K. Regional manufacturer owners: $150K-$400K+. Margin pressure from tariffs and material costs currently compressing take-home.

### Values
- Technical excellence over sales volume (at the custom/boutique level)
- Long-term builder/architect relationships over transactional sales
- Product performance as reputation — a failed window is a failed relationship
- Precision and specification accuracy
- Industry credibility through association involvement (FenBC, Fenestration Canada)

### Self-Image vs. Market Perception
- **Self-perception:** Experts. Problem-solvers. The people who make the architect's vision actually work thermally, structurally, and aesthetically. Trusted advisors to builders navigating code requirements.
- **Market perception:** Homeowners see them as "the window guys" — a commodity purchase. Builders see them as critical trade partners (when custom) or interchangeable suppliers (when commodity). Architects see them as either specification partners or obstacles to design intent, depending on capability.

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week (Boutique Dealer / Regional Manufacturer Owner)
- **Monday:** Reviewing quotes and project timelines. Following up on outstanding proposals. Checking production schedules or supplier delivery dates.
- **Tuesday-Wednesday:** Builder site visits, showroom appointments with homeowners, architect meetings to review specifications. Driving between projects across the Lower Mainland, Sea-to-Sky, or island.
- **Thursday:** Dealing with installation issues — coordinating installers, handling warranty claims, troubleshooting condensation or air leakage complaints. Managing inventory and incoming shipments.
- **Friday:** Administrative catch-up — invoicing, payroll, supplier negotiations. Reviewing upcoming project pipeline.
- **Weekends:** Often handling urgent calls from builders mid-install who need answers about rough openings, flashing details, or delivery changes.

They're pulled in every direction: sales, technical consultation, project management, logistics, and customer service — often simultaneously.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night

1. **Tariffs and material costs.** The US doubled Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum to 50% in June 2025. Raw material expenses increased an average of 6% year-over-year. 76.2% of manufacturers cite trade uncertainties as their foremost challenge (Q1 2025 NAM survey). Aluminum extrusions, steel skins, and hardware are core inputs — tariff changes flow through to BOMs within weeks.

2. **The specification game.** Getting architects to specify their products instead of competitors'. Once a competitor's product is written into a spec, switching costs are enormous. Losing a key architect relationship can mean losing hundreds of thousands in downstream revenue.

3. **Energy code escalation.** BC's Energy Step Code is ratcheting up requirements. Step 3 mandatory since January 2025 for Part 9 residential. Step 5 (effectively Passive House level) coming by 2032. Products must hit U-factor 1.2 now, dropping to 0.8 by 2030 — requiring triple glazing and advanced low-e coatings. Companies that can't offer high-performance products will be regulated out of the custom market.

4. **Labor — installation and manufacturing.** 92% of construction firms report difficulty finding qualified workers. Window and door installers are specifically called out as one of the hardest positions to fill. Employee retention is harder than recruitment. The Manufacturing Institute projects a 1.9 million worker shortage by 2033.

5. **Housing starts uncertainty.** BC urban starts dropped from 42,200 to projected 34,500. CMHC projects further weakness in 2026-2027. Custom home starts more resilient than multifamily, but pipeline is thinning.

6. **Competition from big manufacturers.** The top 50 window companies account for ~80% of industry revenue. Regional players compete by offering custom solutions, local service, and builder relationships that nationals can't match — but the margin pressure is relentless.

7. **European competition entering the market.** Schuco, Internorm, Rehau, and other European brands are aggressively entering the Canadian market with Passive House-certified products. They offer U-values as low as 0.62 W/(m2K) — performance levels most North American manufacturers can't match yet. This creates both opportunity (for dealers who carry them) and existential threat (for manufacturers who don't).

### What Frustrates Them About Vendors
- People who don't understand lead times and manufacturing/procurement complexity
- Marketing agencies that produce generic content with no understanding of fenestration
- Photographers who can't handle reflections, glare, and the technical challenges of shooting glass
- Anyone who wastes their time or doesn't follow through

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Makes the Decision

**Boutique dealers (5-15 people):** The owner decides. Period. Often the same person doing sales, specs, and project management. No marketing department. Marketing decisions happen between other tasks or not at all.

**Regional manufacturers (15-50 people):** Owner or VP of Sales approves, but a marketing coordinator (where one exists) may research options and manage logistics. Larger companies (50+) may have a marketing manager with budget authority for shoots under $5K.

**Key insight:** Unlike builders (where spouses often influence) or architects (where principals decide on aesthetic grounds), window/door company owners treat photography as a **business decision, not a creative one.** They want to know: will this help me close more builder accounts? Will this get my product specified by architects?

### What Triggers the Purchase

1. **New product line launch.** Introducing a European brand, a new triple-pane system, or a large-format sliding door demands professional imagery to sell through to builders and architects.
2. **Showroom renovation or opening.** Physical showrooms remain critical — builders and homeowners need to see, touch, and operate products. New showroom = new photography.
3. **Signature project completion.** A high-profile custom home featuring their windows/doors prominently — especially one designed by a notable architect. This is the #1 organic trigger.
4. **Builder/architect marketing partnership.** A builder photographs a project and the window company wants to participate in the cost-share and receive images featuring their products.
5. **Website overhaul.** Realizing their website has manufacturer stock photos that look like every other dealer's site. Their projects look generic because they're using someone else's imagery.
6. **Award submissions.** Fenestration Canada awards, Georgie Awards (as a trade partner), industry publications wanting project imagery.
7. **Losing a spec to a competitor** whose marketing materials looked more professional. This is the emotional trigger — the one they won't admit to.

### How They Currently Handle Photography

| Method | Prevalence | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturer-supplied stock photos | Most common | Generic, doesn't show their projects |
| iPhone photos from installers | Very common | Terrible — glare, reflections, no composition |
| Builder shares their project photos | Occasional | Good quality but window company is afterthought |
| Hire a general photographer | Rare | Often can't handle glass/reflections |
| Professional architectural photographer | Very rare | Excellent (The Window Merchant is an exception) |

### How They Evaluate Photographers
1. **Can they photograph glass?** The #1 technical concern. Reflections, glare, and blown-out highlights through windows are immediate disqualifiers.
2. **Do they understand how to showcase the product within the architecture?** They need images that show sightlines, frame profiles, hardware, and how light enters the space — not just pretty room photos.
3. **Builder/architect referral.** If a trusted builder says "use this photographer," they will.
4. **Portfolio showing windows as a feature, not just background.** Most architectural photography treats windows as neutral elements. They want someone who sees the window.
5. **Practical and efficient.** They can't dedicate days to coordinating shoots.
6. **Clear pricing.** They operate on project-based budgets, not creative retainers (at first).

### What Makes Them Say Yes
- Portfolio that clearly shows windows/doors as hero elements, not just background
- Demonstrated ability to handle reflections and showcase light quality
- Existing relationships with builders they work with (cost-share angle)
- Clear ROI framing: "these images help you get specified"
- Quick, professional process that respects their time
- Images they can share with builders and architects as a sales tool

### What Makes Them Say No
- Portfolio looks like real estate photography (windows are just holes in walls)
- No understanding of technical product features
- Feeling like this is a vanity expense rather than a business tool
- Complicated scope or unclear deliverables
- "We already get photos from the builder" (even though the builder's photos don't feature the product)

### Price Sensitivity — The Real Story

**More sensitive than builders, less than you'd think.** Their margins are already under pressure from tariffs (6% material cost increase YoY) and competition. 73% of companies raised prices in 2025, averaging 3.6% increases — they're feeling the squeeze.

But: a single architect relationship is worth $200K+/year. A set of images that helps them win that specification is worth 50x the cost of the photography.

- **Budget range:** $1,500-$5,000 per project shoot
- **Showroom photography:** $2,500-$7,500 (one-time, major investment)
- **Annual photography budget:** $3K-$12K for a boutique dealer; $15K-$50K for a regional manufacturer
- **Cost-share opportunity:** High. They're often already on projects with builders Matt shoots — adding the window company to the cost-share is natural.
- **Marketing budget context:** Window/door companies typically spend 2-5% of revenue on marketing. For a $2M boutique dealer, that's $40K-$100K — photography should be 10-20% of that.

## The Language That Works

### Technical Language They Respect
- **U-value / U-factor** — the fundamental measure of thermal performance. Lower = better. They know their numbers.
- **Sightlines** — the visible frame width when looking at a window. Minimal sightlines = more glass, more view. A major selling point for slim-profile aluminum and steel systems.
- **Thermal break** — the insulating barrier between interior and exterior frame surfaces. A technically sophisticated feature they take pride in explaining.
- **Low-e coatings** — low-emissivity glass coatings that control heat transfer and UV. They'll specify coating types by number (Low-E 180, Low-E 272).
- **Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC)** — how much solar radiation passes through. Critical for passive solar design.
- **Triple glazing / triple pane** — the premium performance standard, becoming mandatory under Step Code.
- **Tilt and turn** — European operating mechanism. A point of pride for companies carrying European products.
- **Air infiltration** — measured in L/(s·m2). Tight numbers = quality product.
- **Passive House certified** — the gold standard for energy performance. Products certified by PHI (Passive House Institute).
- **Curtain wall / window wall** — commercial glazing systems. Different from residential but some companies span both.

### Say This
- "Document how light enters the space through your product" not "take photos of windows"
- "Showcase the sightlines" — they'll know exactly what you mean
- "Capture the thermal performance story" — sounds unusual but resonates: showing comfort, condensation-free glass, views unobstructed by heavy frames
- "Your product deserves to be the hero, not the background"
- "Help architects see your product in context" — speaks to their specification anxiety
- "Project documentation" not "marketing content"

### Never Say
- "Content creation" or "social media strategy" — too marketing-speak
- "Just windows" or "just doors" — minimizing their product is an insult
- "Stock photos would work fine" — triggers their frustration with generic imagery
- "ROI" or "lead generation" — too sales-y for an industry built on technical relationships
- Anything suggesting their product is a commodity

### Killer Lines
1. "Architects specify what they can visualize. If your product isn't professionally documented in real projects, you're invisible in the spec process."
2. "Every builder project you're on is a portfolio piece you're not capturing."
3. "The difference between a $300 vinyl window and your $3,000 system disappears in a blurry iPhone photo."
4. "Manufacturer stock photos make every dealer look the same. Project photos make you the one architects remember."
5. "Your window is designed to be invisible — to disappear into the view. Photographing that well is the hardest thing in architectural photography. That's exactly what I do."

## Photography-Specific Angles

### Why Window/Door Photography Is Technically Unique

This is one of the most challenging subjects in architectural photography:

1. **Light transmission and reflection.** Glass simultaneously transmits, reflects, and refracts light. A photograph must show what's through the window (the view), what the window does to light entering the space, and the frame/hardware — all in one image. This requires advanced exposure blending, time-of-day planning, and often polarizing filters.

2. **Sightline documentation.** The frame profile is a key selling point. Ultra-slim aluminum or steel frames with minimal sightlines need to be photographed to show how little frame intrudes on the view. This requires precise angles and careful composition.

3. **Installation context.** A window in a factory looks nothing like a window in a home. The value proposition only becomes real when the product is installed — when you can see the view it frames, the light it transmits, the space it creates. Installation photography requires coordination with builders during the narrow window between substantial completion and homeowner move-in.

4. **Before/after transformation.** For renovation window companies, showing the dramatic difference between old and new windows is the most powerful sales tool. This requires planning a pre-installation shoot, which most companies never think to do.

5. **Showroom photography.** Product vignettes, operating demonstrations (tilt-and-turn in action), hardware details, cross-sections showing triple glazing and thermal breaks. This is part product photography, part architectural photography.

6. **Light quality as product evidence.** A well-photographed room with their windows should make the viewer feel the quality of light — even, warm, controlled. This is the most powerful way to sell thermal performance without a single number.

### Why Manufacturer Stock Photos Aren't Enough

- **Generic context.** Stock photos show the product in someone else's project, often in a different climate, style, and context than BC custom homes.
- **Every dealer looks the same.** If you and your competitor both use Internorm's stock gallery, there's zero differentiation.
- **No local proof.** Architects and builders want to see the product installed in BC — in West Coast modern homes, with mountain/ocean views, in local conditions.
- **No relationship building.** When you photograph a builder's project and share images featuring their windows, you create a co-marketing asset that strengthens the builder relationship.
- **Missing the story.** Stock photos show a product. Project photos tell the story of how that product transformed a space — which is what actually drives specification decisions.

### The Cost-Share Angle (Critical)

Window/door companies are natural cost-share partners on builder projects:
- They're already on the project (supplied the product)
- They benefit from professional images featuring their product in context
- Adding them to a cost-share reduces the builder's cost while adding a revenue stream
- Matt's 90% cost-share hit rate means this is already a proven model
- The window company gets images they'd never commission on their own

**Pitch:** "I'm already photographing [Builder Name]'s project where your windows are installed. For [cost-share amount], you get full-resolution images of your product in a completed custom home. The builder's already on board."

## Objection Handling

### "We get our images from the manufacturer"

**The psychology:** They're not being cheap — they genuinely don't realize how much those stock photos are costing them in lost specifications. Every dealer using the same manufacturer gallery is invisible.

**Response:** "Those images show the product. What architects and builders need to see is the product in *your* projects — BC custom homes, local conditions, the kind of work your clients actually build. That's what gets you specified. I can show you three dealers using the exact same gallery right now."

### "Our builders share their photos with us"

**The psychology:** This feels like a free solution. Builder shares images, everyone's happy.

**Response:** "That's a great start, and it tells me your builders value the relationship. But in those photos, your window is background — it's part of someone else's story. When I shoot with your product as the hero, architects can see sightlines, frame profiles, how light enters the space. That's what drives specifications. I actually work with several builders you supply — we can structure this as a cost-share so everyone benefits."

### "We don't have the budget for photography right now"

**The psychology:** Margins are tight (6% material cost increase, tariff pressure). Photography feels like a luxury.

**Response:** "I hear that — tariffs have hit everyone's margins. Here's what I see: you're already on builder projects I'm photographing. For a fraction of what a standalone shoot costs, you can join the cost-share and get professional images of your product installed in a $2M+ custom home. That's a $[X] investment for images that help you win your next architect specification — which is worth what, $50K? $200K in downstream revenue?"

### "We tried a photographer before and the reflections were terrible"

**The psychology:** They've been burned. Glass photography is genuinely hard. Most photographers can't do it well.

**Response:** "That's the most common problem, and it's exactly why I specialize in this. Photographing glass is the hardest technical challenge in architectural photography — you need to control reflections, balance interior and exterior exposure, and capture how light actually moves through the product. I can show you my work for [The Window Merchant / specific project] — the sightlines are clean, the views are sharp, the light tells the performance story."

### "We only need a few product shots for the website"

**The psychology:** Thinking small. Seeing photography as a website checkbox, not a business tool.

**Response:** "A few product shots will update the website, absolutely. But here's what I've seen work: when window companies have professional project photography — their product in real BC custom homes — they use those images in architect presentations, builder pitch decks, trade show displays, social media, award submissions, and showroom displays. One shoot creates assets that work across every channel for years."

### "Can't you just Photoshop out the reflections?"

**The psychology:** Genuinely doesn't understand the photography process.

**Response:** "You can reduce some reflections in post, but it never looks natural. The real solution is shooting at the right time of day, with the right technique, so the glass looks the way it actually performs — clear views, beautiful light, minimal distortion. It's the same principle as your product: the engineering happens before installation, not after."

### "Our market is builders, not consumers — we don't need pretty pictures"

**The psychology:** Thinks professional photography is consumer-facing vanity.

**Response:** "Builders are actually more visually sophisticated than consumers — they can see quality instantly. And the real audience isn't just builders, it's the architects who specify your product for those builders. When an architect is choosing between your triple-pane tilt-and-turn system and a competitor's, they're comparing how each product looks in completed projects. Professional imagery is how you win that specification."

## BC / Pacific Northwest Market Context

### BC-Specific Dynamics

**Energy Step Code Driving Premiumization:**
- Step 3 mandatory since January 2025 for new Part 9 residential
- Products must meet U-factor 1.2 now; dropping to 0.8 by 2030
- At Step 4: 50% of Lower Mainland new construction requires triple glazing
- At Step 5 (mandatory by 2032): 80%+ triple-glazed share
- Sliding windows cannot receive triple glazing — casement windows becoming standard for high-performance homes
- **This is a massive tailwind for premium window companies.** Commodity vinyl can't meet these requirements. Companies offering triple-pane, Passive House-certified products are positioned to own the custom market.

**Tariff Pressure:**
- US Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum doubled to 50% in June 2025
- Canada imposed 25% tariffs on steel derivative products (including doors and windows) effective December 26, 2025
- CUSMA exemption protecting some cross-border products is "absolutely at risk" in 2026 review
- Manufacturers adapting: locking input pricing, diversifying sourcing, accelerating vinyl/fiberglass capacity, raising prices (73% raised prices in 2025, averaging 3.6%)
- Net effect: higher costs across the board, with custom/premium products absorbing more shock than commodity

**European Competition:**
- Schuco, Internorm, Rehau aggressively entering Canadian market
- Internorm (largest European passive-house-certified manufacturer) has Canadian dealers through InterHaus
- U-values as low as 0.62 W/(m2K) — significantly outperforming most North American products
- European tilt-and-turn operating mechanisms gaining market share
- **Opportunity for dealers carrying European products** who need to differentiate from commodity vinyl dealers with local, professional imagery

**Housing Market:**
- BC residential sales forecast to rise 12% in 2026 to 78,690 units
- Average price rising ~3% to $982,800
- New construction starts declining (CMHC projects historically weak levels 2026-2027)
- Custom home building: $400-$550/sq ft provincial average; $800+/sq ft in Vancouver/West Vancouver
- Custom market more resilient than multifamily — fewer projects but higher specification standards

### Key BC Window/Door Companies (Prospect Universe)

**Boutique Dealers (Primary targets — most need for differentiation):**
- **The Window Merchant** — Vancouver. Boutique dealer of European aluminum + Canadian fiberglass. Services Lower Mainland, Whistler, Sunshine Coast, Vancouver Island. 200+ custom projects. *Matt already has this as a case study — proof of concept.*
- **InterHaus** — Internorm dealer. High-performance European tilt-and-turn systems. Passive House certified products.
- **Apogee Passive House Products** — specializing in Passive House-suitable windows.
- **West Coast Windows** — premium windows and doors, BC-focused.

**Regional Manufacturers (Larger budgets, marketing departments):**
- **Westeck Windows & Doors** — 200+ employees, Chilliwack HQ, 3 production facilities, 20 sales consultants across BC. Custom residential and commercial.
- **Centra Windows** — 100% employee-owned, 40+ years, BC manufacturer. Strong content marketing presence already.
- **Euro-Line Windows & Doors** — Delta location, showroom-focused.

**National/International with BC Presence (Larger, harder to reach but bigger budgets):**

### Sea-to-Sky Corridor Specifics

- Mountain views and outdoor living define the architecture — floor-to-ceiling glazing is baseline

- Most window companies servicing this corridor operate from Vancouver/Lower Mainland

## The Emotional Journey of Hiring a Photographer

### Hesitations
- "We've never hired our own photographer — the builder handles that."
- "Our products look the same as everyone else's in photos — what's the point?"
- "Can a photographer actually capture what makes our windows different?"
- "We're spending so much on tariff-related cost increases, I can't justify another expense."
- "We don't even have a marketing plan — photography seems premature."

### What Creates Loyal Repeat Clients
- **Making the product the hero.** When you deliver images where their window is clearly the star — not just a hole in the wall — they'll be shocked. Most have never seen their product photographed well.
- **Cost-share facilitation.** Bringing them into builder shoots they didn't even know were happening. Free pipeline they never had to generate.
- **Technical understanding.** Knowing what sightlines are, understanding tilt-and-turn vs. casement, asking about their glazing stack — this signals you're not another generic photographer.
- **Speed and professionalism.** They're juggling 15 projects. Show up, nail it, deliver.
- **Architect-ready assets.** When you deliver images that an architect can use in their own portfolio and presentations, you've created a specification tool that makes the window company indispensable to that architect.
