The Cost-Share Model

On any custom home or renovation, multiple parties benefit from professional photography: the builder, the architect, the interior designer, the landscape architect, even the window and millwork suppliers. Rather than each hiring their own photographer, or none at all, you split the investment.

How it works

01
One Party Books

The builder, architect, or designer initiates the shoot. They control scheduling and have primary access to the site.

02
Collaborators Opt In

The photographer reaches out to other project collaborators. Each receives a licensing fee (typically 25-35% of the shoot cost) for usage rights to the full image set.

03
Everyone Gets Images

All parties receive the complete edited gallery with a license for their marketing use: website, social, awards, proposals, and print.

This is standard practice, not a favour. The photographer manages all outreach, licensing, and distribution. You don't need to coordinate with anyone. And it means every project collaborator gets professional images of their contribution, which makes the entire industry better.

Section 03

Is Your Project Photo-Ready?

The difference between a good shoot and a great one often comes down to preparation. Use this checklist to ensure your project is ready. Every item matters. One missed detail can compromise an entire composition.

Construction Completion
All construction 100% complete. No visible tools, materials, or debris.
All fixtures installed and functioning (switches, outlets, hardware, plumbing)
Touch-up paint complete on all surfaces
No painter's tape anywhere in the building
Baseboards, trim, and caulking all finished
All labels and stickers removed from windows, appliances, and mirrors
HVAC registers and covers installed
No visible scuff marks on floors or walls
Exterior & Landscaping
Landscaping installed and established (not fresh sod with visible seams)
Driveway, walkways, and patios completed
Exterior lighting installed and functioning
Construction fencing, signage, dumpsters, and trailers removed
Gutters and downspouts installed
Windows cleaned inside and out (critical for light quality)
Interior Finishing
All light bulbs installed and same colour temperature (mismatched bulbs ruin photos)
Window coverings installed (or intentionally omitted for clean aesthetic)
All doors on hinges with handles installed
Appliances installed with all packaging removed
No visible wiring or cable runs

The most common trap: "Everything is done except the landscaping." Exterior shots anchor the entire portfolio. A beautiful interior gallery undermined by a muddy, bare yard tells an incomplete story. If the landscaping isn't in, wait.

Section 04

Staging & Styling

The architecture should be the star. Staging creates depth, scale, and warmth, but it should never compete with the design. Less is almost always more.

The Day Before

  • Professional deep clean of the entire space
  • Windows cleaned inside and out (this is the single most common oversight)
  • Floors spotless, including corners and under furniture
  • Stainless steel polished, no fingerprints
  • All mirrors cleaned
  • Stone and quartz countertops polished

Styling That Elevates

  • Fresh flowers or greenery (simple, architectural, not busy)
  • Curated coffee table books relevant to the aesthetic
  • A lit fireplace for warmth and focal point
  • Fresh, matching towels in bathrooms
  • Bed made with hotel-level precision
  • One or two lifestyle elements (cutting board, wine glass, open book)

What to remove

Always Remove

  • All kitchen counter clutter (one curated arrangement only)
  • Fridge magnets, notes, and photos
  • Bathroom counter items (one soap, one towel max)
  • Garbage and recycling bins
  • All visible cords and cables
  • Doormats that don't match the aesthetic
  • All toilet lids should be closed

Common Mistakes

  • Over-styling: the architecture should breathe
  • Personal family photos in staged spaces
  • Holiday decorations (unless the shoot is seasonal)
  • Assuming the photographer will rearrange furniture (discuss scope in advance)
  • Last-minute additions that clutter the composition
Section 05

Timing, Light & Weather

Light is the single most important variable in architectural photography. The direction your building faces, the time of day, and the weather all determine whether the photos capture the design intent or flatten it.

Orientation Guide

S
South-Facing

Most flexible. Good light through most of the day. Ideal for consistent interior illumination.

E
East-Facing

Best for morning exterior shots. Warm, soft light through front-facing windows early in the day.

W
West-Facing

Best for afternoon and twilight. Golden hour illuminates the facade. Ideal for the signature dusk shot.

Twilight is the money shot. The 20-40 minute window after sunset when the sky turns deep blue and interior lights glow warm. This is the hero image that goes on your website banner. It requires all interior and exterior lights to be on and functioning. Plan for it.

BC Seasonal Guide

Spring
April - May

Ideal conditions. Green landscapes, longer days, moderate sun angles. Perfect balance of exterior and interior light.

Summer
June - August

Great for exteriors but harsh midday sun. Best to shoot mornings or evenings. Watch for wildfire smoke in the Interior.

Fall
September - October

Beautiful foliage for contextual shots. Golden quality of light. Days getting shorter, so twilight windows are brief.

Winter
November - February

Shortest days. Moody, dramatic exteriors. Snow on the right project is stunning. Interior-focused shoots work well.

Section 06

What Happens During a Shoot

A professional architectural photo shoot is methodical, not rushed. For a typical custom home (2,500-4,000 sq ft), expect a full day on site. Here's the process.

8:00
Arrival & Walkthrough

Assess every room. Identify hero compositions. Check light direction. Note any issues that need addressing before shooting begins.

8:30
Styling Pass

Adjust furniture placement, remove distracting items, add styling elements. This is where the space transforms from "lived in" to "photographed."

9:00
Systematic Shooting Begins

Work room by room, starting with spaces that have the best morning light. Multiple angles per room: wide establishing, medium detail, tight vignette. 30-60 minutes per composition.

12:00
Midday Break

Midday sun creates harsh shadows. Use this window for lunch, gear reset, and interior-only compositions that don't depend on window light.

14:00
Afternoon Session & Exteriors

Resume interiors with afternoon light. Begin exterior compositions timed to sun position. Drone aerials for site context and elevation views.

Sunset
Twilight Session

All lights on. The 20-40 minute blue hour window. This produces the signature hero image. Weather-dependent and requires advance planning.

Who should be on site

Yes

  • The photographer and any assistants
  • One point of contact (builder PM, architect, or designer)
  • A stylist if the project warrants it

Not Ideal

  • The homeowner during the shoot (too many opinions, slows the process)
  • Multiple stakeholders with competing visions
  • Pets (hair on surfaces, animals wandering into frames)

What You Receive

Professional architectural photography is not about pressing a shutter button. Each delivered image is the result of precise exposure control, post-production blending, colour science, and geometric correction.

40-80Complex Projects
2-4Weeks Turnaround
2Resolution Formats

What "editing" means in architectural photography

Technical Processing

  • Exposure blending: combining multiple exposures so windows and interiors are both properly lit
  • Colour correction: ensuring whites are white, wood tones are accurate, paint colours match reality
  • Vertical line correction: making walls and columns truly vertical (correcting lens distortion)
  • Geometric correction: ensuring parallel lines remain parallel throughout the frame

Creative Processing

  • Sky enhancement or replacement when overcast conditions don't serve the project
  • Object removal: construction debris, garbage bins, electrical panels, neighbour's clutter
  • Flambient blending: combining flash and ambient light for natural-looking interiors
  • Detail retouching: minor blemishes, power lines, temporary distractions

About raw files: Asking for unedited raw files is like asking a builder to hand over a half-framed house. Raw files are a starting point, not a deliverable. The editing is where the craft lives.

Section 08

Mistakes That Ruin Shoots

Every one of these has happened on a real project. They're all avoidable.

Before the Shoot

Booking before construction is truly complete, then scrambling on the day
Not coordinating with all parties. The designer stages furniture, the builder removes it. Nobody communicated.
Skipping the pre-shoot walkthrough. Surprises on shoot day waste expensive time.
Not cleaning windows. The single most common oversight that degrades image quality.
Ignoring the landscaping. Beautiful interiors undercut by a muddy, bare yard.

During the Shoot

Too many stakeholders on site giving competing opinions
Homeowner rearranging styling while the photographer is shooting
Dead bulbs or mismatched colour temperatures (3000K next to 5000K)
HVAC running during long exposures, causing curtains to move
Pets on site. Hair on surfaces, animals wandering into frames.

After the Shoot

Requesting excessive retouching. "Can you remove the neighbour's house?" is not a minor edit.
Waiting too long to use the images. Seasonal relevance fades.
Not sharing with collaborators. Missing the cost-share opportunity entirely.
Compressing images for web without proper optimization. Killing quality.
Section 09

Questions
Everyone Asks

"How much does architectural photography cost?"

Typically $2,000-$5,500+ per project depending on scope, size, and deliverables. This is significantly more than real estate photography ($300-500) because the use case is fundamentally different. Real estate photography is documentation. Architectural photography is storytelling. And with cost-sharing, the effective cost per party drops substantially.

"Can't we just use our phones?"

Phone cameras cannot handle the dynamic range of interior spaces. You get blown-out windows or dark rooms, never both properly exposed. They distort vertical lines and spatial proportions. Award programs reject non-professional imagery. And they position your $2M build next to a condo listing. The gap between phone and professional is not incremental. It's categorical.

"How far in advance should we book?"

Notify your photographer when the project is 4-6 weeks from completion. This allows time for scheduling, a pre-shoot walkthrough, and coordination with collaborators. Peak season (April-September) fills faster.

"What if it rains on shoot day?"

Professional photographers include a weather reschedule policy. Interiors can often still be shot on overcast days (and actually benefit from even, soft window light). Exteriors and twilight are weather-dependent. Most photographers reschedule at no additional charge within reasonable limits.

"Who owns the photos?"

Copyright remains with the photographer. You receive a license for specific uses: website, social media, awards, proposals, print materials. This is standard practice across the industry and is how cost-sharing works. Each party licenses usage rights to the same image set.

"Do we need to stage the space?"

Some level of staging is essential. An empty room photographs flat and lifeless. Furniture creates depth, scale, and warmth. The level of staging depends on the project. Your photographer can advise on what's needed and what's excessive.
