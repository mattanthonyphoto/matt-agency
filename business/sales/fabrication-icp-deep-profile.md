# Structural Fabrication ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 30, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Engineer-Artisan Tension

Structural fabrication company owners live at the intersection of engineering precision and physical craftsmanship. They came up through the trades — welders, fitters, ironworkers — and built businesses around their ability to make steel do exactly what drawings demand. Unlike builders who manage subcontractors, fabricators *make the thing.* Their identity is rooted in transformation: raw steel in, finished structure out. Every beam, connection, and weld is a direct reflection of their skill and standards.

The defining psychological feature: they are **makers trapped in business-owner bodies.** They would rather be on the shop floor solving a fit-up problem than reviewing marketing proposals. Their pride comes from precision — holding tolerances, passing CWB inspections, delivering on impossible timelines. When their work is invisible inside a finished building, they feel simultaneously proud (it's structural, it holds everything up) and undervalued (nobody sees it).

**Key traits:**
- **Process-obsessed perfectionists.** Their entire reputation depends on doing things right the first time. A bad weld isn't a cosmetic issue — it's a liability. This extends to how they evaluate everything, including vendors.
- **Technically confident, marketing-averse.** They can read engineered drawings that architects can't interpret, but ask them to describe what makes their shop special and they freeze. "We do quality work" is the best they can articulate.
- **Skeptical of anything non-technical.** They live in a world of specs, tolerances, and certifications. Marketing feels soft, unquantifiable, and unnecessary — especially when work has historically come through relationships and reputation.
- **Quietly competitive.** They know exactly which shops in the region do good work and which cut corners. They track who wins the big projects and who underbids. But they rarely talk about it publicly.
- **Fiercely independent.** Most started their own shops because they believed they could do it better. They resist outside advice, especially from people who don't understand steel.

### Demographics
- **Age:** 45-60 core concentration. Average welder age is 55, and shop owners tend to be senior welders/fitters who started their own operations 15-25 years ago.
- **Gender:** Overwhelmingly male. Spouses or office managers handle admin, bookkeeping, and increasingly, the marketing decisions.
- **Education:** Trade certifications (Red Seal, CWB), some with engineering technology diplomas. Very few have business degrees. Learning happened on the shop floor.
- **Company size:** 56.3% of Canadian steel product manufacturing establishments are small (5-99 employees). Another 29.5% are micro (under 5 employees). This is a fragmented industry of small shops.
- **Revenue range (target):** $1M-$15M. Owner compensation typically $80K-$200K depending on shop utilization and margins.

### Values
- Precision and quality above all (they chose custom over commodity deliberately)
- Safety (welding is dangerous — a culture of care defines good shops)
- Reliability (delivering on time, to spec, every time)
- Technical capability (investing in equipment, certifications, skilled people)
- Loyalty (long-term relationships with GCs and architects who trust their work)

### Self-Image vs. Market Perception
- **Self-perception:** Master craftspeople. The backbone of construction. Without steel fabricators, buildings don't stand. They solve problems that architects and engineers create on paper but don't understand in practice.
- **Market perception:** Invisible. Architects get design credit. Builders get project credit. GCs get management credit. Steel fabricators are a line item on a budget. Their work literally holds everything together, but nobody photographs it, celebrates it, or even knows their name.
- **The gap:** This invisibility is both their biggest pain point and their biggest photography opportunity.

### Personality Profile (ISTP — "The Craftsman")
Welders and fabricators overwhelmingly test as ISTP personality types: hands-on problem-solvers who are flexible, logical, self-directed, and independent. They adapt to changing situations, prefer action over discussion, and judge people by competence rather than credentials. They respect people who understand their world and dismiss those who don't.

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Day in the Shop
- **5:30-6:00 AM:** Owner arrives before the crew. Checks material deliveries, reviews the day's cut list, walks the shop floor.
- **6:00-7:00 AM:** Crew arrives. Morning briefing on priorities — which projects ship today, which connections need to be welded, what's behind schedule.
- **7:00 AM-Noon:** Active fabrication. Owner splits time between the office (estimating, coordinating with engineers, reviewing shop drawings) and the floor (quality checking, troubleshooting fit-up issues, jumping on a welder when short-handed).
- **Noon-3:00 PM:** Site visits for active installations. Meeting with GCs, engineers, or inspectors. Measuring for field connections. Problem-solving when things don't fit (and they always don't fit exactly).
- **3:00-6:00 PM:** Back to the shop for administrative work — quoting new projects, reviewing shop drawings from detailers, chasing payments, ordering material.
- **Evenings/weekends:** Catching up on estimating. The never-ending backlog of quotes.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night

1. **Labor shortage.** The average Canadian welder is 55. Roughly 30% of the welding workforce reached retirement eligibility by late 2025. The Canadian Apprenticeship Forum estimates a gap of 60,000+ registered apprentices. Finding CWB-certified welders who can hold tolerances is the single biggest constraint on growth. Vacancy-to-hire ratios for welders tightened to 1.5 in 2025.

2. **Material costs and tariffs.** Canada tightened tariff rate quotas for steel from non-FTA partners from 50% to 20% of 2024 levels (effective December 26, 2025). Over 60% of steel used by Canadian manufacturers in 2024 was imported. A 25% tariff on steel derivatives (wind towers, prefabricated buildings, fasteners) adds further cost pressure. Material prices swing weekly, making estimating a gamble.

3. **Cash flow and payment cycles.** Fabricators front material costs (steel isn't cheap), invest labor in fabrication, then wait 30-90 days for payment. Progress draws help on large projects, but holdback (10% in BC) creates permanent cash flow drag. Small shops are one slow-paying GC away from crisis.

4. **Scheduling chaos.** When the CNC plasma table or beam line goes down, the entire shop starves for parts within hours. Rush orders, engineering changes mid-fabrication, and erratic material deliveries create constant schedule disruption. WIP typically sits 3-5 days with 25-30 jobs being kitted simultaneously.

5. **Engineer approvals and RFIs.** Shop drawings need engineer stamps. Responses take days or weeks. Meanwhile, the GC wants steel on site yesterday. The fabricator is squeezed between two timelines they don't control.

6. **Mass timber competition.** BC is the mass timber capital of North America — 11x more mass timber buildings per capita than the rest of the continent. Mass timber generates 40-70% lower embodied carbon than steel. BC's building code now allows mass timber to 18 storeys. For mid-rise projects, steel is losing share to CLT/glulam, and fabricators feel it.

7. **Finding the right projects.** Commodity structural steel is a race to the bottom on price. Custom architectural metalwork and complex connections are where margins live, but those projects require relationships with architects and designers that most fabricators don't have.

### What Frustrates Them About Vendors
- People who don't understand steel, fabrication processes, or construction timelines
- Overpromising and underdelivering (they've been burned by marketing agencies, web designers, and branding firms)
- Requiring too many meetings or too much hand-holding
- Anyone who treats their shop like a dirty factory instead of a precision manufacturing facility
- Photographers who don't know what a moment connection looks like or why a clean weld matters

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Makes the Decision

**$1M-$3M (5-15 employees):** The owner decides everything, but the **office manager or spouse** increasingly influences marketing-adjacent decisions. Many shops have one admin person who handles the website, social media (if any), and marketing materials. That person is often the actual buyer.

**$3M-$10M (15-40 employees):** Owner approves budgets, but a project manager or business development person may champion the idea. In ornamental/architectural shops, the lead designer or shop foreman often has opinions about how work should be documented.

**$10M-$15M (40-80 employees):** More likely to have someone designated for marketing, or an outside agency. Owner still approves significant spend but delegates research. These shops are the most likely to already have professional photography — but usually inconsistent, project-by-project.

### What Triggers the Purchase

1. **A signature project completing** — a dramatic staircase, a complex canopy, a high-profile building where their steel is visible and architectural. "This one deserves real photos."
2. **Website rebuild or update** — they finally look at their site and realize it's iPhone photos from 2018. Especially triggered when they lose an RFQ to a shop with a better portfolio.
3. **CISC Awards submission** — the BC Region Awards of Excellence in Steel Construction (next gala: May 28, 2026, Paradox Hotel Vancouver) requires high-quality project photography. Submissions are judged on "complexity, originality, and beauty." You can't win without professional images.
4. **Capability documentation for RFPs** — larger projects (institutional, commercial) require qualification packages showing past work. iPhone photos signal "small shop." Professional photos signal "serious operation."
5. **New equipment investment** — they just spent $500K on a CNC plasma table or robotic welding cell. They want to document the capability to attract higher-value projects.
6. **Architect or designer request** — the architect wants photos of their project, and the fabricator's work is prominently featured. This is the strongest trigger because it comes with implicit validation.
7. **Competitor visibility** — they see a competitor's Instagram or website with professional project photos and clean shop documentation. Quiet competitive jealousy kicks in.

### How They Currently Handle Photography

| Method | Prevalence | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| iPhone photos from the shop floor | Most common | Poor — bad lighting, cluttered backgrounds |
| Nothing at all | Very common | N/A — projects ship and disappear |
| GC or architect's photographer | Occasional | Decent but doesn't showcase the metalwork specifically |
| One-off photographer for awards | Rare | Inconsistent — photographer usually doesn't understand steel |
| Professional architectural/industrial photographer | Very rare | Excellent (only at $10M+ shops or ornamental specialists) |

### The Core Problem

Steel fabricators build things that go into other people's projects. Once installed, the steel becomes invisible — hidden behind finishes, credited to the architect, or simply unnoticed. **They have no visual record of their own work.** Their portfolio is a collection of phone snaps, and their website is either nonexistent or looks like it was built in 2012.

This means:
- They can't differentiate from commodity shops in RFPs
- They can't attract architect-direct relationships
- They can't submit for awards
- They can't recruit young welders (who do look at websites and Instagram)
- They can't demonstrate capability growth (new CNC equipment, robotic welding cells, larger projects)

### How They Evaluate Photographers
1. **Do you understand steel?** If you call a moment connection a "joint" or don't know the difference between MIG and TIG, you're done.
2. **Portfolio relevance.** Real estate interiors photography means nothing to them. They want to see industrial environments, dramatic scale, raw materials.
3. **Referral from someone in their world** — a GC, engineer, architect, or another fabricator.
4. **Low friction.** They will not rearrange their shop or delay production for a photo shoot. You need to work around their operations.
5. **Speed.** They don't want a 6-week turnaround. Deliver fast or they'll forget they hired you.

### What Makes Them Say Yes
- Portfolio includes industrial/architectural work (steel, concrete, heavy materials)
- Clear, simple pricing — lump sum, not hourly
- You speak their language (tolerances, connections, CWB, clean welds)
- Cost-sharing with the architect or GC on installed work
- Promise of fast turnaround
- Demonstrated understanding of what makes their specific work impressive

### What Makes Them Say No
- Portfolio looks like wedding or real estate photography
- Too much "marketing speak" — they will physically recoil
- Feeling sold to rather than consulted
- Complicated pricing structures
- Requiring significant time or coordination from them
- Any implication that their shop needs to be "cleaned up" for photos

### Price Sensitivity — The Real Story

**More sensitive than builders, but for different reasons.** Fabricators operate on thinner margins (typically 8-15% net) and have never budgeted for photography. There is no line item for it in their mental accounting. The first conversation isn't about whether $2,500 is too much — it's about whether photography is worth anything at all.

- ~75% see photography as a **pure expense** ("nice to have, not a priority")
- ~20% see it as **project documentation** (useful for awards and capability packages)
- ~5% see it as a **strategic investment** (portfolio building, architect relationships, recruiting)
- Your sales job: move them from camp one to camp two, then build a case for camp three.

**Budget reality:**
- Marketing budgets are essentially zero for most small-medium fabrication shops
- Typical marketing spend: $0-$5K/year (website hosting, business cards, trade show booth)
- Photography budget: nonexistent — this is a category that needs to be created
- Entry point: $1,500-$3,000 for a first shoot (shop + 1-2 installed projects)
- Recurring potential: 2-4 shoots/year for active shops with regular project completions

## Where They Hang Out

### Online (Actually Used)
- **LinkedIn** — primary professional platform. Where they connect with GCs, engineers, and architects. B2B decision-makers overwhelmingly use LinkedIn; fabricators who post get 3-5x engagement on process videos vs. static posts.
- **Instagram** — growing, especially for ornamental/architectural shops. Before-and-after transformation content performs well. Shop process videos (sparks, CNC cutting, crane lifts) get genuine engagement.
- **Facebook** — local community, trade groups, sometimes used as a primary web presence for smaller shops.
- **YouTube** — consume heavily (welding technique videos, equipment reviews, project walkthroughs). Rarely create.
- **Houzz** — relevant only for ornamental/residential shops (railings, gates, stairs).

### Industry Publications & Media
- **The Fabricator (thefabricator.com)** — the bible. Every shop owner reads it. Covers shop management, technology, market forecasts, and business strategy.
- **Modern Metals** — covers industry awards, technology, projects.
- **Canadian Metalworking** — Canadian-focused industry coverage.
- **Welding Journal (AWS)** — technical welding content, certification updates.
- **Construction Canada / Daily Commercial News** — broader construction industry, project leads.

### Where They Network
- **CISC (Canadian Institute of Steel Construction)** — the national industry body. BC Region Awards of Excellence is the marquee event (May 28, 2026, Paradox Hotel Vancouver).
- **CWB Group (Canadian Welding Bureau)** — certification body, but also networking through courses and events.
- **Local construction associations** — VRCA (Vancouver Regional Construction Association), BCCSA (BC Construction Safety Alliance).
- **Trade shows:** NASCC: The Steel Conference (national), CISC regional events, FABTECH (manufacturing technology).
- **Informally:** Through GCs and engineers on job sites. Relationships are built project by project, not at networking events.

### How They Get Work (in order)
1. **Repeat GCs and builders** — 60-70% of revenue for established shops
2. **Engineer/architect specifications** — being spec'd by name on a project
3. **Bid invitations** — invited to bid based on past performance
4. **Open tenders** — commodity structural work, lowest price wins
5. **Direct architect relationships** — rare but highest-margin (custom/ornamental work)
6. **Website/online presence** — almost never, but growing for ornamental shops

## The Language That Works

### Say This
- **"Document"** not "shoot" or "photograph"
- **"Precision"** not "quality" (quality is generic; precision is their language)
- **"Your craftsmanship"** not "your product" or "your work"
- **"Tolerances"** — shows you understand what matters
- **"Clean welds"** — the visual signature of a good fabrication shop
- **"Shop capability"** — what their equipment and team can do
- **"Fabrication-to-installation"** — the full lifecycle of their work
- **"The drama of steel"** — scale, geometry, raw power
- Reference specific processes: plasma cutting, CNC, welding, fit-up, erection
- Reference certifications: CWB, CISC, Red Seal

### Never Say
- "Marketing" or "content creation"
- "Branding" or "brand story"
- "Social media strategy"
- "ROI" or "lead generation"
- "Aesthetic" (too soft — use "visual documentation" or "portfolio")
- "We'll make your shop look great" (implies it doesn't already)
- Anything that sounds like advertising

### Killer Lines

1. **"Every project you fabricate and don't photograph is a project that can never win you an award, land you a spec, or prove your capability."**
2. **"The shop that wins the CISC Awards isn't building better steel than you. They're documenting it better."**
3. **"You just spent $500K on that CNC table. Your website still shows phone photos from 2018. Which one is telling architects what you're capable of?"**
4. **"Architects spec fabricators they've seen do impressive work. If you can't show it, you don't exist."**
5. **"I photograph buildings for architects. I see what steel fabrication looks like inside a finished project — and I know who should be getting credit for it."**
6. **"A 20-year-old welder looking for an apprenticeship checks your website before they call. What are they seeing?"**

## Photography-Specific Angles

### The Three Photography Categories for Fabricators

**1. Shop & Process Photography**
The shop floor is inherently dramatic. Sparks, raw steel, massive equipment, precision work at scale. This is the most visually compelling content category and the easiest sell because owners already see their shop as impressive — they just haven't thought to document it professionally.

Shot list:
- CNC plasma/laser cutting (sparks, precision, scale)
- Welding (MIG/TIG arcs, sparks against dark backgrounds, welder portraits)
- Material handling (crane lifts, steel beams stacked, raw material yard)
- Assembly/fit-up (workers positioning large components, checking alignment)
- Equipment details (CNC controls, beam lines, robotic welding cells)
- Team portraits (welders in gear, shop foreman, crane operators)
- Shop environment (long shots showing scale, organized bays, overhead cranes)
- Detail shots (weld quality close-ups, connection details, tolerances visible)

**2. Installed Work Documentation**
The finished product in context — steel as architecture. This is harder to coordinate (requires access after installation, before finishes cover the steel) but highest-value for capability packages and awards.

Shot list:
- Architectural staircases and railings (light on metal, geometry, shadow play)
- Steel canopies and entrance features
- Exposed structural connections (moment connections, custom brackets)
- Ornamental screens, gates, and custom features
- Before/after: raw fabrication in shop vs. installed in building
- Scale shots showing human figure against steel structure
- Detail shots of connections, finishes, and hardware
- Light studies: how natural light interacts with steel surfaces

**3. Fabrication-to-Installation Sequences**
The full lifecycle story. This is the most powerful content for RFPs, capability packages, and awards submissions because it demonstrates end-to-end control.

Sequence:
1. Shop drawings/3D model on screen
2. Raw material (steel beams, plate, tube stock)
3. Cutting/profiling
4. Fit-up and assembly
5. Welding (with close-ups of weld quality)
6. Surface preparation and finishing (blasting, priming, powder coating)
7. Loading for transport
8. Site delivery and rigging
9. Erection and installation
10. Completed work in architectural context

### The Visual Drama of Steel

Steel is inherently photogenic in ways most fabricators don't realize:
- **Scale:** A 40-foot beam being moved by overhead crane creates instant visual impact
- **Sparks:** Plasma cutting and grinding against dark shop interiors is cinematic
- **Geometry:** Steel connections, web stiffeners, and gusset plates create abstract compositions
- **Light on metal:** Brushed steel, polished welds, powder-coated finishes all interact beautifully with directional light
- **Contrast:** Raw industrial environment vs. precision craftsmanship
- **Human element:** A welder in full gear, face shield down, working at height — this is aspirational for recruiting

### Photography Timing Windows

- **Shop work:** Anytime during active fabrication. Best during active welding/cutting. Morning light through shop doors creates natural drama.
- **Installed structural steel:** The narrow window between steel erection and when other trades cover it. Coordinate with the GC for access during this phase.
- **Ornamental/architectural steel:** After installation but before building occupancy. Can also shoot after occupancy for in-context lifestyle shots.
- **Awards submissions:** CISC BC Awards deadline was January 30, 2026 — plan shoots 2-3 months ahead of next cycle.

## BC Market Context (March 2026)

### The Tariff Landscape — A Domestic Fabrication Tailwind

Canada's steel protectionist measures are the single most important market factor for BC fabricators right now:

- **Tariff rate quotas tightened** from 50% to 20% of 2024 levels for non-FTA steel imports (effective December 26, 2025)
- **25% tariff** on steel derivative imports (prefabricated buildings, wind towers, fasteners) regardless of origin
- **Over 60% of steel** used by Canadian manufacturers in 2024 was imported — these tariffs force a domestic supply shift
- **$1B+ in new domestic demand** for Canadian steel expected from these measures
- **50% freight rate discount** on interprovincial steel/lumber shipments beginning Spring 2026
- **Public procurement rules** now favor Canadian steel, shifting demand to domestic mills

**What this means for fabricators:** More work, tighter supply, longer lead times, higher prices. Shops that can demonstrate capacity and capability will win. Professional documentation of shop capability becomes a competitive differentiator.

### BC Infrastructure Steel Demand

- **$37.7 billion** in taxpayer-supported capital investments over three years (Budget 2026)
- **$13.8 billion** for transit and transportation infrastructure
- **$3.9 billion** to build, renovate, and seismically upgrade schools
- **Broadway Subway extension** — $3 billion (massive structural steel requirement)
- **Highway 1 corridor improvements** — $1.2 billion between Kamloops and Golden
- Seismic forces in the Lower Mainland increased approximately **20-30%** under new BCBC 2024 code (effective March 2025). More seismic force = more steel required to resist it.

### Mass Timber Competition

BC leads North America in mass timber — 11x more mass timber buildings per capita than the rest of the continent. Mass timber now allowed to 18 storeys in BC. For mid-rise, mass timber is winning on carbon performance (40-70% lower embodied carbon). But:

- **Steel still dominates** high-rise, infrastructure, bridges, industrial
- **Hybrid steel-timber** construction is growing — steel connections in mass timber buildings create work for fabricators
- **Seismic requirements** in the Lower Mainland and Capital Region favor steel's ductility
- **Custom residential** — ornamental metalwork (stairs, railings, gates, screens) is unaffected by mass timber competition

### The Ornamental/Architectural Segment

The luxury residential market in BC's Sea-to-Sky, Sunshine Coast, and Vancouver is where ornamental metalwork thrives. Custom steel stairs, railings, gates, fireplace surrounds, screens, and architectural features are standard in $2M+ custom homes. This segment:

- Works directly with architects and designers (not GCs)
- Commands higher margins than structural work
- Values portfolio and design capability over lowest price
- Is the most photography-receptive segment of the fabrication market

## Objection Handling

### Objection 1: "We're a B2B business. We bid on specs, not photos."

**The psychology:** This is the most common and most deeply held objection. They believe work comes through relationships and bid competitiveness, full stop. Marketing is for consumer businesses.

**Response:** "You're right — you win on specs, price, and capability. But who gets invited to bid in the first place? When a GC has three shops they could call, what makes them think of you? When an architect is writing a spec and considers naming a preferred fabricator, what do they know about your work? A capability package with professional documentation of your shop, equipment, and installed projects is the difference between being one of thirty bidders and being one of three."

**The data point:** 82% of B2B sales leaders say referrals are their highest-quality leads. Professional project documentation is referral ammunition — it gives your advocates something concrete to share.

### Objection 2: "We don't have time for a photo shoot."

**The psychology:** They picture a disruptive all-day affair requiring them to stop production, clean up the shop, and pose for photos.

**Response:** "I work around your schedule, not the other way around. I show up during active fabrication — that's when the shop looks best anyway. Sparks flying, steel being cut, welders working — that's the content. I need 2-3 hours on your shop floor during normal operations and zero from you personally. For installed work, I coordinate with the GC and handle access myself."

### Objection 3: "Our work speaks for itself."

**The psychology:** Deep pride in the quality of their fabrication. Belief that good work leads to more work automatically.

**Response:** "Your work does speak for itself — when people can see it. But once it's installed, it's either hidden behind drywall or credited to the architect. The architect gets published. The builder gets the Georgie. Your steel holds it all up, but nobody knows that. Documentation is how you get credit for what you've already built."

### Objection 4: "Photography is expensive and we have thin margins."

**The psychology:** No mental budget category for photography. Every dollar is allocated to material, labor, and equipment. Marketing is overhead.

**Response:** "A single project photo shoot runs $1,500-$3,000. You spent $50K-$500K fabricating that project. The photography is a fraction of a percent to capture lasting documentation you'll use in every RFP, awards submission, and website update for years. And I facilitate cost-sharing with the architect and builder on every project — often reducing your cost to zero."

### Objection 5: "We tried getting photos before and they were terrible."

**The psychology:** They hired a generic photographer who didn't know what they were looking at. The photos looked like stock imagery or missed the details that matter.

**Response:** "I shoot buildings for architects, which means I understand structural systems, connections, and material quality. I know the difference between a fillet weld and a full-penetration groove weld, and I know which one to feature in a close-up. When I photograph your installed staircase, I'm lighting the steel to show the craftsmanship — not just the shape."

### Objection 6: "Nobody cares what our shop looks like."

**The psychology:** They undervalue their own environment because they see it every day. It's just where they work.

**Response:** "A young welder deciding where to apprentice cares. An architect wondering if your shop can handle a complex project cares. A GC evaluating whether to add you to their preferred list cares. Your shop is a 10,000-square-foot proof of capability — the equipment, the organization, the team. Right now, nobody can see it except the people who walk through the door."

### Objection 7: "We get all our work through relationships. We don't need marketing."

**The psychology:** True for established shops — until it isn't. Key contacts retire, GCs change ownership, architects move firms.

**Response:** "Relationships got you here, and they'll keep working. But the generation of GC project managers and architects coming up now researches online first. When a procurement manager Googles your shop before returning your call, what do they find? When your best GC contact retires next year, how does the new person know what you're capable of? Visual documentation is how relationships scale beyond the people who've been to your shop in person."

### Objection 8: "Just use the photos from the architect's photographer."

**The psychology:** Why pay for photography when someone else is already shooting the building?

**Response:** "The architect's photographer shoots the building, not your steel. In those photos, your work is background — a staircase in the corner of a wide-angle shot. I shoot for your portfolio: tight on the connections, the weld quality, the material finish, the geometry of the metalwork. You need photos where your fabrication is the hero, not set dressing."

## The Emotional Journey of Hiring a Photographer

### Hesitations
- "Is this really worth it? We've never needed photos before."
- "Our shop is messy. It's not photogenic." (It absolutely is.)
- "The last time someone came to the shop they didn't understand anything about what we do."
- "Who's even going to see these photos? Our clients don't browse Instagram."
- "I'd rather spend that money on a new welder or more steel."

### What Creates Loyal Repeat Clients
- **Understanding of steel.** Know what a clean weld looks like. Know what CWB certification means. Know the difference between structural and ornamental.
- **Zero disruption.** Work around active production. Don't ask them to stop a cut to get a better angle.
- **Speed.** Fast turnaround. They think in project timelines — weeks, not months.
- **Making them proud.** When they see professional photos of their work and say "that's what we do" — that's the moment they become a repeat client.
- **Architect bridge.** If you also shoot for the architect who spec'd the project, you become a connector. You bring the fabricator into the architect's visual world, creating spec opportunities.

## Strategic Approach for Matt Anthony

### Why This ICP Works

1. **Geographic advantage.** Multiple fabrication shops in Squamish and the Sea-to-Sky corridor (Carson's Custom, Puremetal, Whistler Welding Services), plus dozens in the Lower Mainland.
2. **Underserved market.** Almost zero fabrication shops have professional photography. The bar to impress is extremely low.
3. **Dual-revenue opportunity.** Photograph the fabrication AND the installed work. One project = two shoots. Or photograph the installed work alongside the architect/builder shoot you're already doing.
4. **Cost-share multiplier.** On projects with architect, builder, and fabricator involvement, you can split cost three ways. Each party pays less, you earn more.
5. **CISC Awards angle.** The BC Region Awards are a concrete, deadline-driven trigger. Target fabricators 6+ months before submission deadlines.
6. **Recruiting content.** With average welder age at 55 and acute labor shortages, shops need visual content to attract apprentices and young welders. This is a pain point they haven't connected to photography yet.
7. **Tariff tailwind.** Domestic demand for Canadian steel is surging. Shops are busier, more profitable, and completing more projects worth documenting.

### Entry Strategy

1. **Start with ornamental/architectural shops** — they already care about design, work with architects directly, and produce inherently photogenic work (stairs, railings, gates, screens).
2. **Leverage existing architect/builder relationships** — ask architects and builders which fabricators they use. Offer to include fabrication documentation as part of existing project shoots.
3. **Use the CISC Awards as a door opener** — "The next BC Awards cycle is coming up. Let me help you put together a submission that actually wins."
4. **Offer a shop documentation package** — low-commitment entry point that demonstrates your understanding of their world.
5. **Build a "steel portfolio" section on your website** — even 2-3 fabrication projects create a magnetic pull for an underserved market.

### Pricing Model

- **Shop Documentation Package:** $1,500-$2,500 (2-3 hours on shop floor, 15-25 edited images)
- **Installed Work Documentation:** $2,000-$3,500 (similar to architectural photography pricing)
- **Fabrication-to-Installation Sequence:** $3,500-$5,000 (multiple shoots across project lifecycle)
- **Awards Submission Package:** $2,500-$4,000 (shop + installed, specifically curated for CISC criteria)
- **Ongoing documentation:** Quarterly shop visits + project documentation, $6,000-$10,000/year
