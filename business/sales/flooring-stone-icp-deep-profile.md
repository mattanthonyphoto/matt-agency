# Flooring & Stone ICP — Deep Psychographic Profile

*Research compiled March 30, 2026*

---

## Who They Are Psychologically

### The Material Obsessive

Flooring and stone company owners are material people in the most literal sense. They think in terms of geological time, surface physics, and sensory experience. A granite fabricator can tell you the quarry a slab came from by looking at the crystal structure. A tile showroom owner has spent decades developing an eye for veining, movement, and color that most people will never appreciate. Their expertise is invisible to the untrained eye, which is both their strength and their curse.

The core psychological tension: **material expertise vs. design credibility.** They know more about their products than anyone in the room — more than the architect, more than the designer, more than the homeowner — but they're often treated as order-takers rather than creative collaborators. They source, fabricate, and install materials that define the entire character of a space, yet the designer gets the credit and the builder gets the project page.

**Key traits:**
- **Sensory-first thinkers.** They evaluate the world through touch and visual texture. They can feel the difference between a honed and leathered finish blindfolded. This extends to how they judge photography — they will immediately see if an image fails to capture the tactile quality of their product.
- **Quiet expertise.** Unlike builders who are craftsman-entrepreneurs or architects who are design-intellectuals, stone and flooring people are specialists who went deep, not wide. They know one thing exceptionally well.
- **Generational or immigrant-family businesses.** Many flooring/stone operations are family-run, often with roots in Italian, Portuguese, Greek, or Eastern European stone-working traditions. 25% of flooring businesses are multi-generational. The family identity is inseparable from the business.
- **Pride in provenance.** They don't sell "tile." They sell Calacatta from Carrara, travertine from Tivoli, basalt from the Columbia Plateau. Where a material comes from matters as much as what it looks like.
- **Showroom-centric.** The showroom is their cathedral. It's where material expertise becomes tangible, where they can put a slab in your hands and change your mind. Their identity is deeply tied to the physical experience of their space.

### Demographics
- **Age:** 45-60 core concentration, skewing older than builders. Average tile setter is 43; owners trend 50+.
- **Gender:** Male-dominated in fabrication and installation. Showroom sales and design consultation roles increasingly female. Spouses often run showroom operations, marketing, and accounts.
- **Education:** Trades-oriented. Many learned the craft through family or apprenticeship. Showroom/distributor owners may have business education but respect hands-on material knowledge above all.
- **Revenue (typical independent):** $500K-$5M for installers/fabricators. $2M-$20M for distributors and multi-location showrooms. Average flooring installation business turns over ~$234K.
- **Business structure:** Sole proprietors and family operations at the small end. Multi-location distributors (Ames, Julian Tile, Fontile) at the larger end. Very few mid-market — you're either a small shop or a regional distributor.

### Values
- Material authenticity (natural stone over engineered, or at minimum, honest about what it is)
- Craftsmanship in installation (a poorly installed slab is a personal offense)
- Long-term relationships with designers and builders (repeat specification is their lifeblood)
- The showroom experience (you can't sell stone from a screen — you need to feel it)
- Provenance and sourcing integrity

### Self-Image vs. Market Perception
- **Self-perception:** Material experts. Trusted advisors to designers. The people who make the vision real. "We're not just laying tile — we're grounding the entire design."
- **Market perception:** Subcontractors. Commodity suppliers. Interchangeable with the next tile shop. Homeowners Google "cheap tile near me." Designers treat them as vendors, not partners.
- **The gap:** This perception gap is where photography becomes powerful. Professional imagery of their installed work — not just product shots — elevates them from "supplier" to "design partner."

## Daily Life & Pain Points

### A Typical Week

**Showroom/Distributor Owner:**
- **7:00-8:00 AM:** Opening showroom, reviewing orders, checking shipment status from European or Asian suppliers
- **8:00-11:00 AM:** Designer consultations, slab selections, guiding clients through material options
- **11:00 AM-2:00 PM:** Coordinating deliveries, managing inventory, resolving installation issues on active projects
- **2:00-5:00 PM:** More consultations, quoting, following up on open proposals
- **Evenings:** Bookkeeping, ordering, occasionally attending design events or builder networking

**Fabricator/Installer Owner:**
- **6:00-7:00 AM:** Loading trucks, coordinating crews
- **7:00 AM-4:00 PM:** On job sites supervising installations, templating new projects, troubleshooting
- **4:00-7:00 PM:** Quoting, invoicing, material sourcing, dealing with suppliers
- **Weekends:** Catch-up work, showroom open on Saturdays (critical revenue day)

They don't have a photography problem. They have a **capacity problem.** Every hour spent on marketing is an hour not spent with a client in the showroom or on a job site.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night

1. **Skilled labor shortage.** The #1 industry crisis. The pool of qualified stone and tile installers has been shrinking for years. Average installer age is 43 and climbing. Many companies are booking installations 10+ weeks out, operating at 50-60% of normal labor capacity. Mandatory Saturday installations are increasingly common just to keep up. This isn't a future problem — it's a current emergency.

2. **Supply chain fragility.** Italian marble, Spanish porcelain, Chinese ceramic — global sourcing means global vulnerability. Shipping delays, container costs, and now tariff uncertainty (25% on goods crossing the US-Canada border, 4-6% price increases across flooring in 2026) compress margins that were already thin. The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) is adding documentation and potential carbon levies on natural stone imports starting late 2025.

3. **Material cost escalation.** Natural stone requires specialized equipment and skilled labor. Production costs keep climbing. Premium materials like bookmatched marble can cost $200-400/sq ft installed — the margin on the installation labor is where they make money, not the material markup.

4. **The education problem.** Natural stone requires significantly more training and sales time than engineered alternatives. Showing the range of color and visual variation, explaining maintenance profiles, managing expectations about natural variation — it's a longer, more involved sale. Many consumers default to quartz or LVP because it's "easier."

5. **Competing with engineered products.** Porcelain slabs that look like marble. LVP that mimics hardwood. Engineered quartz that's maintenance-free. Natural stone companies constantly fight the perception that their product is "too expensive" or "too much maintenance" when the real story is durability, uniqueness, and long-term value.

6. **Designer relationships.** Designers specify materials; homeowners approve budgets. If you're not on the designer's shortlist, you don't exist. Maintaining those relationships requires consistent showroom presentation, reliable service, and — increasingly — strong visual content that designers can reference and share.

### What Frustrates Them About Vendors
- People who don't understand materials (calling granite "marble," not knowing the difference between honed and polished)
- Marketing vendors who push generic templates that look the same as every other tile shop
- Photographers who make their showroom look like a warehouse instead of a design gallery
- Anyone who wastes their Saturday (the busiest showroom day)
- Agencies that optimize for clicks when they need foot traffic

## How They Buy Photography

### Who Makes the Decision

**Small fabricator/installer ($500K-$2M):** Owner decides everything. Photography is almost never considered. If it happens, it's triggered by a specific event (website rebuild, awards entry, a designer requesting images).

**Mid-size showroom/distributor ($2M-$10M):** Owner or general manager approves. Showroom manager or marketing coordinator (if one exists) may research options. The spouse/partner often manages the showroom aesthetic and has significant influence on visual decisions.

**Large distributor ($10M+):** Marketing department handles it. May work with agencies. More likely to have an existing photography budget and established relationships.

**The hidden decision-maker:** In showroom environments, the person who designs the vignettes and arranges the displays often has the strongest opinion about photography quality. This is frequently a showroom manager or senior design consultant — not the owner.

### What Triggers the Purchase

1. **Showroom renovation or expansion** — the single biggest trigger. New displays need new photos. This happens every 3-5 years.
2. **New material line launch** — Italian manufacturer sends new collection, need lifestyle imagery that supplier catalogs don't provide for local context.
3. **Website redesign** — realize their site looks like a 2015 template with stock photos that don't match their actual inventory.
4. **A completed signature installation** — a bookmatched marble feature wall, a custom terrazzo floor, an intricate mosaic. Too impressive not to document.
5. **Designer or builder requesting images** — "Can you send me photos of that stone you installed in the West Van project?" and they have nothing.
6. **Competitor's website looks better** — see a rival showroom with beautiful installed photography and realize their own site is embarrassing.
7. **Awards submission** — regional design awards, supplier awards, industry recognition programs.
8. **Social media pressure** — Instagram and Pinterest are overwhelmingly visual. Running accounts with supplier stock images feels hollow.

### How They Currently Handle Photography

| Method | Prevalence | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Supplier-provided product images | Most common | Generic, no local context |
| iPhone photos from installer/showroom staff | Very common | Poor lighting, wrong angles, no composition |
| Nothing (bare website, minimal imagery) | Common for installers | N/A |
| Stock photography | Occasional | Looks fake, doesn't show actual products |
| One-off local photographer | Rare | Inconsistent, usually doesn't understand materials |
| Professional architectural/product photographer | Very rare | Excellent (usually only large distributors) |

The critical gap: **Supplier images show the product in isolation. They never show the product in a real BC home, with real light, in a real installation context.** Every showroom in Canada has access to the same Porcelanosa catalog images. None of them have professional photography of that same tile installed in a Sea-to-Sky home with mountain light pouring across the surface.

### How They Evaluate Photographers
1. **Material understanding.** Can you capture veining? Do you know how to light a honed surface differently than a polished one? Can you show the depth in a piece of quartzite?
2. **Installed context.** They want to see their product in a finished space, not just a product swatch.
3. **Showroom photography capability.** Can you make a 2,000 sq ft showroom look like a design gallery?
4. **Referrals from designers or builders** — the designers who specify their materials are the most trusted referral source.
5. **Speed and flexibility.** Installations are time-sensitive. Once furniture goes in, you can't photograph the floor.
6. **Understanding of scale.** Floors, walls, feature surfaces — these are large-scale installations. Need someone who can convey both the macro impact and the micro detail.

### What Makes Them Say Yes
- Portfolio shows material texture and installed context (not just rooms with floors in them)
- Photographer can articulate the difference between shooting a stone slab and shooting an interior
- Simple pricing, ideally tied to a specific deliverable (showroom shoot, project shoot, product catalog)
- Cost-share facilitation with the builder or designer on the project
- Quick turnaround — they need images before the next designer meeting or trade show
- Understanding of their specific materials

### What Makes Them Say No
- Portfolio looks like real estate photography (wide angles, no material detail)
- Photographer doesn't understand materials ("it's all tile to me")
- Too expensive for "just showroom photos"
- Complicated process that requires too much of their time
- No clear connection between photography investment and business outcome

### Price Sensitivity — The Real Story

**More sensitive than builders, less than they should be.** Flooring/stone companies operate on thinner margins (especially fabricators), and photography feels like a luxury rather than a necessity. Marketing budgets average 3-4% of revenue ($2K-$10K/month), but photography competes with Google Ads, trade show fees, and showroom upkeep.

- ~75% see photography as an **expense** ("we just need some photos for the website")
- ~25% see it as an **investment** (designers share our images, better images = better specifications)
- **The unlock:** Frame photography as a tool that strengthens designer relationships — the #1 revenue driver for high-end stone and flooring companies.

## The Language That Works

### The Material Vocabulary

This ICP speaks a rich, specific language about their products. Using it correctly signals credibility instantly; getting it wrong signals you don't belong.

**Stone & Marble:**
- "Veining" — the natural lines and patterns in stone. Never "stripes" or "lines."
- "Movement" — the overall visual flow across a slab. High movement = dramatic. Low movement = subtle.
- "Bookmatching" — mirroring consecutive slabs so veining creates a symmetrical pattern. The pinnacle of luxury stone installation.
- "Character" — the unique variations in a piece. "Character" is always positive; "imperfection" is never used.
- "Patina" — how stone ages and develops over time. Desirable, not degradation.
- "Honed vs. polished vs. leathered" — three finish types with completely different light behavior. A photographer who understands this distinction earns immediate credibility.

**Tile & Porcelain:**
- "Large format" — tiles 24x48" or larger. The dominant trend. Minimal grout lines, seamless visual flow.
- "Rectified" — precision-cut tile with sharp edges for tight grout joints.
- "Body color" — whether the tile color runs through the entire piece (important for edge visibility and chipping).
- "Porcelain slab" — up to 5'x10', used for countertops, walls, and floors. Blurring the line between tile and stone.

**Flooring:**
- "Warmth underfoot" — how a floor feels to bare feet. Radiant heating + natural stone = the luxury standard.
- "Grounding a space" — the floor as the foundation of the design. The material that everything else relates to.
- "Transition" — where one material meets another. A critical detail that reveals installation quality.
- "Wear layer" — relevant for engineered products. Thickness = longevity.

**Texture & Finish:**
- "Tactile quality" — how a surface invites touch. Matte and honed surfaces are "warm." Polished is "dramatic."
- "Depth" — the three-dimensional quality visible in natural stone. What makes real stone look alive.
- "Variation" — natural stone varies slab to slab. This is a feature, not a bug, and selling this concept is central to their business.

### Say This
- "Capture the materiality" not "take photos of tile"
- "Document the installation" not "shoot the floor"
- "Show the variation" — acknowledge that every slab is unique
- "Your materials in context" — not supplier catalog images, but real installations
- Reference specific details: the veining direction, the grout joint width, how light hits the surface at different times of day
- "The showroom experience, online" — framing photography as extending their showroom's impact

### Never Say
- "It's just flooring" (the single most offensive phrase)
- "Tile" when it's stone, or "marble" when it's quartzite
- "Stock photos work fine" (dismisses their entire value proposition of unique, natural products)
- "Content creation" or "social media strategy" (too marketing-speak)
- "Imperfection" when describing natural variation
- "Cheap" or "affordable" when discussing their materials

### Killer Lines
1. "Every slab in your showroom is one-of-a-kind. Your supplier's catalog images show one version. Your clients deserve to see yours."
2. "Designers specify materials from images. If the only images are stock photos, you're invisible."
3. "You spend hours educating clients on the difference between honed and polished. One photograph can do that in a second."
4. "Your showroom is a $500K investment. Your website is showing it through an iPhone."
5. "The floor is the largest continuous surface in any room. It's also the most under-photographed."
6. "Bookmatching, veining, movement — these are visual stories. They need to be photographed, not described in a spec sheet."

## Photography-Specific Angles

### Why This ICP Needs Professional Photography More Than They Know

**The Texture Problem:** Flooring and stone are fundamentally tactile products. The entire sales process in a showroom revolves around touching, feeling, and seeing materials in person. Translating that sensory experience to a screen is extraordinarily difficult and requires professional skill with lighting, angle, and lens choice. An iPhone cannot capture the depth of veining in a quartzite slab. It cannot show the subtle warmth of a honed limestone. It cannot convey the way light moves across a bookmatched marble wall throughout the day.

**The Perspective Problem:** Floors are inherently challenging to photograph. Shot from standing height, they compress into a flat, uninteresting surface. Shot from too low, context disappears. The ideal flooring photograph balances the material detail with the spatial context — showing the floor as both a surface and as the foundation of the room. This requires architectural photography skill: understanding composition, leading lines, the relationship between floor and furniture, the way natural light rakes across a surface to reveal texture.

**The Reflection Problem:** Polished stone, glazed tile, and sealed surfaces all create specular highlights that can either enhance or destroy an image. Managing reflections while maintaining the surface's natural luster requires controlled lighting and precise positioning. This is not iPhone territory.

**The Scale Problem:** A 5'x10' porcelain slab looks like a 12"x12" tile in a bad photo. Conveying the scale and seamlessness of large-format installations requires compositional skill — using furniture, architectural elements, and human references to communicate size.

### Three Photography Categories for This ICP

**1. Showroom Photography**
- The showroom as design gallery, not warehouse
- Vignettes showing materials in curated settings
- Slab walls lit to reveal veining and movement
- The experience of walking through the space
- Value: refreshed every 2-3 years with showroom updates, used across website, Google Business Profile, social media

**2. Installed Project Photography**
- Materials in real homes with real light
- The floor as foundation of the designed space
- Detail shots showing texture, grout lines, transitions
- Before/after of transformations
- Lifestyle context: a family kitchen with a marble island, bare feet on heated stone floors
- Value: the most powerful sales tool. "This is our marble in a West Vancouver home" beats any catalog image.

**3. Material Detail / Product Photography**
- Macro shots of veining, crystal structure, surface texture
- Slab photography showing full pattern and movement
- Comparison shots (honed vs. polished same stone)
- Color accuracy critical — stone color must match reality
- Value: website product pages, specification sheets, designer reference material

### Why Stock Photos Fail This ICP

Stock photography of flooring and stone is uniquely problematic because:
- Every natural stone slab is genuinely unique. Stock shows one variation of one material. The showroom may carry 200 variations.
- Stock doesn't show local context. Italian marble in a Squamish home looks different than in a Miami condo — the light, the architecture, the surrounding materials are all different.
- Stock can't show the specific products they carry. If a client sees an image and asks for "that stone," the showroom needs to deliver exactly that.
- Designers can spot stock immediately. Using supplier catalog images signals that you don't invest in your own presentation — which makes designers question whether you'll invest in their project.

## The Emotional Journey of Hiring a Photographer

### Hesitations
- "We've always used supplier images — they're free and professional enough."
- "It's flooring. People walk on it. How much can you really do with a photo?"
- "We don't have the budget. Our margins are too thin."
- "The designer/builder should be the one hiring the photographer."
- "By the time the project is done, the homeowner has moved in and it's too late."
- "I don't want someone who doesn't understand materials making our stone look flat."

### What Creates Loyal Repeat Clients
- **Material literacy.** Photographer who knows the difference between Calacatta and Statuario, who asks about the finish before the shoot.
- **Texture mastery.** Images that make people want to reach through the screen and touch the surface.
- **Speed.** Photos delivered before the next designer meeting or trade show deadline.
- **Dual use.** Images that work for both the flooring company AND the builder/designer, making cost-share natural.
- **Consistency.** Every shoot captures material accurately — color, texture, scale.
- **Making designers share.** When a designer posts the flooring company's images on their own Instagram, that's the ultimate ROI.

## Objection Handling

### "We just use the supplier's images"

**Psychology:** This is the default because it's free and "professional enough." They don't realize the limitation until you point it out.

**Response:** "Supplier images show one version of one stone, photographed in a studio in Italy. Your clients want to see that stone installed in a real home, with real light, in their market. Every showroom in BC has the same Porcelanosa catalog. None of them have professional photos of that tile in a Squamish kitchen with morning light coming through the mountains. That's what makes you different."

### "It's just flooring — people don't care that much about the photos"

**Psychology:** Internalized dismissal. They've been treated as "just the floor guy" for so long they've started to believe it.

**Response:** "The floor is the largest continuous surface in any room. It's the first thing you feel and the foundation of every design decision in the space. Architects know this. Designers know this. The reason flooring doesn't get photographic attention isn't that it doesn't matter — it's that nobody has photographed it well enough to show how much it matters. When I photograph a bookmatched marble floor, people stop scrolling."

### "We can't afford professional photography"

**Psychology:** Thin margins are real. $1,500-$3,000 for a shoot feels like a lot when you're operating on installation labor margins.

**Response:** "The designers who specify your materials make their decisions from images. If your installed work isn't photographed, you're invisible in that decision process. One shoot that gets shared by three designers reaches more of your target audience than six months of Google Ads. And if we photograph the installation as part of the builder's project shoot, we can split the cost — you get showroom-quality images of your materials in context for a fraction of the standalone price."

### "The builder or designer should hire the photographer"

**Psychology:** They see themselves as a sub, not a principal. Photography is someone else's job.

**Response:** "Builders photograph for their portfolio. Designers photograph for theirs. Neither one is thinking about how to showcase your materials. When I shoot a kitchen, the builder wants the full room. The designer wants the styling. You want the close-up of the veining in the waterfall edge, the way light catches the honed surface, the detail of the mitered corner. If you're not directing part of the shoot, your materials become background."

### "We just renovated the showroom — we'll take some photos ourselves"

**Psychology:** They just spent $200K on the renovation. Adding photography feels like one more expense.

**Response:** "You just invested six figures in creating an experience. The showroom looks incredible in person. But 80% of your customers research online before they ever walk in. If your website shows iPhone photos of a half-million-dollar showroom, you're undermining the investment you just made. One professional shoot captures the full experience — the lighting, the vignettes, the material displays — and gives you 2-3 years of imagery across your website, Google profile, social media, and designer presentations."

### "Our products speak for themselves"

**Psychology:** Material confidence. They genuinely believe the stone is so beautiful it doesn't need professional photography.

**Response:** "I agree — your materials are extraordinary. That's exactly why they deserve to be photographed properly. A piece of Calacatta Gold has more visual depth than most works of art. But an iPhone compresses that depth into a flat image. Professional lighting reveals the layers of veining, the way the surface catches light at different angles, the warmth of the tone. I'm not adding anything to your product — I'm revealing what's already there."

### "We tried a photographer once and the colors were all wrong"

**Psychology:** Legitimate bad experience. Color accuracy in stone photography is notoriously difficult and critically important. A warm-toned marble that photographs cool will generate returns and angry clients.

**Response:** "Color accuracy is non-negotiable in stone photography. I calibrate for the specific material — warm stones need different white balance than cool ones. I'll show you proofs on a calibrated monitor before delivery, and I carry a color checker on every shoot so we can verify accuracy against the actual slab. If the images don't match the material, they're worthless to you. I get that."

## BC / Pacific Northwest Specifics

### The BC Flooring & Stone Market

**Key Players (Lower Mainland / Sea-to-Sky):**
- **Fontile** — 60+ years, European specialist. Only Western Canadian rep for Porcelanosa, Versace, Roberto Cavalli tiles. 270 Terminal Ave, Vancouver. High-end, design-forward.
- **Julian Tile** — Est. 1978. One of the top importers/distributors in Western Canada. Showrooms in Langley and Burnaby. Huge selection, industry staple.
- **Ames Tile & Stone** — 100+ years. Western Canadian distributor. Multi-location.
- **BC Floors** — Supply and installation across Greater Vancouver, Fraser Valley, Sea-to-Sky, Greater Victoria.
- **Creekside Tile** — Imports from Mexico, Europe, North Africa. Specialty/artisan focus.
- **DM Tile & Stone** — High-end installations, West and North Vancouver focus.
- **World Mosaic** — Vancouver, artisan and designer tile.
- **GL Stone Tiles** — Custom 2D/3D designs, overseas manufacturing partnerships.

**Local Stone Sources:**
- **Vancouver Island Marble Quarries** — locally quarried marble in white, grey, and black. Multiple operations near Cowichan Lake and Effingham Inlet.
- **K2 Stone** — Newcastle Quarry (Nanaimo) for sandstone, Crown Isle (Courtenay) for granite.
- **Margranite Industries** — nine local granite varieties from East Anderson River, Beaverdell, and Skagit Valley.
- **BC has 114+ known dimensional stone occurrences** — granite, gneiss, marble, quartzite. The "buy local" angle is real and underutilized.

### BC-Specific Market Dynamics

**Tariff pressure (2025-2026):**
- 25% tariff on goods crossing US-Canada border affecting some supply chains
- 4-6% price increases across flooring materials in 2026
- USMCA 2026 "sunset" review creates additional uncertainty
- EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism adding documentation burden and potential levies on natural stone imports
- Net effect: imported materials getting more expensive, creating opportunity for local stone and domestically-sourced alternatives

**Climate and design trends:**
- **Radiant heated floors** are the luxury standard in BC. Stone and tile are the ideal materials for radiant heat — they conduct and retain warmth better than any alternative. This is a major selling point in the BC market where the rainy climate makes "warmth underfoot" a visceral desire.
- **Indoor-outdoor living** drives demand for weather-resistant porcelain and natural stone that flows from interior to exterior. BC's mild climate extends the outdoor living season.
- **West Coast modern aesthetic** dominates — clean lines, natural materials, large-format tile, matte/honed finishes. Aligns perfectly with 2026's "warm minimalism" trend (bone, sand, clay, fog tones).

**Design trend alignment (2026):**
- Large-format porcelain slabs (up to 5'x10') — minimal grout lines, seamless flow. Dominant in new luxury construction.
- "New Nostalgia" — terracotta, ochres, deep greens, burgundy. Italian-influenced warmth.
- 3D and fluted wall tiles — sculptural surfaces creating shadow lines and architectural depth.
- Marble-look porcelain in dramatic colorways — black, white, green, rosso. Bookmatching in porcelain achieving luxury look at lower cost.
- Terrazzo comeback — $25.6B market, 38% commercial, growing fast in high-end residential. Completely custom colors and patterns.
- Matte and honed finishes overtaking polished — "soft, velvety appearance" that fits modern and organic interiors.

### How PNW Flooring & Stone Companies Differ
- Stronger sustainability consciousness (local stone sourcing, CBAM awareness, environmental building codes)
- Higher integration with radiant heating systems than most North American markets
- Designer-driven market — Vancouver's interior design community is sophisticated and well-connected
- Seasonal compression — good weather months are prime installation season AND prime photography season
- Mountain and ocean light create unique photography opportunities that don't exist in other markets

## Outreach Strategy Notes

### Entry Points
1. **Post-showroom renovation** — monitor for showroom reopenings, renovation announcements. This is the highest-conversion trigger.
2. **New collection launches** — when a showroom announces a new Italian or Spanish line, they need local lifestyle imagery.
3. **Cost-share with builders** — photograph a completed project and deliver images to both the builder and the flooring/stone supplier. Two clients, one shoot.
4. **Designer referral** — if a designer you work with specifies a particular showroom, introduce yourself as someone who can photograph the installed result for both parties.
5. **Google Business Profile audit** — many showrooms have thin or no GBP imagery. Offer a quick audit showing competitors with better visual presence.

### Package Framing
- **Showroom Refresh** — half-day shoot of showroom displays, vignettes, slab gallery. 25-40 images. Refresh every 2-3 years.
- **Project Documentation** — photograph installed materials in completed homes. 15-25 images per project. Cost-share with builder/designer.
- **Material Detail Package** — macro and product shots of new collections. 30-50 images per collection. Annual or semi-annual with new lines.
- **Annual Visual Partner** — combine all three. Quarterly showroom updates + 3-4 project shoots + new collection coverage.

### The Cost-Share Angle
This ICP is uniquely suited to cost-sharing because every flooring/stone installation exists within a larger project that a builder and/or designer also wants photographed. Position yourself as the photographer who serves all three parties from a single shoot:
- Builder gets their project images
- Designer gets their portfolio images
- Flooring/stone company gets material-focused detail and context shots
- Total cost shared three ways makes it accessible for all
