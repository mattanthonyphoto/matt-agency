# Lighting Guide

Lighting approaches for interior, exterior, twilight, and drone photography. Written for consistency across shoots and for onboarding second shooters.

---

## Philosophy

Natural light is primary. Supplemental lighting is invisible — it should feel like the room always looks this good. If someone can tell flash was used, it was used wrong.

---

## Interior Lighting

### Approach 1: Natural Only

**When:** Bright, well-lit spaces with large windows. Overcast days (nature's softbox). Spaces where the light IS the design (skylights, clerestory, light wells).

**How:**
- Expose for the room's mid-tones
- Let windows blow slightly if needed — or bracket for a window pull
- Use a reflector to bounce light into deep shadows if needed
- Shoot during the room's optimal light window (from scout notes)

**Settings baseline:**
- ISO 100-400
- f/8-f/11 (depth of field across the room)
- Tripod mandatory
- Bracket ±2 stops for blending options

### Approach 2: Fill Flash (Ambient Dominant)

**When:** Rooms with strong directional light creating deep shadows. Spaces where natural light alone leaves one side too dark. Kitchens, bathrooms, hallways with limited windows.

**How:**
1. Set exposure for ambient (what the room looks like to the eye)
2. Add flash at -2 to -3 stops below ambient — it should lift shadows, not light the room
3. Bounce off ceiling or wall behind camera — never direct
4. If ceiling is colored (wood, painted), bounce off a white card instead
5. Multiple pops from different positions for even fill (light painting)

**Gear:**
- 1-2 speedlights (manual mode, not TTL)
- Bounce card / white umbrella
- Radio triggers
- Step stool (for ceiling bounce in tall rooms)

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Flash too bright — overpowers ambient, creates that "real estate" look
- Direct flash — hard shadows, hot spots
- Forgetting to match flash white balance to ambient
- Visible flash reflection in glass, mirrors, or polished surfaces

### Approach 3: Window Pull (Exposure Blend)

**When:** Interior shots where preserving the exterior view through windows matters — mountain views, garden views, architectural context.

**How:**
1. Expose for the interior (windows blow out)
2. Expose for the window view (interior goes dark)
3. Blend in Lightroom or Photoshop — mask the window area
4. The blend should be invisible. No halos, no HDR glow.

**Settings:**
- Tripod locked (compositions must be identical)
- Bracket: typically 3 exposures at 2-stop intervals
- Don't move the camera between brackets

### Approach 4: Full Ambient Lighting

**When:** Dark spaces with minimal natural light (wine cellars, media rooms, interior bathrooms). Spaces where the architectural lighting design is the feature.

**How:**
1. Turn on all designed lighting (pendants, sconces, under-cabinet, cove)
2. Assess what the architect/designer intended the space to feel like
3. Add supplemental flash only to fill shadows the designed lighting doesn't reach
4. Keep supplemental light at a lower color temperature to blend with warm interior fixtures
5. Expose to show the lighting design — don't flatten it

**Key principle:** In these spaces, the lighting IS the design. Document it, don't overpower it.

## Exterior Lighting

### Daylight Exteriors

**Timing:**
- Overcast: shoot any time — even, diffused light, no harsh shadows
- Sun: early morning or late afternoon for warm, raking light across facade
- Avoid midday sun — harsh overhead shadows under eaves, porch

**Approach:**
- No supplemental lighting needed for most exteriors
- Expose for the building, let sky be natural
- No sky composites — shoot when the sky cooperates or accept what's there
- Include landscape and context — the site is part of the architecture

### Twilight / Blue Hour

**Timing:**
- Arrive 30 min before sunset to prep and get dusk shots
- Peak blue hour: 15-25 minutes after sunset (sky goes deep blue, interior lights glow warm)
- Window closes fast — have compositions pre-planned from scout notes

**Setup:**
1. Turn on every interior light in the house (ALL of them — even closets near windows)
2. Turn on all exterior lighting (landscape, pathway, entry, garage)
3. Light fireplace / fire pit if available
4. Pool / hot tub lights on (if applicable)
5. Walk the house one more time — any dark rooms visible from exterior need light

**Camera settings:**
- Tripod mandatory
- ISO 100-200
- f/8-f/11
- Long exposures (2-15 seconds typical)
- Bracket for blending if needed

**Key principle:** Twilight is about contrast — warm interior glow against cool blue sky. The building becomes a lantern. Time it right, prep the lights early, and work fast once the window opens.

## Drone Lighting

### Timing rules

| Light condition | When | Best for |
|----------------|------|----------|
| Overcast | Anytime | Even exposures, no harsh shadows on roof |
| Golden hour | Morning or late afternoon | Warm light, long shadows show terrain |
| Midday | Only if overcast | Avoid — harsh shadows, flat light on facades |
| Twilight | 10-20 min after sunset | Dramatic — requires high ISO or bracketing |

### Approach

- Color-match drone shots to ground-level set (per style guide)
- Horizon perfectly level — non-negotiable
- Include environmental context — show the property in its setting
- Vary altitude: high (context), mid (3/4 elevation), low (near eye level)
- Shoot RAW for maximum grading flexibility

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Mixed color temps (daylight + tungsten + LED) | Gel the flash to match dominant source. Correct remaining in post. |
| Flash reflection in glass/mirrors | Angle flash away from reflective surfaces. Shoot reflection-free frame first, blend. |
| Dark corners that flash can't reach | Light paint: long exposure, walk flash around the space firing manually. |
| Ceiling too high to bounce | Use umbrella at 45° behind camera, or light paint from floor level. |
| Interior lights on unwanted color cast | Turn off problem fixtures. Document which ones for post-production notes. |
| Twilight window closing too fast | Pre-plan 3 compositions max. Shoot wide first (most impactful), details second. |
