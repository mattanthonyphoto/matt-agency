# Getting Your Work Into Magazines

A practical guide for submitting architectural photography to design publications. Written for a photographer who shoots the work, prepares the submission, and manages the entire process on behalf of clients.

---

## How the Editorial World Actually Works

### The Editor's Day

An editor at a publication like Dezeen, Dwell, or Western Living receives anywhere from 20 to 100+ project submissions per week. They're scanning, not reading. Your pitch gets about 8 seconds before they decide to look closer or move on.

What makes them stop:

1. **A strong hero image.** Before they read a single word, they see the thumbnail. If it doesn't grab them, nothing else matters.
2. **A clear design story in the first sentence.** Not "beautiful home in the mountains" but "a concrete and timber residence cantilevered over a granite bluff on Howe Sound, designed to disappear into the rock face at certain angles."
3. **Complete information.** Editors don't chase missing credits, floor plans, or project descriptions. If the submission is incomplete, they skip it.

### What Gets Published vs. What Gets Ignored

**Published:**
- Projects with a clear concept (not just expensive finishes)
- Strong site response (the building has a relationship to its landscape)
- Material specificity (the architect chose Cor-Ten steel for a reason, not just because it looks nice)
- Unusual typologies or constraints (a house built on a flood plain, a renovation of a heritage building, a net-zero cabin)
- Human stories (who lives here, what was the brief, what compromises were made)
- Clean, intentional photography that communicates design intent

**Ignored:**
- Generic luxury homes with no concept beyond "expensive"
- Over-processed images (heavy HDR, replaced skies, extreme wide-angle)
- Incomplete submissions (no credits, no description, six images instead of fifteen)
- Marketing language ("world-class craftsmanship", "stunning panoramic views")
- Projects that have already been published on three other platforms with no exclusivity awareness

### The Photographer's Unique Position

Most project submissions come from architects or their PR firms. The photographer almost never submits directly. This is an enormous opportunity.

Why:
- Architects are busy running projects. Submissions get pushed to "someday."
- PR firms batch-submit generically. They don't tailor pitches to specific editors.
- The photographer is the only person who has both the images and an understanding of what editors want visually.
- When you submit on behalf of the architect, you become indispensable. You're not just delivering files, you're delivering press.

This is the entire basis of the competitive moat. No architectural photographer in BC explicitly offers this as a structured service.

## The Publication Landscape

### Tier 1: Global Reach

These publications reach hundreds of thousands to millions of readers. Getting published here changes your career trajectory and your client's.

**Dezeen**
- The most-read architecture and design publication in the world
- Wants exclusivity. If you submit here, don't submit anywhere else until you hear back.
- Strong editorial voice. They pick projects with a design narrative, not just pretty photos.
- Submission: Email submissions@dezeen.com with a short pitch and 5-6 hero images
- Subject line format: `[City, Country] [Project Name] by [Architect Firm]`
- Response time: 2-8 weeks. No response after 3 weeks likely means no.
- One polite follow-up at 3 weeks is acceptable. After that, move on.
- BC advantage: They love dramatic landscape settings. Mountain homes, ocean-facing residences, and projects embedded in challenging terrain perform well.
- What to avoid: Projects already on ArchDaily (kills exclusivity). Generic luxury without concept.

**ArchDaily**
- The largest architecture platform globally. 15M+ monthly visitors.
- Does NOT require exclusivity. You can submit here alongside other publications.
- Self-serve submission portal. Create an account, upload images and project data.
- Highest acceptance rate of any major publication (~40-60% for quality work).
- Requirements: Minimum 15 images at 2880px+, full project metadata, credits, and description.
- Floor plans significantly increase acceptance odds. If you have them, include them.
- Turnaround: 1-4 weeks. Portal shows submission status.
- Why this matters even though it's "easy": Every ArchDaily publication is a Google-indexed page with your photo credit. Architects search here. It builds your name in the ecosystem. And you can reference it when pitching higher-tier outlets.

**Architectural Record**
- US-focused, institutional. Very competitive.
- Best for significant public or commercial commissions.
- Less relevant for BC residential work unless the project is exceptional.

**AD (Architectural Digest)**
- Interiors-focused. Global prestige.
- Wants exclusivity. Very selective.
- Best for high-end residential interiors with a design story.
- Often works through PR firms, but direct pitches to the editor are possible.

### Tier 2: Design and Lifestyle

**Dwell**
- The bridge between architecture and lifestyle. Reaches design-conscious homeowners.
- Cares about the human story as much as the design. Who lives here? What was their brief?
- Strong Instagram presence (1M+ followers). A Dwell feature gets shared widely.
- Will often want to interview the homeowner and architect separately.
- Submission: Email editorial@dwell.com or use their online form
- Subject line: `Story Pitch: [Project Name], [City]`
- Lead with the lifestyle angle, not the architecture. "A young family's decision to build a passive house on a cleared forestry lot in Squamish" is better than "a 3,200 sq ft residence featuring exposed timber framing."
- Best for: Residential with a human story, renovations with before/after, sustainability-driven projects.

**Western Living**
- Dominant in BC and Western Canada. 25,000 print distribution.
- Every architect, designer, and builder in the region reads it.
- The Design of the Year (DOTY) awards are the most prestigious in Western Canada.
- BC projects are essentially required. This is your home turf advantage.
- Email the editor directly (check the current masthead at westernliving.ca).
- They want the West Coast aesthetic: natural materials, indoor-outdoor connection, mountain and ocean context.
- Strong kitchen, living room, and primary bedroom coverage is important.
- The designer or architect needs to be willing to provide quotes for the feature.

**Azure Magazine**
- Canadian design publication. Covers architecture, interiors, product design.
- Design-forward editorial voice. Appreciates material craft and concept.
- Good for Canadian projects that have a strong design narrative.
- Less lifestyle-focused than Western Living, more intellectually design-driven.

**Wallpaper***
- Global design and lifestyle. Very selective.
- Wants exclusivity. Best for projects with material innovation or conceptual ambition.
- Less likely for standard residential, but worth pitching for the right project.

**Luxe Interiors + Design**
- Regional editions across North America. Luxury residential focus.
- Worth investigating whether a Pacific Northwest or Western Canada edition exists or if BC projects fit their West Coast coverage.

### Tier 3: Canadian and Regional

**Canadian Architect**
- Every architect in Canada reads this. Professional industry publication.
- Technical depth is appreciated. Include floor plans.
- Their Photo Awards of Excellence let you enter directly as a photographer (more on this below).
- Good acceptance rate for quality Canadian projects.

**Canadian Interiors**
- Interior design focus. Canadian projects.
- Less competitive, good for building publication credits.

**House & Home**
- Canadian residential. Lifestyle and interiors focus.
- Broader audience than the trade publications.

### Tier 4: Online and High-Volume

**Designboom**
- Accepts simultaneous submissions. Quick turnaround.
- Good for building a body of published work.

**Leibal**
- Minimalist design focus. Clean aesthetic.

**Archinect**
- Community-driven architecture platform.

**v2com newswire**
- Canadian press distribution service for design projects. Sends your project to dozens of editors simultaneously.
- Worth investigating as a distribution channel after you've secured your exclusive pitches.

## The Submission Package

Every project you intend to submit needs a complete package prepared before you pitch anyone. Editors will request the full set if they're interested, and you need to send it within 24 hours. Delays lose placements.

### 1. Project Brief

A single-page document with all the facts:

- **Project name** — use whatever the architect calls it
- **Location** — city, province, country
- **Project type** — new build, renovation, addition, adaptive reuse
- **Size** — square footage, lot size if relevant
- **Completion date** — month and year
- **Budget range** — only if the client approves sharing it. "Undisclosed" is perfectly fine. Many top projects don't share budget.
- **Full credits** — this is critical. Every firm involved:
  - Architecture
  - Interior design
  - General contractor / builder
  - Landscape architecture
  - Structural engineering
  - Any specialty consultants (lighting, acoustic, envelope)
  - Photography (you)

Editors cross-reference credits. Missing credits signal an incomplete submission.

### 2. Project Description

200-400 words written in third person, past tense. This reads like editorial copy, not marketing material.

**Structure it like this:**

*Paragraph 1:* The design problem or site condition. What constraint or opportunity shaped this project? ("Perched on a south-facing slope above the Squamish River estuary, the site offered unobstructed views of the Stawamus Chief but presented a 40% grade that conventional construction couldn't address.")

*Paragraph 2:* The design response. How does the building solve or respond to that condition? Spatial organization, material logic, relationship to landscape. ("The residence steps down the slope in three interlocking volumes, each rotated slightly to follow the contour and frame a different aspect of the mountain.")

*Paragraph 3:* Specific details that reward attention. A material junction, a framed view, a threshold experience. ("The transition from the concrete entry sequence into the double-height living space is marked by a shift from board-formed concrete to oiled Douglas fir, a deliberate material softening that signals arrival.")

*Paragraph 4 (optional):* Performance, sustainability, or broader context. ("The heavy timber structure sequesters approximately 45 tonnes of carbon, and the building's compact envelope achieves Passive House-adjacent performance without certification.")

**Language rules:**
- Be specific. "Board-formed concrete" not "beautiful finishes." "Western red cedar rainscreen" not "warm wood cladding."
- Name materials. Douglas fir, Cor-Ten steel, basalt, zinc, terrazzo.
- Describe spatial experiences. How does it feel to move through the building?
- Reference orientation, light, and views. "South-facing glazing" tells the reader about solar strategy.
- No marketing language. Cut every instance of: stunning, breathtaking, luxurious, world-class, unique, spectacular, exquisite, state-of-the-art, seamless, bespoke (unless genuinely describing bespoke fabrication).

### 3. Image Set

**Quantity:** 15-25 selects per project.
- ArchDaily requires minimum 15
- Dezeen typically wants 20+
- Most others want 10-15 for initial review, full set on request

**Technical specs:**
- 2880px+ on the long edge (your Z7ii's native 6720 x 4480 is more than sufficient)
- JPEG, sRGB color space, quality 10-12 in Lightroom
- IPTC metadata populated: photographer name, copyright notice, location, caption

**File naming:** `[project-name]-[descriptor]-matt-anthony.jpg`
- Example: `howe-sound-residence-exterior-south-matt-anthony.jpg`
- Example: `howe-sound-residence-kitchen-detail-matt-anthony.jpg`

**What to include:**

| Shot Type | Count | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Hero exterior | 1-2 | The thumbnail. The image that sells the click. |
| Supporting exteriors | 2-3 | Different angles, showing the building in context |
| Establishing / site context | 1-2 | Drone or wide shot showing the building in its landscape |
| Twilight / dusk | 1-2 | Dramatically increases editorial selection odds |
| Hero interior (living/main space) | 1-2 | The primary interior experience |
| Kitchen | 1-2 | Highest-performing interior content across all publications |
| Primary bedroom / bathroom | 1-2 | Editors expect these for residential |
| Detail shots | 3-5 | Material junctions, hardware, joinery, stair details, light patterns |
| Threshold / transition moments | 1-2 | Entry sequence, indoor-outdoor connection, hallway views |
| Human scale reference | 1 | Furniture arrangement, a doorway, something that gives proportion |

**Image selection principles:**
- Lead with your strongest exterior. This is the image that determines whether an editor clicks through.
- Twilight shots are disproportionately effective. The warmth of interior light against a blue sky creates immediate editorial appeal.
- Detail shots should tell a material story. A close-up of a steel bracket meeting a timber beam says more about the design than a wide shot of a living room.
- Show the building in its site. Editors want to understand the relationship between the architecture and its landscape. This is especially important for BC projects where the landscape is dramatic.
- Avoid over-processing. No heavy HDR. No sky replacements. No extreme perspective correction that makes the building look like a rendering. Editorial standards are cleaner and more natural than commercial real estate standards.
- Include at least one image that shows indoor-outdoor connection. This is the defining feature of West Coast residential architecture and editors know it.

**Captions:** Every image needs a caption. Format: `[Room/location], [key materials], [orientation/view]`
- Example: "Living room facing south toward Howe Sound. Douglas fir ceiling structure, board-formed concrete fireplace surround, polished concrete floor."
- Example: "Entry sequence from carport. Cor-Ten steel canopy, basalt pathway, planted roof visible above."

### 4. Floor Plans and Drawings

This is the single easiest way to increase your acceptance odds. Many photographers skip this because they don't think to ask.

Ask the architect for:
- Site plan (showing building in context)
- Main floor plan (at minimum)
- Upper/lower floor plans if the spatial story requires them
- One section drawing if the project has a vertical story (split levels, double heights, hillside stepping)

Format: High-resolution PDF or PNG, clean linework. If the architect gives you CAD files, ask for a clean export.

Most architects will provide these gladly. The project is getting published, which benefits them. If they hesitate, remind them that publications like Canadian Architect and ArchDaily almost expect plans, and having them increases the odds significantly.

### 5. Exclusivity Log

For every project, maintain a simple record:

| Publication | Date Pitched | Exclusive? | Response | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dezeen | 2026-04-15 | Yes | Declined 2026-05-01 | Closed |
| ArchDaily | 2026-05-02 | No | Accepted 2026-05-10 | Published |
| Western Living | 2026-05-02 | No | No response | Follow up sent 2026-05-20 |

This prevents you from accidentally double-pitching an exclusive, forgetting a follow-up, or losing track of what's been submitted where.

## The Pitch

### Anatomy of a Good Pitch Email

Editors are scanning. Your email needs to work in 8 seconds. Here's the structure:

**Subject line:** Follow the publication's convention.
- Dezeen: `[Squamish, Canada] Howe Sound Residence by STARK Architecture`
- Dwell: `Story Pitch: Howe Sound Residence, Squamish BC`
- Others: `Project Feature: Howe Sound Residence, Squamish BC`

**Opening (2-3 sentences):** The design hook. What makes this project editorially interesting? Not adjectives. A concept, a constraint, a spatial idea.

> A concrete and timber residence steps down a 40% grade on the Squamish River estuary, with each of its three volumes rotated to frame a different aspect of the Stawamus Chief. Architect Joanna Stark designed the structure to disappear into the granite bluff when viewed from the water below.

**Project summary (2-3 sentences):** The facts. Type, size, location, completion, one specific material or spatial detail.

> The 2,800 sq ft home was completed in fall 2025 for a couple relocating from Vancouver. The heavy timber structure uses locally milled Douglas fir, with board-formed concrete retaining walls that terrace into the hillside.

**What you're offering (1-2 sentences):**

> I have 22 publication-ready images at full resolution, plus floor plans and a section drawing from the architect. This project has not been published elsewhere.

Or if offering exclusivity:

> I'm offering [Publication] an exclusive first look at this project.

**Close (1 sentence):**

> Happy to send the full image set and project brief if you're interested.

**Signature:**
```
Matt Anthony
Architectural Photographer
mattanthonyphoto.com
604.765.9270
```

**Total length:** Under 200 words. Shorter is better. Attach or embed 5-6 hero images (not the full set).

### What NOT to Do

- Don't write a novel. If your pitch is longer than one screen on a phone, it's too long.
- Don't lead with yourself. "I'm an architectural photographer based in Squamish" is wasted space. The editor doesn't care about you yet. Lead with the project.
- Don't use marketing language. Editors are allergic to "stunning," "breathtaking," and "world-class."
- Don't attach 25 full-resolution images to the first email. Send 5-6 heroes. They'll ask for the rest.
- Don't submit to Dezeen and ArchDaily simultaneously. Dezeen wants exclusivity. Pitch Dezeen first, wait, then move on.
- Don't follow up more than once. One check-in at 2-3 weeks. After that, silence is your answer.
- Don't pitch a project that isn't ready. If you don't have the full package (images, description, credits, plans), wait until you do.

### Tailoring the Pitch by Publication

Each publication has a different editorial lens. The same project gets pitched differently to each:

**Dezeen:** Lead with the design concept. What's architecturally interesting? Material innovation, site response, structural ambition. Spare, intellectual language.

**Dwell:** Lead with the human story. Who are the owners? Why did they build? How do they live in the space? Warm, narrative tone.

**Western Living:** Lead with the BC context. The landscape, the region, the West Coast lifestyle. Mention indoor-outdoor connection, natural materials, mountain or ocean views.

**Canadian Architect:** Lead with technical credibility. Design rationale, structural approach, performance. Professional tone. Mention awards if relevant.

**ArchDaily:** Straightforward and comprehensive. The portal asks for specific fields. Fill everything in completely. The description can be more technical here.

## Getting Client Permission

You need written approval before submitting anyone's project. This is non-negotiable, both ethically and practically. Editors may contact the architect directly, and if the architect doesn't know you submitted, it creates an awkward situation that reflects badly on everyone.

### The Ask

Keep it short and frame it as an opportunity for them, not a favor for you:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'd like to submit [Project Name] to [Publication(s)] for editorial consideration. The images are strong and I think it has a real shot at getting featured.
>
> Here's what I'd need from your side:
> - Go-ahead to submit
> - Floor plans or a section drawing if you're open to sharing
> - Full credits list (all consultants)
> - A quick review of the project description I'll draft
>
> No cost to you. I handle the entire submission. If it gets published, your firm gets a full feature with credits.
>
> Let me know and I'll put the package together.
>
> Matt

Most architects and designers will say yes immediately. Published work benefits them enormously. The only hesitation you'll encounter is if:
- The project had issues they don't want publicized (budget overruns, client disputes)
- They're planning to submit through their own PR channel
- The builder or another consultant has requested that the project stay private

If they say no, respect it without question.

### Building This Into Your Process

Ideally, publication submission becomes part of your standard delivery conversation. When you hand off final images:

> "These turned out really well. I think [Project] could do well with [Publication]. Want me to put together a submission?"

Over time, clients will expect this from you. That's when it transitions from a bonus into a service, and eventually into a retainer model.

## The Awards Connection

Awards and publications are two sides of the same credibility engine. They feed each other.

### How Awards Drive Publications

- Award-winning projects get editorial coverage. Publications cover the winners.
- An award shortlist or win gives you a press hook. "Recently shortlisted for the AFBC Awards of Excellence" makes your pitch to Dezeen or Western Living stronger.
- Award entries require professional photography. This is how you get hired for the shoot in the first place. The submission package you prepare for the award is 80% of the publication package.

### How Publications Drive Awards

- Published projects carry more weight in award juries. If the project is already in ArchDaily or Western Living, jurors take it more seriously.
- Publication credits in your portfolio make architects more likely to hire you for award-critical shoots.
- "Published in Dezeen" on your website makes the next architect think "this photographer gets my work seen."

### Awards You Can Enter Directly

You don't need a client's permission for these. You're entering as the photographer:

**Canadian Architect Photo Awards of Excellence**
- This is the single highest-ROI submission you can make.
- Submit your best single image of a Canadian building (no more than 2 years old).
- Fee: ~$75 CDN.
- Opens ~August 1, deadline ~September 12.
- Winners are published in the December issue of Canadian Architect and across all digital platforms.
- Every architect in Canada sees the December issue. A win or shortlist puts your name directly in front of your entire target market.
- Enter this every year without exception.

**Architizer A+ Awards**
- International platform. Photography categories available.
- Broader exposure. Global audience.
- Higher fees, but global reach.

**APA (Architectural Photography Awards)**
- International, annual.
- Less relevant for client acquisition but good for portfolio credibility.

### Awards You Submit on Behalf of Clients

These require the architect's or builder's participation, but you prepare the photography package:

| Award | Deadline | Fee | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| AFBC Awards of Excellence | ~July 25 | Varies | BC's highest architecture award. Biennial (odd years). |
| RAIC Governor General's Medals | Dec 11, 2026 | ~$415 | Canada's most prestigious. Biennial (even years). |

## Building Editor Relationships

Getting published is not just about individual submissions. Over time, editors learn your name and associate it with quality. That's when submissions turn into invitations.

### Months 1-3: Establish Your Presence

**Goal:** Get 2-3 projects published on ArchDaily.

ArchDaily is where you start because the acceptance rate is high and the process is straightforward. Every publication here gives you a credible reference point. "Published on ArchDaily" carries real weight even though it's the most accessible platform.

During this phase:
- Submit your 2-3 strongest existing projects to ArchDaily
- Follow editors at Western Living, Dezeen, and Canadian Architect on Instagram and LinkedIn
- Engage genuinely with their content. Comment on features you actually find interesting. Share their work. Don't pitch in DMs yet.
- Enter the Canadian Architect Photo Awards when submissions open (~August)

### Months 4-6: Pitch Upward

**Goal:** Land one Canadian publication (Western Living, Azure, or Canadian Architect) and pitch Dezeen once.

Now you have ArchDaily credits to reference. Use them:

> "This project was recently published on ArchDaily [link]. I'm reaching out to offer Western Living an in-depth feature with floor plans and an interview with the architect."

During this phase:
- Pitch your strongest BC project to Western Living
- Pitch your most design-forward project to Dezeen
- Submit to Canadian Architect with floor plans
- Attend industry events where editors are present (RAIC Conference in May is ideal, it's in Vancouver in 2026)
- Share published work on LinkedIn, tagging publications and architect firms

### Months 7-12: Become a Known Name

**Goal:** Accumulate 4-6 publications across tiers. Build a press page.

At this point, your name is starting to appear in editor inboxes regularly. The relationship shifts from cold pitching to warm familiarity.

During this phase:
- Submit to Dezeen with your strongest project of the year
- Follow up on any award shortlists by pitching those projects as press-worthy
- Pitch Dwell with a project that has a strong human/lifestyle story
- Build a "Press" or "Published In" section on your website
- Add publication credits to your email signature
- Reference publications in cold outreach emails and proposals

### Year 2 and Beyond

**Goal:** Editors recognize your name. Some publications start reaching out to you.

The flywheel is turning:
- Published work attracts architects who want their projects published
- Your retainer clients get systematic publication submissions as part of their service
- Award wins generate press coverage without you having to pitch
- Your publication credits become part of your sales pitch to new clients
- Higher-end projects come to you because architects know you'll get their work seen

## The Quarterly Review

Run this process every three months (January, April, July, October).

### Step 1: List every project delivered in the past 6 months

For each, score design merit:
- **5** — Exceptional design concept, strong site, publication-worthy at the highest tier
- **4** — Well-designed with a good story, suitable for Dwell/Western Living and ArchDaily
- **3** — Solid work, good for ArchDaily and Canadian publications
- **2** — Professional but not publication-ready (client marketing use only)
- **1** — Standard documentation, no editorial angle

### Step 2: Check submission status

For every project scored 3+:
- Has it been submitted anywhere?
- What's the status of pending submissions?
- Are there follow-ups overdue?
- Are there publications it hasn't been submitted to that it should be?

### Step 3: Prepare missing packages

For any project scored 3+ without a complete submission package, build one. This is the bottleneck. Most projects don't get submitted because the package never gets finished.

### Step 4: Submit

Follow the priority logic:
1. Merit 5 projects → Dezeen first (exclusive), wait 3 weeks, then everywhere
2. Merit 4 projects → Dwell or Western Living (if lifestyle angle), ArchDaily simultaneously
3. Merit 3 projects → ArchDaily + Canadian Architect simultaneously

### Step 5: Update tracking

Log everything in the Publication Tracker sheet.

## Common Traps

**"I'll wait for a better project."** The best project is the one you have now. Perfection is the enemy of publication. Submit what you have, learn from the responses, and improve with each submission. Your first ArchDaily feature will teach you more about what editors want than months of planning.

**"ArchDaily is too easy to count."** Wrong. ArchDaily has 15M+ monthly visitors. Architects actively browse it. Every feature is a Google-indexed page with your credit. It's the foundation of your published portfolio. Start here without apology.

**"I'll submit to everything at once."** Respect exclusivity or you'll burn relationships with top-tier editors. The damage from a Dezeen editor discovering you simultaneously submitted to ArchDaily is not worth the speed.

**"The project description doesn't matter that much."** It matters enormously. A weak description filled with marketing language signals to editors that the submission isn't serious. A precise, concept-driven description signals that you understand their world.

**"I don't need floor plans."** You don't technically need them. But including them dramatically increases acceptance odds at every publication. It takes one email to the architect. Just ask.

**"Following up shows persistence."** One follow-up at 2-3 weeks is professional. Two is pushy. Three burns the relationship. Editors have long memories. If they want the project, they'll respond.

**"I should wait until I have more experience."** You have the experience now. You know what you're shooting, you understand design intent, and you can write a compelling pitch. The only thing between you and a publication credit is actually submitting.

## After Publication

When a project gets published, you have a 48-hour window where the news is fresh and the momentum is highest. Use it.

### Immediate Actions (Within 24 Hours)

1. **Email the client.** Congratulate them. Include the direct link. This is the moment they realize your service extends beyond photography.
2. **Share on Instagram.** Tag the architect/designer, the builder, and the publication. Keep the caption short. Let the images and the publication credit speak.
3. **Share on LinkedIn.** Professional tone. Tag all firms involved. Include the link. This reaches a different audience than Instagram.
4. **Update your website.** Add the publication credit to the project page. If you have a press page, add it there too.

### Within One Week

5. **Update your email signature.** Add "Published in [Publication]" below your name.
6. **Update your proposals.** Any active or template proposals should reference the publication.
7. **Update cold email copy.** "I recently got [Firm]'s work published in [Publication]" is a powerful credibility statement.
8. **Send a note to any prospect in your pipeline.** "Wanted to share this, [Project] was just published in [Publication]. This is the kind of outcome I work toward with every project."

### The Retainer Conversation

A publication is the strongest conversion moment for a retainer pitch. The client just experienced the value of your submission service. Within a week or two of publication:

> "Now that [Project] is in [Publication], imagine if we did this systematically for every project you complete this year. That's what the Publication Partner retainer looks like — six shoots, two publication submissions, and quarterly content drops for $3,000 a month."

You won't close every client this way. But the ones who care about reputation, awards, and legacy will seriously consider it.

## The Bottom Line

Getting published is not about luck or connections. It's about:

1. Shooting work that communicates design intent
2. Preparing complete, professional submission packages
3. Writing pitches that lead with the design story
4. Submitting systematically and tracking everything

6. Making this a repeatable service, not a one-time effort
