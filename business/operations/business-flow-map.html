What's working

Three loops are genuinely strong. Cold outbound: 541 leads in three Instantly campaigns, first replies converting (Kombi, GD Nielsen). Proposal speed: turnaround in hours is a documented competitive advantage almost no peer matches. Cost-share economics: 90% hit rate is roughly 3x the AOS assumption — the highest-margin revenue line in the business and the one peers don't even try to capture.

What's bending

Production capacity is capped at Matt's hours. The capacity rule (intake pauses at three active projects) admits this directly. Edit handoff to Alena is the single highest-leverage bottleneck unsolved as of last update. The CRM has stages but no close-rate baseline, which means the funnel can't be optimized — only intuited. Repeat clients drive close to half of revenue, but there is no formal program asking for the second engagement.

What's silent

The accounts where revenue could exist but doesn't. Awards and publications: clients submit work, Matt does not — peers like StudioHDP have built entire books around photography that won 350+ design awards. Image licensing as a product: every cost-share moment is a single-use transaction, not an ongoing license. Referral incentive: repeat clients are 49% of revenue with zero formal ask. Paid acquisition: $300/mo budgeted and $0 spent for two consecutive quarters.

The 2025 business made $105K with one person, no close-rate data, no follow-up sequence, no paid budget actually spent, no awards strategy executed, and Instagram dark for five months. That isn't a ceiling, it's a baseline.

Reading the numbers honestly
02

Ideal. How a research-backed boutique agency would run this.

A model designed from the outside in: pods of work that run on their own rhythm, an owner whose week has only three modes, and a financial structure where retention compounds and cost-share is treated as a real revenue line, not a happy accident.

Fig. 02.1
Ideal architecture as a four-pod system. Each pod runs on its own rhythm. Owner sits below, in three modes only. Agents run the boring work. Command center sets the cadence.
The shift

Today, every arrow ends at Matt. In the ideal model, each pod has its own input, output, and operator. Matt's role compresses from everything to three modes — shoot, sell, decide — on a fixed weekly rhythm. Below, seven principles, each with the research that supports it.

Principles
01

Productize ruthlessly. Three tiers, fixed scope, fixed price.

McKinsey on productized services • PriceIntelligently

Productized services sustain ~40% gross margin where hourly work compresses to ~20% as the operator tires of negotiating. Three-tier ladders (entry → core → premium) outperform flat menus because they let the customer self-sort by intent. Build Tier 1 (Project, $3.5-5.5K), Tier 2 (Creative Partner, $2.5-4K/mo), Tier 3 (Brand film/Embedded, $10-18K). Every quote picks one tier; no bespoke math at the table.

02

Decouple production from creative direction.

Gerber, The E-Myth Revisited • Warrillow, Built to Sell

The owner-operator who edits, schedules, and bills cannot also strategize and sell. Make Alena the default editor on day one of every shoot, no exceptions. Producer (eventually a part-time coordinator, today the Producer agent) handles prep, handoff, delivery, NPS. Matt's QC pass is 15 minutes per project, not six hours. This single move 2-3x's throughput per Matt-hour, which is the lever that breaks the $150K solo ceiling.

03

Diversify acquisition across five channels, none above 40%.

Salesforce State of Marketing • HubSpot annual benchmarks

Multi-channel customers spend 10-30% more and stay longer. Today the business is dangerously close to single-channel (cold email + word of mouth do most of the work). Target mix: cold outbound 40%, referral/word-of-mouth 25%, organic SEO + content 15%, paid retargeting 10%, partnership/PR 10%. This insulates against any one channel breaking — Instagram dark for five months should not have been a financial event.

04

Compound retention into expansion, not just repeat.

Bain & Company (Reichheld) • Wharton on referrals

A 5% retention lift translates to 25-95% profit lift across services businesses. Repeat clients are already 49% of revenue, but they're being captured passively. Build a retention engine: NPS at delivery, quarterly check-in by the Producer agent, anniversary review (one year in, what's next), automated win-back at 9 months silent. Referred customers carry 16% higher lifetime value — formal referral incentive ($500 credit per closed referral) is the single highest-ROI program not yet in place.

05

Cost-share is a revenue line, not a happy accident.

Industry standard practice • observed peer pricing

90% hit rate is exceptional, but the current 30% bundled premium probably under-prices the work. Industry standard is split production cost plus separate per-party licensing fees ($500-1,500 per party per use case). Restructure: one production fee divided by participating parties, plus per-party license tiered by use (web, print, paid social, broadcast). The Producer agent invoices each party automatically. Estimated lift: $8-15K/yr at current shoot volume.

06

Build authority as a moat. Awards and publications systematized.

StudioHDP model (350+ design awards) • PPA top-quartile benchmarks

Competitors who own publication channels (Ema Peter — AD/Dwell/Azure; Mike Kelley — Fstoppers media empire) sit at the top of the category and command premium. Matt has zero publication wins as of last report; clients submit work, he doesn't. Move publication submission inside the delivery pod: every project enters an award and publication queue at delivery. Submit calendar runs quarterly. Target: one publication credit and one award shortlist by end of 2026, three of each by end of 2027.

07

Owner pays self first. Profit First allocation.

Michalowicz, Profit First

The personal P&L ($6,580/mo spend vs $3K target) is currently the largest financial leak in the business — bigger than software bloat, bigger than LOC interest. Allocate every deposited dollar across separate accounts: 50% Owner Pay, 15% Tax, 5% Profit (locked, quarterly), 30% Operating. Personal spend constraint forces the model to work for Matt, not the other way around. LOC paid down monthly from Profit envelope.

The agency model is not a different business. It's the same business, with the operator's role compressed.

Reading the table

Three Critical items if everything else slips: capacity ceiling, editor handoff, close rate tracking. Each is a single decision Matt can make this week. Together they unlock everything below them — without them, no amount of marketing or pricing work matters because the production line cannot absorb the volume and the funnel cannot be optimized.

The most expensive line in the business is not on any invoice. It's the cost of every shoot Matt edits himself, every proposal that goes silent without a follow-up sequence, every cost-share moment priced at 30% instead of full per-party fees, and the personal P&L that is currently $43K/yr larger than the model assumes.

The bet

Three things are true at once. The fundamentals are stronger than they look — 90% cost-share hit, six retainer-equivalents, a documented competitive advantage in proposal speed, a working agent fleet, a 75-page website doing real SEO work. The architecture is weaker than the fundamentals — Matt at the center of every loop, no close rate, no follow-up sequence, no formal retention. And the gap between today and the ideal is not a question of capability; it is a question of moves made in order.

This document is the order. Start with the three Critical items (Alena, close-rate, follow-up sequence). The rest compounds.
