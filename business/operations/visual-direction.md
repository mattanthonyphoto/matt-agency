# Visual Direction System

How to establish and communicate the visual tone for each project before the shoot.

---

## Default Visual Language

Every project starts from the baseline in `../team/style-guide.md`:
- Natural, honest, intentional
- True-to-material color grading
- Straight verticals, clean geometry
- Expose for the room, protect highlights
- No HDR, no heavy presets, no trendy looks

The visual direction for a specific project layers on top of that baseline — it doesn't replace it.

---

## Project Visual Profiles

Each project falls into one of these profiles. Select one in the creative brief and it shapes everything from shooting approach to editing.

### 1. Clean Residential

> The default for most builder and designer projects.

**Tone:** Warm, inviting, lived-in but polished
**Light:** Natural ambient dominant. Soft, even. Supplemental fill only where shadows go too deep.
**Palette emphasis:** Wood grain, stone texture, fabric softness. Warm whites, not clinical.
**Composition:** Two-point perspective, eye height. Generous framing — show the room, not just the feature.
**Editing lean:** Warm neutral. Slightly lifted blacks. Soft highlight rolloff.
**Reference feel:** West Elm catalog meets architectural documentation.
**Human element:** Level 1 minimum. Someone sitting on a couch, hands on a countertop, walking through a hallway. Warm, natural, belonging. The person looks like they live here.

### 2. Architectural Precision

> For architect clients and design-forward projects where the building IS the statement.

**Tone:** Clean, sharp, deliberate. The geometry is the hero.
**Light:** Natural light as a design element. Hard shadows welcome when they reveal form. Minimal fill.
**Palette emphasis:** Concrete, steel, glass, exposed structure. Let cool materials stay cool.
**Composition:** Strong leading lines. Symmetry when the design calls for it. Tilt-shift for elevation work. Include enough context to show how the building meets the site.
**Editing lean:** Neutral to slightly cool. Higher contrast. Crisp detail.
**Reference feel:** ArchDaily feature or Dwell magazine spread.
**Human element:** Single figure, small in frame. Silhouette in a corridor, person at the base of a staircase, figure dwarfed by a cantilevered roof. The architecture dominates — the person reveals its scale and intention.

### 3. Editorial / Award-Ready

> For publication pitches, Georgie submissions, magazine features.

**Tone:** Curated, intentional, magazine-ready. Every frame could be a cover.
**Light:** Controlled. Window pulls. Supplemental lighting where needed to balance. Time the shoot for best natural light.
**Palette emphasis:** Whatever the designer emphasizes. Color accuracy is critical — paint colors, fabric swatches, material finishes must read true.
**Composition:** Mix of establishing wides and intimate details. Strong hero compositions. Every angle considered.
**Editing lean:** Polished but not over-produced. Contrast and color accuracy above all.
**Reference feel:** Western Living, Azure, or Architectural Digest feature.
**Human element:** Level 2-3. These are the portfolio heroes. Plan 5-8 moment compositions. Silhouettes against windows, figures in doorways, someone ascending stairs. Every frame considered. Book a model for award submissions.

### 4. Raw / Construction

> Build documentation, team content, progress photography.

**Tone:** Honest, active, real. Craft and effort on display.
**Light:** Whatever's there. Don't fight it. Harsh construction lighting, open-air sites, natural weather.
**Palette emphasis:** Raw wood, concrete forms, steel framing, sawdust, mud. Texture over polish.
**Composition:** Wide establishing shots + tight detail of craft. Include people (candid, not posed). Show scale.
**Editing lean:** Slightly desaturated. Preserve grit. Don't clean up the site — that's the point.
**Reference feel:** Builder's journal or construction feature — work in progress, not portfolio.
**Human element:** Built in by default — the crew IS the content. Candid always. Hands on tools, figures in framing, boots on subfloor. Never posed, never looking at camera.

### 5. Lifestyle / Experiential

> For projects where the feeling of living in the space matters more than documenting every room.

**Tone:** Atmospheric, warm, storytelling. How it feels, not just how it looks.
**Light:** Golden hour preferred. Warm ambient. Firelight, candlelight, dusk.
**Palette emphasis:** Soft textures, warm tones, natural elements. Cozy over clinical.
**Composition:** Wider apertures for selective focus where appropriate. Vignettes. Moments over rooms.
**Editing lean:** Warmest of all profiles. Rich shadows. Romantic without being soft.
**Reference feel:** Kinfolk or Cereal magazine aesthetic applied to architecture.
**Human element:** The most human of all profiles. Wider apertures, selective focus on hands or faces. Someone reading by a window, pouring coffee, looking out at the mountains. The space is a backdrop for living.

## The Human Element — Philosophy

The difference between documentation and storytelling is the presence of life. Including people in architectural photography is not about staging lifestyle scenes — it's about capturing the relationship between a person and a space.

### Principles

1. **Architecture is always the hero.** The figure enhances the space, never competes with it. ~10-20% of the frame.
2. **Capture moments, don't direct scenes.** Say "walk toward the window" not "look contemplative." Movement over poses. Candid over controlled.
3. **One figure, maximum impact.** A single person in a vast architectural frame creates more emotional resonance than a group.
4. **No eye contact with camera.** The person is experiencing the space, not performing for the viewer.
5. **Shoot with and without.** Always capture the clean architectural shot first. The figure version is a bonus, not a replacement.
6. **Composition is final in-camera.** Place the figure intentionally within the architectural frame at the moment of capture. Don't plan to crop later.

### Wardrobe guidance

- Neutral tones — no logos, no bold patterns
- Dark clothing in light spaces, light clothing in dark spaces
- The person should feel like they belong in this home, not like they're visiting
- Bare feet on interior shoots (shoes break the "this is home" feeling)

### Building this skill

This is a practice, not a switch. Dedicate the last 60-90 minutes of every shoot to figure work. In 12 months, your portfolio will look fundamentally different from every other architectural photographer in BC.

Study: Ema Peter (architectural photojournalism), Magnum Photo Agency (decisive moment), Cereal Magazine (lifestyle architecture), Kinfolk (human warmth in designed spaces).

## How to Use the Profiles

1. **During the creative brief:** Select the primary profile. Note any secondary leans (e.g., "Clean Residential with Architectural Precision in the stairwell and entry").
2. **During the scout:** Let the profile guide which compositions you prioritize and how you plan the light.
3. **On shoot day:** Reference the profile when making on-set decisions about angles, timing, and supplemental lighting.
4. **In post-production:** Use the editing lean to guide the grade. The profile keeps the full set cohesive.
5. **For the second shooter:** Include the profile name in the shoot brief so they match the visual tone.

## Visual Reference Collection

### Building your reference library

For each profile, maintain a folder of reference images:

```
Dropbox/Matt Anthony Photography/Reference/
├── 01-Clean-Residential/
├── 02-Architectural-Precision/
├── 03-Editorial-Award/
├── 04-Raw-Construction/
└── 05-Lifestyle-Experiential/
```

Sources for reference images:
- Your own past work (strongest references — you already know how to achieve them)
- ArchDaily, Dezeen, Dwell, Western Living, Azure, Architectural Digest
- Competitor portfolios (Ema Peter, Measured Architecture, etc.)
- Client-provided mood boards or inspiration images

### Per-project reference boards

For projects where the visual direction needs to be communicated clearly (cost-share shoots, second shooter, contracted editor):

1. Pull 5-8 reference images that capture the tone
2. Add them to the project folder: `Project Name / Reference /`
3. Include 2-3 from your own portfolio + 2-3 from external sources
4. Note what you're referencing in each: "this light quality," "this composition style," "this material rendering"

## Communicating Visual Direction

### To the client

Keep it simple. Clients don't need profile names or technical language. Use phrases like:
- "We'll keep the feel warm and natural — the materials in this home are beautiful and we want them to read true."
- "For the award submission, we'll shoot this more editorially — every angle considered, every detail intentional."
- "This is a documentation shoot, so we'll capture the craft and progress honestly."

### To the second shooter

Include in the shoot brief:
- Profile name (they should read this document)
- 3-5 reference images from the project folder
- Specific notes on what to match: "warm neutral grade," "higher contrast than usual," "embrace the hard shadows"

### To the editor

Include in the editing handoff:
- Profile name
- 2-3 reference images from your own work that set the grade
- Notes on any deviations from the style guide baseline
- Flag any rooms or shots that need different treatment (e.g., "bathroom is all white tile — watch the blue cast")
